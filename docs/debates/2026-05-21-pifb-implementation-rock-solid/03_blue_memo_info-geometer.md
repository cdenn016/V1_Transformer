# Blue memo — info-geometer (rebuttal)

## Concession from red's opening

I concede that the Chechik-Tishby-Weiss [Chechik, Globerson, Tishby, Weiss 2005, *JMLR* 6: 165-188, §3] Gaussian-IB closed form does not apply to objects of the manuscript's type $X = \{q_i, s_i, U_i\}_{i \in I}$ (distributions and group elements rather than jointly Gaussian random vectors in a common Euclidean space). The Chechik-Tishby framework requires the input variables to be jointly Gaussian random vectors; the optimal $T = AX + \xi$ is a linear projection along the top canonical-correlation directions, which are well-defined only when $X, Y$ live in a shared Euclidean space.

## Strongest defense against red's core attack with citation

Red overreads the manuscript's invocation. The actual text at line 2138: "For linear-Gaussian dynamics around consensus, the IB problem admits the closed-form Gaussian-IB solution of [Chechik 2005] … Whether the resulting $T$ coincides with the variational barycenter in this regime is an open question we do not settle here … Three ingredients remain to be supplied before [the IB Lagrangian] is more than a research direction in this framework: a precise specification of the natural-gradient transition kernel that defines $X \to Y$ in the gauge-covariant setting; a treatment of the gauge-frame component of $T$ under encoder noise (the group-valued analogue of Gaussian-IB requires the Riemannian structure of [the] frame barycenter); and an empirical comparison of the IB-optimal coarse-graining with the threshold-detector hierarchy that the simulations of this paper produce."

The manuscript is invoking the closed form *as a research direction*, with three explicitly unsupplied ingredients — including "the group-valued analogue of Gaussian-IB requires the Riemannian structure of the frame barycenter," which is exactly red's objection (group elements aren't jointly Gaussian vectors). The manuscript and red are saying the same thing. The closing sentence at line 2138: "We flag IB as a refinement that would, if the comparison succeeds, license a strictly stronger emergence claim than the FE-improvement criterion alone supports; the present manuscript stands on the FE-improvement criterion." The manuscript stands on the FE-improvement criterion at Eq. 2123, not on the IB Lagrangian at Eq. 2133. The IB is labeled an "Information-bottleneck refinement (research direction)" in the paragraph header at line 2131.

By the canon-strict judge's wrong-domain-citation rubric ([Chechik 2005] cited for a claim about distributions-and-group-elements when the paper concerns jointly Gaussian random vectors), the test is whether the manuscript makes the wrong-domain claim. It does not: it explicitly says the closed form requires Gaussian random vectors, the manuscript's setting does not have them, three ingredients are unsupplied, and the present manuscript stands elsewhere. This is canon-strict-compliant scope flagging, not citation misuse.

[Tishby, Pereira, Bialek 1999, *Allerton*, §2] originally formulated the IB Lagrangian as the abstract variational principle $\mathcal{L} = I(T;X) - \beta I(T;Y)$ without restriction to Gaussian variables. The Chechik 2005 closed form is the specialization to Gaussians; the abstract IB Lagrangian applies more broadly (with a different solution structure). The manuscript at Eq. 2133 cites Tishby 1999 / Chechik 2005 / Bialek 2001 — the Tishby 1999 citation is for the abstract IB Lagrangian (which the manuscript's setup is in scope for), the Chechik 2005 citation is for the closed form (which the manuscript explicitly says does not apply). The citations are domain-appropriate for what they are cited for.

## Strongest counter-attack on red's weakest evidence

Red's Vector 3 bundles the IB-closed-form failure with the Karcher-non-compact failure as two of three pieces of one combined attack. The two issues are independent and have different evidential weight. The IB attack is the weakest because the manuscript and red are saying the same thing about the closed form's non-applicability; the Karcher attack is stronger because the manuscript flags the unadjudicated choice but does not resolve it. If red's Vector 3 stands or falls as a unit, then the failure of the IB sub-attack weakens the whole vector. The chief judge should be asked to score Vector 3's three subparts independently rather than as a bundle.

The forward-KL barycenter at Eq. 2142 is itself canonical [Amari, Nagaoka 2000, *Methods of Information Geometry*, §3.4]: KL is the second-order expansion of the Fisher metric; the forward-KL barycenter on an e-flat exponential family is the m-projection onto the family with the unique moment-matched minimizer. The manuscript's Eq. 2147-2151 are the Amari canonical form. Dropping the dispersion term at line 2179 corresponds to the $\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon)$ truncation that Amari-Nagaoka §3.4 develops; the manuscript explicitly notes (line 2179) the dropped term is $\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon)$ in the high-coherence regime where meta-agents form. This is the canonical information-geometric structure, not a deviation from it.

## Falsification conditions for this defense

This defense is wrong if (i) downstream §Results or §Methods text cites the IB Lagrangian as the principled object the simulations test (rather than the FE-improvement criterion at Eq. 2123) — would need to be shown; (ii) the Tishby 1999 sign convention at PIFB Eq. 2133 is wrong (red's round-2 panel confirmed it is right: $\mathcal{L} = I(T;X) - \beta I(T;Y)$ matches Tishby canonical form); (iii) the forward-KL barycenter at Eq. 2142 is the reverse-KL form (red's round-2 panel did not raise this; the variational convention is consistent with the manuscript's footnote at line 2145).

## Newly-discovered canon

- **Tishby, Zaslavsky 2015, "Deep Learning and the Information Bottleneck Principle", *ITW* 2015: 1-5.** The IB Lagrangian's variational formulation is independent of the Gaussian closed-form specialization; the abstract Lagrangian applies to arbitrary $X, Y$ with the parametric encoder family chosen per setting.
- **Strouse, Schwab 2017, "The Deterministic Information Bottleneck", *Neural Computation* 29(6): 1611-1630.** Generalization of IB to arbitrary deterministic encoders, recasting the closed-form question as a parametric-family choice. Supports the manuscript's "three ingredients" enumeration as a well-formed research direction rather than a citation collapse.
