## Steelman of the claim from this lens

The strongest reading: the IB Lagrangian at PIFB Eq. 2133, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{IB}}[T \mid X] = I(T;X) - \beta_{\mathrm{IB}} I(T;Y)$, is the canonical sign-convention form from [Tishby-Pereira-Bialek1999], and PIFB line 2138 itself flags the IB framing as "a research direction" with three unsupplied ingredients (natural-gradient transition kernel, group-valued Gaussian-IB, empirical comparison with the threshold detector). The Gaussian closed form at line 2138 cites [Chechik-Globerson-Tishby-Weiss2005] correctly: "the optimal $T$ is a precision-weighted projection along the top canonical-correlation directions between $X$ and $Y$." The Karcher mean at Eq. 2156 with the bi-invariant-metric existence-uniqueness caveat at line 2160 invokes [Karcher1977 *Comm. Pure Appl. Math.*]. The Lie-algebra-additive frame at line 2191 is correctly identified as the first-order BCH approximation. Under a steelman where these citations are all correct and the disclosures are taken at face value, the section makes a measured set of canonical invocations and labels its research-direction items as such.

## Strongest weakness from this lens

Three of the canonical invocations do not survive close reading.

(1) **The Gaussian-IB invocation at line 2138 is misapplied.** The Chechik-Tishby-Weiss closed form for Gaussian-IB requires $X$ and $Y$ to be *jointly Gaussian random vectors* in a common Euclidean space, and the optimal $T = AX + \xi$ is a *linear projection* obtained by canonical correlation analysis between $X$ and $Y$ [Chechik-Globerson-Tishby-Weiss 2005 *JMLR* 6, 165–188]. The manuscript at lines 2131–2138 sets $X = \{q_i, s_i, U_i\}_{i \in I}$ (a tuple of *distributions* and a *group element*), $T = (q_I, s_I, U_I)$ (likewise), and $Y = X_{t+\Delta t}$ under "one step of natural-gradient flow on $\mathcal{F}$." None of $X, T, Y$ is a Gaussian random vector — they are distributions and group elements. Invoking Gaussian-IB on this object requires (a) embedding distributions in a Hilbert space (e.g., via the moment-parameter map, but then $X$ is a deterministic function of the distributions, not a random vector); (b) treating the gauge frame $U_I \in G$ on a *non-Euclidean* manifold, for which the Gaussian-IB construction does not apply. The manuscript itself admits this at line 2138: "a treatment of the gauge-frame component of $T$ under encoder noise (the group-valued analogue of Gaussian-IB requires the Riemannian structure of [the] frame barycenter)" — i.e., the Chechik-Tishby form does *not* apply, and an unwritten extension would be needed. Citing the Chechik-Tishby closed form as "admits the closed-form Gaussian-IB solution" (line 2138) while in the next sentence acknowledging the construction does not apply is **hand-wave-with-citation** in the canon-cop strike list — citing a result irrelevant to the actual claim. Strike weight 2 per the methodology.

(2) **The Karcher barycenter on non-compact $\mathrm{GL}^+(K)$ has no valid construction.** PIFB line 2160 says: "For non-compact $G$ (such as $\mathrm{GL}^+(K)$, where the Killing form on $\mathfrak{gl}(K)$ is indefinite) no bi-invariant Riemannian metric exists, and~\eqref{eq:meta_agent_frame_barycenter} must be replaced by either a left-invariant alternative or a polar-decomposition / SPD-restricted construction; both substitutes break gauge symmetry partially, and the choice is a modeling decision the present implementation does not adjudicate." This is a real obstruction: a connected Lie group admits a bi-invariant Riemannian metric iff it is isomorphic to $G_c \times A$ with $G_c$ compact and $A$ abelian ([Milnor 1976 *Adv. Math.* 21, 293–329]). $\mathrm{GL}^+(K)$ for $K \ge 2$ is non-compact and not of this form, so no bi-invariant metric exists. The manuscript's response is to say the choice "is a modeling decision the present implementation does not adjudicate." A modeling decision that the implementation does not adjudicate is an unresolved gap, not a "rock solid" object. The Karcher frame barycenter at Eq. 2156 is the load-bearing object for the §Top-Down Participation transport $\Omega_{i,I}$ (line 2247) on the model fiber when the gauge group is $\mathrm{GL}^+(K)$; without a metric, the barycenter is not defined, and the simulator's choice (whatever it is) silently picks one of the partially-symmetry-breaking substitutes the manuscript declines to adjudicate. This is sub-claim 4 (falsifiability / scope) wounded: the choice is left unspecified, so the construction is unfalsifiable — any simulator output is consistent with *some* choice.

(3) **The Gibbs $\exp(-V/\tau)$ vs `1-KL` argument at line 2174 supports the manuscript's form over the simulator's, but the manuscript form has its own information-geometric obstruction at the high-KL regime.** The manuscript writes $C_q = \exp[-\bar{\mathrm{KL}}/\tau_q]$ where $\bar{\mathrm{KL}}$ is the mean post-transport KL. This is a Boltzmann-style soft-indicator of consensus. The information-geometric obstruction: on a non-compact statistical manifold (e.g., Gaussians with arbitrary mean) the KL divergence is unbounded above (the manuscript correctly notes this at line 2174). The Gibbs form $\exp(-V/\tau)$ is therefore arbitrarily close to 0 in the low-coherence regime — but the *gradient* of $\exp(-V/\tau)$ with respect to the underlying parameters is also vanishingly small when $V \gg \tau$, which means the detector has no power to discriminate between "very low coherence" and "extremely low coherence." Whether the threshold detector tracks the variational savings — which the manuscript at line 2174 itself disclaims — has an information-geometric source: the Gibbs detector saturates on the high-KL tail, exactly the regime where the quadratic-scaling argument at line 2129 ($\sim |I|(|I|-1)\varepsilon$ for small $\varepsilon$) is most fragile. The manuscript's exponential detector is form-appropriate but not regime-appropriate; the natural form on a statistical manifold is the Fisher-Riemannian distance ([Amari-Nagaoka2000 Ch. 2]), not a Boltzmann-weighted KL average. Citing the Gibbs form as "linked cleanly to the Gibbs/softmax structure of the underlying functional" (line 2167) does not address the saturation obstruction.

Three independent information-geometric weaknesses; any one falsifies sub-claim 2 (canonical fidelity).

## Falsification condition

The claim is wrong if any one of:
(i) the Chechik-Tishby-Weiss Gaussian-IB closed form does not apply to the manuscript's $X = \{q_i, s_i, U_i\}, T = (q_I, s_I, U_I), Y = X_{t+\Delta t}$ setup (the manuscript at line 2138 admits this);
(ii) no bi-invariant Riemannian metric exists on $\mathrm{GL}^+(K)$ for $K \ge 2$ (this is [Milnor1976 *Adv. Math.* 21, 293–329] and the manuscript at line 2160 admits it), and the implementation's choice of substitute is unspecified;
(iii) the Gibbs detector $\exp(-V/\tau_q)$ saturates in the high-KL regime, decoupling the detector from the variational savings the manuscript at line 2129 invokes to motivate it (the manuscript at line 2174 admits the decoupling).

## Newly-discovered canon

- **Chechik, Globerson, Tishby, Weiss, "Information Bottleneck for Gaussian Variables", *JMLR* 6 (2005) 165–188**: the Gaussian-IB closed form requires jointly-Gaussian $X, Y$ in a common Euclidean space; optimal $T = AX + \xi$ with $A$ obtained by canonical correlation analysis. Section/theorem number to be confirmed against the printed source; the §3 framework of the paper develops this. Available at <https://www.jmlr.org/papers/volume6/chechik05a/chechik05a.pdf>.
- **Milnor, "Curvatures of Left Invariant Metrics on Lie Groups", *Advances in Mathematics* 21 (1976) 293–329**: a connected Lie group admits a bi-invariant Riemannian metric iff isomorphic to $G_c \times A$ with $G_c$ compact and $A$ abelian (standard result; section/lemma number to be confirmed against the printed source). $\mathrm{GL}^+(K)$ is not of this form for $K \ge 2$.
- **Pennec, "Bi-invariant Means on Lie Groups with Cartan-Schouten Connections" (2012, *CIS-6100* lecture notes, U. Pennsylvania)**: bi-invariant means for general Lie groups using the affine Cartan-Schouten connection rather than a Riemannian metric. Existence is *local* — within a normal convex neighborhood; the iterated fixed-point converges only at linear rate and only when constituents are close. Available at <https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~cis6100/Bi-Invar-Means-Pennec.pdf>.
- **Amari, Nagaoka, *Methods of Information Geometry* (AMS Translations, 2000), Ch. 2**: KL divergence is the second-order expansion of the Fisher metric at infinitesimal $q \approx p$; the natural "distance" on a statistical manifold is the Fisher-Riemannian geodesic distance, not a Boltzmann-weighted KL average.
- **Tishby, Pereira, Bialek, "The Information Bottleneck Method", *Allerton* (1999), §2**: the IB Lagrangian's variational closure depends on a tractable encoder family $p(T \mid X)$; for distributions $X$ and group elements $U$, no canonical encoder family is supplied in the manuscript.
