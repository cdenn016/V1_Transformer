## Steelman of the claim from this lens

The strongest reading: the variational FE-improvement criterion at Eq. 2123 ($\mathcal{F}^*[\text{parent+children}] + C(I) < \mathcal{F}^*[\text{children alone}]$) is a clean variational selection rule on hierarchical models, in the spirit of [Bishop2006 §10.6] structured-variational inference. The barycenter at Eq. 2142 is a forward-KL Bregman barycenter, regularized toward the parent prior — a standard variational object [Amari-Nagaoka2000 Ch. 4]. The closed-form $\mu_I^*$ at Eq. 2147 and $\Sigma_I^*$ at Eq. 2150 are the standard moment-matched mixture barycenter ([Bishop2006 §10.7]). The Ouroboros fragment at Eq. 2270 is a geometric-discount tempered-Bayes update [BissiriHolmesWalker2016] / log-linear pool [GenestZidek1986]. The disclosures at lines 2153, 2174, 2179, 2228, 2284 are appropriate scope hedges. Under a steelman, the section places a sequence of canonical variational objects with their canonical names and labels research directions as research directions.

## Strongest weakness from this lens

The variational closure of the FE-improvement criterion is *not* established. The manuscript at line 2174 explicitly disclaims it: "we do not establish that the detector's product form exactly tracks the variational-criterion savings even in the high-coherence limit." This is sub-claim 1 (mathematical correctness) wounded *and* sub-claim 4 (falsifiability / scope) wounded *and* sub-claim 7 (no unresolved gaps) wounded simultaneously.

(1) **The FE-improvement criterion at Eq. 2123 is the conjunctive head of the variational principle, but the manuscript itself acknowledges the criterion is never evaluated in the simulations.** Line 2167: "The criterion~\eqref{eq:meta_agent_FE_criterion} requires evaluating the optimized free energy under both configurations, which is computationally expensive. The simulations of this paper use a discrete threshold-based detector as a practical surrogate for~\eqref{eq:meta_agent_FE_criterion}." Then at line 2174: the detector's product form does not exactly track the variational savings, and "the two factors are bundled inside the single product $P \cdot C_q \cdot C_s$ of the detector rather than separated as the variational criterion separates them." So: the variational principle is the load-bearing motivation; the simulations never evaluate it; the surrogate doesn't track it. What the simulations test is the threshold detector. The variational principle is *cited* as the principled object but is *not* the operative principle. This is **claim-drift inside a single subsection** — the §Variational Principle subsection (lines 2119–2160) and the §Threshold-Based Implementation subsection (lines 2162–2174) make incompatible operative claims about what the simulations test.

(2) **The cluster-aggregation weight $w_i^I$ has two different definitions in two consecutive paragraphs.** Line 2129 introduces $w_i^I$ in the variational principle as a normalized weight ($\sum_{i \in I} w_i^I = 1$) tied to the cluster-internal alignment KL. Line 2187 then introduces $w_i^I(x) = \chi_i(x) \exp[-\mathrm{KL}(q_i^{(s)} \| \bar{q}_I^{(s)})]$ — an unnormalized, presence-weighted, posterior-saddle-point weight. The manuscript at line 2187 calls this "the leading-order saddle-point approximation of the variational cluster-aggregation weights $w_i^I$ in~\eqref{eq:meta_agent_barycenter} when the parent prior $p_I$ is taken to center on the emerging consensus." The two $w_i^I$'s are not the same object: the variational $w_i^I$ at Eq. 2142 is a Lagrange-multiplier-style normalized weight on the alignment KL; the implementation $w_i^I(x)$ at line 2187 is an *unnormalized* product of a support function and an exp(-KL) saddle-point weight. The saddle-point relation between the two is asserted but not derived. Under variational inference [BleiKuckelbirgJordan2017 §3], the closed-form weights for a forward-KL barycenter come from setting the variational derivative to zero — and the resulting weights are the *Lagrange-multiplier* weights normalized to sum to 1, not an exp(-KL) form. The exp(-KL) form is the *softmin selector* over constituents, a different object. The manuscript's claim that one is a "leading-order saddle-point approximation" of the other is asserted, not derived.

(3) **The dropped dispersion term at line 2179 is $\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon)$ where $\varepsilon$ is the post-transport pairwise KL — but $\varepsilon$ is not actually small in the early-formation regime where meta-agents form.** The variational principle at Eq. 2123 says the savings scale as $|I|(|I|-1)\varepsilon$. For the savings to be positive (the FE-improvement criterion to hold), $\varepsilon$ must be small. But for the dispersion term to be droppable as "$\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon)$", $\varepsilon$ must be small. Both arguments depend on the same $\varepsilon$ being small simultaneously. The manuscript at line 2174 sets $\Gamma_{\min} = 0.5$, which under the Gibbs form $\Gamma \approx \exp(-\bar{V}/\tau)$ corresponds to $\bar{V} \approx \tau \ln 2 \approx 0.7\tau$. With $\tau_q = O(1)$, the post-transport KL at the threshold of formation is $O(1)$, not $\ll 1$. The "$\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon)$" claim is therefore an asymptotic-regime claim being applied at the threshold, which is exactly the regime where the asymptotic does not hold. The implementation formula at Eqs. 2181, 2184 drops a term that is not actually small at the regime where the formula is invoked.

(4) **The cross-scale point-passing at Eq. 2247 is not standard hierarchical variational inference.** The manuscript writes $p_i^{(s)}(x) = \Omega_{i,I}[q_I^{(s+1)}](x)$ — the constituent's *prior* is the transported meta-agent *posterior*. Standard hierarchical variational inference [Friston2017Graphical, ParrPezzuloFriston2022 §9] passes the *full posterior* between levels; mean-field hierarchical schemes pass a point-summary (mean) but not the full distribution. The manuscript at line 2254 says: "Equation~\eqref{eq:topdown_priors} is the formal content of the cross-scale shadow relation~\eqref{eq:cross_scale_shadow} introduced in Section~\ref{sec:statistical_manifolds}: the prior $p_i^{(s)}$ and hyper-prior $r_i^{(s)}$ at scale $s$ are not independent dynamical fields but are determined by the meta-agent at scale $s+1$." So the prior is *equal* to the transported posterior, not approximated by it. This is a *deterministic* point-passing (the full distribution, but treated as a fixed prior) and is therefore not the standard variational hierarchical scheme: the variational q over $q_I^{(s+1)}$ is collapsed to a delta at its posterior mean before being used at level $s$. Under [external_canon_inference.md §3]: "the user's multi-layer cascade … is not standard mean-field across the hierarchy. It is a deterministic point-passing scheme that loses the variational uncertainty about $s_\ell$ when passed to $\ell+1$." Cross-scale shadow priors at PIFB Eq. 2247 are exactly this pattern.

## Falsification condition

The claim is wrong if any one of:
(i) the manuscript explicitly disclaims at line 2174 that the threshold detector tracks the variational savings, *and* the variational principle is the principled object the threshold detector is a surrogate for — sub-claim 1 (mathematical correctness) is wounded;
(ii) the cluster-aggregation weight $w_i^I$ has incompatible definitions at lines 2129 (normalized Lagrange-multiplier) and 2187 (unnormalized exp(-KL) saddle-point), with the relation asserted not derived — sub-claim 3 (internal consistency);
(iii) the dropped dispersion term at line 2179 is $\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon)$ for small $\varepsilon$ but $\Gamma_{\min} = 0.5$ at line 2174 places the formation regime at $\varepsilon = O(1)$;
(iv) the cross-scale point-passing at Eq. 2247 is deterministic, not the full variational hierarchical posterior pass.

## Newly-discovered canon

- **Blei, Kuckelbirg, Jordan, "Variational Inference: A Review for Statisticians", *JASA* 112 (2017) 859–877, §3 "Mean-Field Variational Inference"**: the closed-form weights for a forward-KL barycenter (the CAVI update) are normalized Lagrange-multiplier weights, not exp(-KL) softmin selectors. The exp(-KL) saddle-point is a separate construction (variational EM with a delta-prior approximation at the parent).
- **Bishop, *Pattern Recognition and Machine Learning* (2006), §10.7 "Variational Inference for the Gaussian Mixture"**: the moment-matched mixture barycenter is $\mu_I = \sum_i w_i \mu_i$, $\Sigma_I = \sum_i w_i [\Sigma_i + (\mu_i - \mu_I)(\mu_i - \mu_I)^\top]$. Dropping the dispersion term is *not* a standard approximation — it is a *deviation* from the moment-matched barycenter.
- **Friston, Parr, de Vries, "The graphical brain: belief propagation and active inference", *Network Neuroscience* 1 (2017) 381–414, §3**: hierarchical variational inference passes the full posterior $q(s_\ell)$ between levels, not a point summary or a fixed-prior substitute. A deterministic point-passing scheme is not standard hierarchical variational inference.
- **Amari, Nagaoka, *Methods of Information Geometry* (2000), §3.4 "The Pythagorean theorem and projection"**: the forward-KL barycenter on an e-flat exponential family is the m-projection onto the family; the unique minimizer is the moment-matched mean. The implementation's Eqs. 2181, 2184 deviate from this by dropping the dispersion term, which is the m-projection's covariance correction.
- **BissiriHolmesWalker, "A general framework for updating belief distributions", *JRSSB* 78 (2016) 1103–1130, §2**: tempered Bayes is $p(\theta | y) \propto p(\theta) \exp[-\lambda L(\theta, y)]$; the temperature $\lambda$ is a *learning rate*, not a *geometric discount* across an indexed sequence of likelihoods. The Ouroboros fragment's $\rho^k$ geometric discount on $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ generations is a different object — closer to a discounted infinite-horizon Bayes update, not the Bissiri-Holmes-Walker form. The citation at PIFB line 2275 needs a closer match.
