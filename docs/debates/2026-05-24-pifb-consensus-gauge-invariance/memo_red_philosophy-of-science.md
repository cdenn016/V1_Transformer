## Steelman
The section is unusually candid: it labels the consensus-metric construction "a heuristic
target rather than a completed observable," states the gauge-invariance is "conditional on a
regulator whose construction is left to future work," and flags the consensus-gauge thesis as
"a metaphysical interpretation rather than a derivation, and the hypothesis may not be
falsifiable from within the framework" (:2986, :2992). On its face this is a model of honest
hedging, and the claim under debate is that those hedges are correct AND complete.

## Falsification (the residual gap the hedge does not close)
The hedge names the wrong defect. The manuscript flags the consensus-gauge thesis as
*possibly unfalsifiable*. The deeper problem is that it is **circular**: it presents as a
discovery ("gauge invariance arises as a consistency requirement for multi-agent consensus,"
:2990) something the framework installed as a setup assumption. The belief fiber carries
KL divergence as its objective, and KL between Gaussians is GL(K,ℝ)-invariant by the
Cencov/sufficient-statistic uniqueness of the Fisher metric (info-geometer memo). The
"consensus requirement" — that agents in different frames must agree on shared structure —
is therefore *automatically satisfied* by an objective that was selected to be frame-invariant.
A constraint that is satisfied by construction cannot be the explanation for the property it
guarantees. This is the classic redescription-masquerading-as-derivation pattern: a statement
"compatible with all observations... because it is consistent with all possible observations"
is, in Popper's diagnosis, not an empirical claim but a tautology dressed as one
[Popper, *Logic of Scientific Discovery*; SEP "Karl Popper"]. "May not be falsifiable" treats the
thesis as a bold conjecture that happens to lack a test; the accurate diagnosis is that it has
no empirical content to test because its conclusion is its premise.

The manuscript's own cross-references confirm the framing is interpretive throughout
(:3309 "a correlate of objective reality"; :3313 "an interpretive thesis the formalism is
consistent with, not as a derivation"). That consistency is to the manuscript's credit and
discharges attack-vector (d). What it does NOT discharge is naming the *kind* of gap: an
unfalsifiable conjecture and a circular redescription are different failures, and the section
asserts the former while exhibiting the latter.

## External primary-source citation (not the manuscript)
- Popper, K. (1959/2002), *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* — demarcation: a theory
  "compatible with all possible observations" is unscientific; a definition whose conclusion
  is built into its premise (the "survival of the fittest" tautology case) is not a derivation.
  [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Karl Popper," §on tautology and falsifiers.]

## Falsification condition
This memo is wrong if (i) the belief-fiber objective is NOT chosen for its frame-invariance —
i.e., KL is not GL(K)-invariant on the Gaussian fiber, so the consensus constraint is a
genuine non-trivial restriction the framework could have failed — or (ii) the manuscript
somewhere derives a *quantitative, frame-independent prediction* from the consensus
requirement that does not already follow from the invariance of KL.
