# Memo — gauge-theorist (RED, opening)

## Steelman of the claim
The section is explicit that the temporal sign is not intrinsic to the spectral decomposition: "The temporal sign is not intrinsic to the positive semi-definite spectral decomposition ... it is imported from the postulate set of Section [signature_resolution] and inherits the open-problem status of that section" (:3029). This is an honest disclaimer, and the spectral-theorem facts ($G_i$ symmetric PSD, $\lambda_a \ge 0$, orthonormal eigenbasis) are elementary and correct.

## Falsification target
Concede the spectral facts, then press the consequence the disclaimer concedes but the claim downplays. A pullback of a PSD fiber metric is PSD, so every eigenvalue satisfies $\lambda_a \ge 0$ (:3005). A Lorentzian $(1+3)$ signature is, by definition, an *indefinite* bilinear form of signature $(1,3)$ — one negative and three positive (or the opposite convention) entries on the diagonal in an orthonormal basis [Nakahara 2003, *Geometry, Topology and Physics* (2nd ed.), §7.1–7.2 on Riemannian vs. pseudo-Riemannian metrics and signature; an indefinite metric has at least one negative eigenvalue]. A PSD matrix cannot have a negative eigenvalue. Therefore the Lorentzian decomposition asserted at :3029 cannot be obtained from the spectral decomposition of $G_i$ alone — full stop. The minus sign has to be inserted by hand from outside the spectral construction.

The manuscript inserts it via the signature-section postulates: "imaginary frame component along one base direction with real-part projection, single non-compact generator, $+\mathrm{tr}(AB)$ bilinear-form sign convention" (:3029). The $(1+3)$ structure of the observable sector therefore does not stand on the spectral decomposition; it stands on the signature postulate set. That postulate set "inherits the open-problem status of that section" — and per the inherited Debate 2 disposition, the signature construction is under REMAND. So the load-bearing element of the $(1+3)$ identification (the temporal sign) rests on an unresolved postulate, not on the mathematics of this section.

## The precise overclaim
At :3029 the section says the 4-dimensional sector "admits a $(1\,\text{temporal} + 3\,\text{spatial})$ Lorentzian decomposition." "Admits" is doing concealed work: the spectral decomposition admits no such thing — it admits only a 4-dimensional *Euclidean* (all-$\lambda_a\ge 0$) subspace. The Lorentzian reading is supplied entirely by the imported postulates. A reader who grants the spectral theorem is being invited to also grant the Lorentzian signature as if it followed; it does not. This is the standard error of conflating the metric you can build (PSD pullback) with the metric you want (indefinite) [Nakahara §7.1: signature is an invariant of the form; you cannot change it by a real change of basis — Sylvester's law of inertia].

## Falsification condition (when RED is wrong here)
RED loses this vector if blue shows the $(1+3)$ signature is obtained *without* importing the temporal sign from a separate postulate set — i.e., if the indefinite signature follows from the framework's own construction of $G_i$ — OR if the signature section's REMAND has been lifted to a resolved derivation that this section can cite as established rather than open. As written, :3029 concedes both that the sign is imported and that it inherits open-problem status, so the $(1+3)$ identification is not carried by this section.

## Newly-discovered canon
- Nakahara 2003, *Geometry, Topology and Physics* (2nd ed., IoP), §7.1–7.2: Riemannian metrics are positive-definite; pseudo-Riemannian (Lorentzian) metrics are indefinite with at least one negative eigenvalue; signature is a basis-independent invariant (Sylvester's law of inertia). A PSD form cannot be Lorentzian.
- Sylvester's law of inertia (standard; e.g., Horn & Johnson 2013, *Matrix Analysis* 2nd ed., Thm 4.5.8): the number of positive, negative, and zero eigenvalues of a symmetric bilinear form is invariant under real congruence — a PSD pullback has zero negative eigenvalues and cannot be made indefinite by a real change of base coordinates.
