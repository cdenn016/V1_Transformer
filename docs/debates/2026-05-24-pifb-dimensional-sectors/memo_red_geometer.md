# Memo — geometer (RED, opening)

## Steelman of the claim
The base-vs-fiber bookkeeping is correct and carefully stated. $G_i(c) = \sigma_i^* g_{\mathcal B}|_{q_i(c)}$ is a pullback, hence a tensor on $T_c\mathcal C$, an $n\times n$ object with $n = \dim\mathcal C$ and at most $n$ eigenvalues (:3019). The differential $d\sigma_i|_c : T_c\mathcal C \to T_{q_i}\mathcal B$ maps an $n$-dimensional space into the $\dim(\mathcal B)$-dimensional fiber tangent, so the "vast majority of dimensions invisible" claim is correctly a statement about the $\dim(\mathcal B) - n$ unsampled fiber directions, not about eigen-directions of $G_i$. This is exactly right, and the explicit refusal to conflate the two senses (:3073) is a genuine virtue. I concede this bookkeeping in full.

## Falsification target
The same pullback fact that makes the bookkeeping correct destroys the construction's content in the framework's only realized instance. A pullback of a (0,2)-tensor by $\sigma:\mathcal C \to \mathcal B$ has rank $\le \dim\mathcal C$; it is a genuine (full-rank, positive-definite) Riemannian metric on $\mathcal C$ *if and only if $\sigma$ is an immersion*, and is otherwise degenerate [Lee, *Introduction to Smooth Manifolds* (2nd ed.), Ch. 11 on pullbacks; the immersion criterion: pullback of a Riemannian metric is Riemannian iff the map is an immersion — ProofWiki, "Pullback of Riemannian Metric ... iff Mapping is Immersion," tracking Lee Prop. 13.9/Ch. 13]. The spectral decomposition into three sectors (:3047) presumes a spectrum with enough non-trivial eigenvalues to fill an observable sector, a subthreshold sector, and an internal sector. The manuscript itself flags the precondition: the decomposition holds "when $\dim(\mathcal C)$ is large enough to accommodate them" (:3021).

In the framework's only computationally instantiated construction — transformers — the manuscript states $\dim\mathcal C = 0$ (:482 in the bundle-figure caption, and the section title "Transformer Architectures as the Zero-Dimensional Limit," :1604). When $n = 0$, $G_i(c)$ is the empty $0\times 0$ tensor with *zero* eigenvalues. There is no observable sector, no subthreshold sector, no internal sector — there is no spectrum at all. The three-sector decomposition is not merely speculative there; it is vacuous, because the carrier $T_c\mathcal C$ is the zero vector space. The manuscript demonstrates no construction in which $n$ is large enough to exhibit even two sectors, let alone the asserted three-tier hierarchy.

## Why this is decisive, not pedantic
The claim under debate asserts the decomposition is "well-defined." A decomposition of an empty spectrum is well-defined only in the trivial sense that the empty set partitions into three empty sets. The substantive content — that a generic induced metric *separates* into three sectors of vastly different size — has no witness in the manuscript. The bookkeeping (concededly correct) tells you the spectrum has $\le n$ eigenvalues; it does not tell you $n$ is ever large, and the one place the framework computes anything, $n = 0$.

## Falsification condition (when RED is wrong here)
RED loses this vector if blue exhibits a construction in the framework — transformer or multi-agent — with $\dim\mathcal C \ge 5$ and a section $\sigma$ that is an immersion (so $G_i$ is non-degenerate), in which the induced spectrum *actually* exhibits the three-sector separation rather than having it imposed by thresholds. Absent such a witness, the well-definedness claim is empty in the realized case.
