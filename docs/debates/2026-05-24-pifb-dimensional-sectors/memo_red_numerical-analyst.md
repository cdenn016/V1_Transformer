# Memo — numerical-analyst (RED, opening)

## Steelman of the claim
Eigenvalue thresholding is a standard, well-understood operation (spectral truncation, PCA-style rank selection). If the spectrum has genuine gaps — clusters of eigenvalues separated by orders of magnitude — then partitioning at the gaps is well-defined and stable. The section asserts exactly such gaps (:3061).

## Falsification target
A two-threshold partition of a spectrum into three well-separated clusters is well-defined *only when the gaps exist and are large relative to perturbation*. The relevant canon is the Davis–Kahan $\sin\theta$ theorem: the invariant subspace spanned by a cluster of eigenvalues is stable (and the cluster is meaningfully separable) only when there is an eigengap $\delta$ separating that cluster from the rest of the spectrum; the perturbation of the subspace is bounded by $\|E\|/\delta$, which blows up as $\delta \to 0$ [Davis & Kahan 1970, "The rotation of eigenvectors by a perturbation. III," *SIAM J. Numer. Anal.* 7(1):1–46; Stewart & Sun 1990, *Matrix Perturbation Theory*, Ch. V]. Weyl's inequality bounds how far each eigenvalue can move under perturbation $E$ by $\|E\|$ [Bhatia 1997, *Matrix Analysis*, Ch. III], so a partition placed inside a region of densely-spaced eigenvalues is not robust: an arbitrarily small change to $\sigma$ or to the base coordinates moves eigenvalues across the cut and reshuffles sector membership.

The manuscript provides no evidence that the induced spectrum *has* the gaps it asserts. Eq. at :3061 ($\lambda_{\rm obs}\gg\lambda_{\rm sub}\gg\lambda_{\rm int}\approx 0$) is stated as a property the spectrum satisfies, with no spectral-gap argument, no computed spectrum, and no condition under which the gaps would arise. Without a demonstrated gap, the two thresholds $\Lambda_{\rm obs},\Lambda_{\rm subthresh}$ are placed in the interior of an unknown (possibly gapless) spectrum, and the three-set partition is then arbitrary in the precise Davis–Kahan sense: the resulting sectors are not perturbation-stable, so they carry no invariant meaning. "Choose two cut points on a monotone list" always succeeds and always returns three sets; that the operation succeeds is not evidence the sets are real.

## The structural point
The hierarchy of *sizes* $|\mathcal D_{\rm obs}| \ll |\mathcal D_{\rm sub}| \ll |\mathcal D_{\rm int}|$ (:3057) is doubly free: it depends on both threshold values, and on an unmodeled spectral profile. For any spectrum and any target $(|\mathcal D_{\rm obs}|, |\mathcal D_{\rm sub}|, |\mathcal D_{\rm int}|)$ summing to $n$, two cut points realizing those cardinalities exist. So the size hierarchy at :3057 forbids nothing about the spectrum — it is automatically satisfiable by threshold choice. A construction that can fit any target by dial-turning has no empirical content (this is the numerical face of the philosophy-of-science ad-hoc point).

## Falsification condition (when RED is wrong here)
RED loses this vector if blue exhibits an actual induced spectrum (computed, or argued from a gap theorem) with two genuine eigengaps of magnitude large relative to perturbation, located where the thresholds are placed, so that the three sectors are Davis–Kahan-stable. Asserting the gaps (:3061) without exhibiting them does not meet this.

## Newly-discovered canon
- Davis & Kahan 1970, "The rotation of eigenvectors by a perturbation. III," *SIAM J. Numer. Anal.* 7(1):1–46 — the $\sin\theta$ theorem: subspace/cluster separability requires a nonzero eigengap; perturbation bound scales as $\|E\|/\delta$. (Lecture-note restatement: Columbia COMS-4772 notes, https://www.cs.columbia.edu/~djhsu/coms4772-f16/lectures/davis-kahan.pdf)
- Stewart & Sun 1990, *Matrix Perturbation Theory* (Academic Press), Ch. V — gap-dependent invariant-subspace perturbation bounds.
- Bhatia 1997, *Matrix Analysis* (Springer GTM 169), Ch. III — Weyl's inequality $|\lambda_k(A+E)-\lambda_k(A)| \le \|E\|$; eigenvalues in a dense region are not separably clusterable.
