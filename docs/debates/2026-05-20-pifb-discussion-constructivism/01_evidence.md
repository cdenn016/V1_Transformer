Minimal primary-source pointers teams should consult:

- **Glasersfeld, E. von (1995)**, *Radical Constructivism: A Way of Knowing and Learning*, Falmer Press. Core thesis: "Knowledge is not passively received either through the senses or by way of communication. Knowledge is actively built up by the cognizing subject" (Glasersfeld 1989 abstract; restated in Glasersfeld 1995 §1). Cognition is adaptive and serves the organization of the experiential world, not the discovery of an objective reality.
- **Piaget, J. (1970)**, *Genetic Epistemology*, Columbia University Press. Knowledge is a constructive process; cognitive structures are assembled through assimilation and accommodation, not received from a pre-given reality.
- **von Foerster, H. (1981)**, *Observing Systems*, Intersystems Publications. Second-order cybernetics: the observer is part of the system observed; "objectivity is the delusion that observations can be made without an observer."
- **Maturana, H. and Varela, F. (1980)**, *Autopoiesis and Cognition: The Realization of the Living*, Reidel. Living systems are autopoietic — self-producing — and cognition is the operation of such systems; reality is "brought forth" by the observer (a position later termed *bringing forth a world* in Maturana & Varela 1987, *The Tree of Knowledge*, Shambhala).
- **Varela, Thompson, Rosch (1991)**, *The Embodied Mind: Cognitive Science and Human Experience*, MIT Press. Enactive cognition: knowing is enacting; the world is not pre-given but is brought forth through the structural coupling of an autonomous agent with its environment.
- **Ramstead, Kirchhoff, Friston (2019/2020)**, "A tale of two densities: active inference is enactive inference," *Adaptive Behavior* 27/28. Explicit derivation that the Friston free-energy framework is mathematically a formalization of the Maturana–Varela enactive-cognition program. Quoted in the literature as the bridge that makes constructivist commitments operative in the VFE program. (Already in `references.bib` but not cited in the manuscript.)
- **Friston, K. (2010)**, "The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?", *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* 11(2), 127–138. The variational free-energy formalism itself; in the philosophical literature, this paper's "model the world to predict it" framing is routinely cited as constructivist (predictive processing → constructivist epistemology in Clark 2016 §1).
- **Hohwy, J. (2013)**, *The Predictive Mind*, OUP. Argues predictive processing is "neo-Kantian and broadly constructivist." Cited as the position the framework is closest to but is not currently named.
- **Friedman, M. (2001)**, *Dynamics of Reason*, CSLI. Already cited in the manuscript. The neo-Kantian relativized-a-priori tradition is constructivist-adjacent but distinct from the German-Idealist / Kantian thread already used; teams should distinguish these.

## What this evidence does NOT settle

1. Whether the constructivist content the framework realizes is *substantively distinct* from what Kantian idealism, predictive processing, Friedman's relativized a priori, and Lahav–Neemeh relational physicalism *already contribute* in the Discussion — or whether constructivism would only relabel content the manuscript has already credited.
2. Whether the Ramstead-Kirchhoff-Friston "enactive inference" thesis is a *derivation* (in which case omitting it is a scholarship debt) or a *philosophical commentary* (in which case the manuscript is free to ignore it).
3. Whether "constructivism" is sufficiently well-defined as a single position to be cited compactly, or whether it dissolves into incompatible sub-traditions (mathematical constructivism, Piagetian cognitive constructivism, social constructivism, radical constructivism, enactivism) such that naming "constructivism" without specifying which would be sloppy scholarship.
4. Whether the Discussion is already too long and adding a constructivism subsection would harm readability more than the citation would help intellectual honesty.
5. Whether weaving constructivist citations into existing subsections (preferred to a new subsection) would actually be tractable given the existing paragraph structure.

Teams are required to address points 1–3 with primary-source citations; points 4–5 are editorial judgment calls the judge may rule on.
