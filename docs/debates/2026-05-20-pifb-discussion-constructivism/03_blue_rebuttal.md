# Blue Rebuttal — pifb-discussion-constructivism

## Concession

I grant three points from `02_red_opening.md`, two on terminology and one on the alignment sub-claim as literally written.

**(i)** Red is correct that Ramstead, Kirchhoff & Friston 2020 contains the word "constructivism" zero times. Verified by primary-source fetch of the published article (PMC7418871): the central thesis is that "active inference is the name of the formulation for policy selection... perception is a form of action" and the paper cites Varela, Thompson, & Rosch (1991) and Thompson (2010) under the *enactive* heading, not under any constructivist heading. The evidence pack's framing of the bib-but-not-cited anomaly as a "constructivism" anomaly is mis-targeted. The anomaly is an enactivism anomaly.

**(ii)** Red is correct that Maturana and Varela explicitly disavow the constructivist label. The academic record on this is unambiguous: there is a published article titled "*Maturana Is Not a Constructivist… Nor Is Piaget*" (Reid & Mgombelo 2014, *Complicity* 11(2)), Varela (1996) characterizes his theory as diverging from "any form of constructivism," and the standard scholarly distinction has the enactive program "dissociating" from constructivism over the elimination of the subject-object dichotomy. The claim as literally written, which couples Glasersfeld's *radical constructivism* with the Maturana-Varela autopoietic / enactive line as a single "constructivist tradition," conflates positions that the principals themselves treat as distinct.

**(iii)** The alignment sub-claim as literally drafted overreaches. `Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:3344` commits to "information-geometric structuralism: the substrate is the gauge-theoretic information geometry on principal bundles," and `:3386` commits to a "stronger position, pan-agentic structuralism: there is no observer-independent physical substrate sitting underneath the agents, and physical reality is itself constituted by the gauge-theoretic information geometry of agents-at-scales." This is a positive substrate ontology. Glasersfeld's 1995 radical constructivism refuses precisely this move ("makes no claim to describe an independent reality"). The framework therefore is not Glasersfeld-style radical constructivism. The honest characterization is: *structuralist with a noumenal substrate that the agents have no direct access to and that the manuscript explicitly treats as structureless until accessed*. That is neither Glasersfeld nor Kant; see Core attack below.

These concessions narrow but do not collapse the claim. The omission sub-claim and the targeted remediation sub-claim survive the narrowing, and the brief acknowledged that the omission sub-claim is the strongest sub-claim and survives partial-alignment failure.

## Core attack

Red's argument has a load-bearing weakness at falsification condition #2, where red asserted that "agent-relative geometries is Clark/Seth/Hoffman PP (cited), and consensus-driven alignment is Kuhn/van Fraassen/Friedman (all cited)" and concluded the omission sub-claim is contradicted by primary-source examination of what the cited authors stand for. Two specific failures.

**Failure 1: the cited PP authors are themselves the modern constructivist lineage, and naming them without naming the lineage is the omission the claim points to.** Hohwy 2013 (cited by the manuscript at `Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:3273-3279` indirectly through Clark/Seth/Hoffman, and a standard PP source) is described in the Notre Dame Philosophical Reviews and standard secondary literature as connecting "predictive coding, Bayesian inference, and the constructivist view of perception where the brain actively builds models rather than passively receiving information" [Notre Dame Philosophical Reviews of Hohwy's *The Predictive Mind*, OUP 2013]. The Swanson 2016 *Frontiers in Systems Neuroscience* paper "The Predictive Processing Paradigm Has Roots in Kant" establishes the explicit Kant → Helmholtz → PP lineage [Swanson 2016, §"Helmholtz" and §"Hyperpriors"]. The published philosophy of mind literature describes PP as a "neo-neo-Kantian view of the relationship between mind and world" [Sims & Pezzulo 2017 commentary, *Frontiers*; characterising Hohwy 2013]. Red's move is to cite the manuscript's citation of Clark/Seth/Hoffman as evidence that the constructivist debt is discharged. The opposite reading is on the evidence: the manuscript cites the *authors* but never names what those authors themselves call their position. A citation-form gap is exactly what an omission sub-claim is about.

**Failure 2: red's "manuscript rejection of noumenon" falsification condition mis-identifies the position the manuscript actually holds.** The manuscript does not reject the noumenon; red is right that it posits one (`:3102`, `:3344`, `:3386`). What the manuscript does, at `Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:3442`, is reject the position Kant actually held: "Kant's thing-in-itself is not just epistemically inaccessible but structureless until accessed through an agent's information-processing architecture." Kant retained noumenal causation and noumenal structure — the noumenon affects the senses and "the categories" mediate that affecting. A noumenon that is *structureless until accessed* is not Kant's noumenon; Kant would call this incoherent. The position is closer to Glasersfeld's "matter-of-fact withdrawal from explicit ontological discourse" combined with second-order-cybernetic constitutivity (von Foerster: "objectivity is the delusion that observations can be made without an observer"). Red's falsification condition #2 demanded that blue identify "a specific formal commitment in the manuscript that no currently-cited tradition covers, and that maps to a specific constructivist primary text." The "structureless until accessed" thesis at line 3442 is exactly that: Nagel 1986 ("no view from nowhere") is an epistemic-access claim that does not deny noumenal structure; Friedman 2001 (cited at 3403) is a relativized-a-priori position about constitutive principles within a theory and is silent on noumenal structure; Ladyman & Ross structural realism (cited at 3403, 3448) commits to mind-independent structure, which "structureless until accessed" denies. The framework's move at 3442 is uncovered by any currently-named tradition. The constructivist primary text it maps to is von Foerster 1981 *Observing Systems* and the constructivist commitment that what counts as "structure" is constituted in the act of agent-access; this is the move the framework realizes mathematically through agent-frame-dependent pullback metrics from a base manifold that is treated as substrate-only.

## Defense

It does not name the lineage that connects its own load-bearing moves — the "no view from nowhere" combined with substrate-structurelessness at 3442, the species-relative physics at 3293, the autonomous-agent / semantic-coordinate-system construction at 3193 and 3207 — to the modern philosophical position those moves realize. That position is best named two ways at two places:

   — At line 3442 (the "structureless until accessed" passage), a Hohwy 2013 *The Predictive Mind* citation is the targeted weave. Hohwy's "internalist" predictive-processing position is the published philosophical home of the noumenon-as-structureless reading the manuscript advances, and Hohwy is the figure the secondary literature names as the most prominent PP-constructivist. Cost: one `\cite{Hohwy2013}`. Cumulative editorial overhead: zero new subsection.

   — At line 3193 or 3207 (the autonomous-agent / each-token-has-its-own-internal-gauge-frame passage), a Ramstead, Kirchhoff & Friston 2020 citation discharges the bib-staged anomaly, named correctly as enactive-inference rather than constructivism. The match at 3193/3207 is structural and exact. Cost: one `\cite{RKF2020}`, naming it as the enactive-inference reading rather than as constructivism.

   On the Glasersfeld and Maturana-Varela citations specifically: I do not defend their addition as such. Red is correct that those would be the wrong names. The strongest defensible remediation is the two-citation weave above, plus an optional sentence at 3289 acknowledging that the cognitive-shareability reading sits in the same neighborhood as second-order cybernetics (von Foerster 1981) without claiming identity. The user's brief explicitly anticipated this narrowing: "the targeted weave — a Glasersfeld citation at line 3293, a Ramstead-Kirchhoff-Friston citation at line 3193/3207, and a Hohwy / von Foerster citation at line 3287-3289 — NOT a new subsection."

Both have primary-source homes in the published philosophy literature, both map to specific manuscript lines, and neither is currently cited by name.
