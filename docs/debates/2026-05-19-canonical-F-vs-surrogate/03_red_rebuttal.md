# Red Rebuttal — canonical-F-vs-surrogate

## Concession

Three points from `02_blue_opening.md` are granted on the evidence.

First, the covariance-form gap at `Attention/GL(K)_attention.tex:870–872` is correct as a theorem. Blue's sympy session (`02_blue_opening.md:27–45`) and the direct chain-rule calculation in `01_evidence.md:81–95` both yield `gap − (-τ⁻¹ Cov_{β*}(E, ∂E/∂x)) = 0`. Sub-claim α is decided in blue's favour.

Second, the in-line disclosure at `Attention/GL(K)_attention.tex:874` is verbatim what it announces itself to be: "The gradient expressions below are therefore gradients of the attention-weighted energy `Σ_j β_{ij}* E_{ij}` ... not of the reduced free energy `F_red`; the two differ by (eq:autograd_envelope_gap)." This is plain-language disclosure, not concealment. Sub-claim γ is decided in blue's favour. The same disclosure is repeated at line 1483 ("the autograd objective") and line 1937 ("absent from the gradient of F_red ... but present in standard autograd implementations").

Third, the supplementary §B.1 line 183 claim — that the `Σ_i` gradient is identical under both forms because the attention entropy `τβ log(β/π)` does not depend on `Σ_i` — is mathematically correct when β is held fixed. Sub-claim δ is decided in blue's favour at the stated qualifier. Red maintains the qualifier itself is load-bearing (when β is autograd-tracked through `E_{ij}(Σ_i)`, the gap re-enters on `Σ_i`), but the supplementary's specific equation does not misstate this.

The debate therefore turns on sub-claim ε: does the framework's gradient flow do what the framework analytically claims it does?

## Core attack

Blue's load-bearing defense is move (iv) at `02_blue_opening.md:14`: the retained `∂β/∂x` terms "are not an algorithmic accident — the manuscript at line 1937 identifies them with the softmax-gradient nonlinearity that constitutes the framework's GLU/FFN-style activation in the variational E-step. The autograd convention is therefore *load-bearing* for the FFN-as-VFE-iterations correspondence (§ffn_nonlinearity, line 1944–1946), not a corner cut." This inverts the load-bearing logic of the claim.

The claim is about which functional the framework's gradient flow descends on. The framework's analytical results — the softmax-β derivation at `Attention/GL(K)_attention.tex:768`, the reduced-F definition at `:845–852`, the envelope-form gradient at `:861–866` ("with no explicit `∂β*/∂x` terms, since the cross-term ... vanishes at the optimal attention where `∂F/∂β_{ij} = 0`") — describe `F_red` and its envelope gradient. The implementation, by blue's own concession via line 874, descends on `⟨E⟩_{β*}` and its autograd gradient. Blue defends this gap by reading from the implementation side and labeling what the autograd computes as the framework's predicted GLU activation.

That reading is consistent with the architectural taxonomy of §5.4. It does not justify the analytical-side claim. Three points support this attack.

(1) **The §5.4 GLU correspondence is descriptive, not derivational.** Line 1937 reads "the autograd gradient ... contains an additional nonlinear correction from differentiating `β_{ij}` with respect to `μ_i` (this term is absent from the gradient of the reduced free energy `F_red` by the envelope theorem, but present in standard autograd implementations; cf. §final_free_energy)." The parenthetical is a description of what autograd does relative to the envelope theorem. It does not derive `F_red` from a GLU-required iteration scheme. The §5.4 taxonomy identifies family membership in the Gaussian-gated linear-unit class (lines 1925–1931: "they are not functionally identical to it"; "the correspondence is one of family membership rather than functional identity"), and at line 1944 names the centered softmax-gradient term as "a higher-order non-linearity beyond the first-order GLU gate" that is "unique to the VFE setting." Calling this an FFN-style activation is an architectural analogy, not a proof that `F_red` is what is being minimized. Blue's move (iv) reads the analogy backwards: it argues that because the framework's architectural reading wants the `∂β/∂x` term, the framework's variational objective is therefore being descended on. That is the converse of what the manuscript's analytical chain shows.

(2) **The "share critical points at joint stationarity" defense (move iii) is asymptotic.** Blue at `02_blue_opening.md:13` writes: "Both objectives share the same critical-point set on the joint fixed-point manifold where `β = β*(x)` solves `∂F/∂β = 0` *simultaneously* with `∂F_red/∂x = 0`. The E-step's β recomputation at each iteration keeps β on the stationarity manifold for any current x; the gap is therefore the off-equilibrium-in-x discrepancy only, not an arbitrary objective swap." This concedes the entire load-bearing point. The framework's training trajectory is precisely the off-equilibrium-in-x regime — the whole point of running an E-step is that `(μ, Σ, φ)` are not at the joint stationary point yet. Restricting agreement to the joint fixed-point manifold is not a defense that the framework descends on `F_red` during training; it is a defense that the two coincide once training has already finished. The manuscript at `:874` itself states this explicitly: "this holds at the joint stationary point of `F_red` in `x` and in the high-temperature limit `τ → ∞`, but not generically off-equilibrium." Off-equilibrium is where the framework operates.

(3) **The standard-transformer analogy is a non-sequitur at the variational-objective level.** Blue at `02_blue_opening.md:57` cites Vaswani et al. 2017 §3.2.1 and the field-standard practice of differentiating through softmax. The manuscript at `:874` cites the same: "Standard transformer training follows the autograd convention (differentiating through the softmax), and we adopt the same convention." Standard transformers do not claim to minimize a variational functional `F` whose stationary points are derived from a soft-assignment Lagrangian; they minimize cross-entropy directly via SGD, and "differentiate through the softmax" is sound at the level of computational practice. The framework's claim that `F` is being minimized is a stronger claim than standard transformers make about their loss function, and the stronger claim is not preserved by importing the autograd convention. Reading line 874's "we adopt the same convention" as a justification of the variational-objective interpretation is a category transfer: computational practice (autograd through softmax) does not entail variational-objective fidelity (descent on `F_red`).

The previous-debate verdict at `docs/debates/2026-05-19-softmax-beta-stationary-point/04_verdict.md:13` is reinforcing here. That verdict declared `RED_WINS` on the grounds that the canonical `F_align^(τ)` is "engineered" — the entropy term `τβ log(β/π)` is "added to make the softmax form of β a stationary point" — not derived from a joint ELBO. The decisive citation was the supplementary's own additive language at line 183. Combining that verdict with the present debate: the canonical `F_red` is a posited soft-assignment Lagrangian (per the prior debate), and the implementation then descends on a different posited functional (`⟨E⟩_{β*}`) by line 874's adoption. The framework's analytical headline — that `F_red` is the variational objective being minimized — therefore stacks two interpretive translations: (a) `F_align^(τ)` is engineered rather than derived, and (b) the implementation descends on the surrogate of (a) rather than (a) itself. Each translation is disclosed; the compound effect is that `F_red` is, on the evidence, neither derived from a Bayesian joint nor minimized by the algorithm. It is a functional whose stationary-point structure motivates the softmax rule and whose autograd surrogate is what gradient descent acts on.

## Defense

The red opening's load-bearing assertion is sub-claim ε: the framework's E-step gradient flow under the autograd convention does not descend on `F_red` off-equilibrium. Blue's defense leaves this intact.

First, blue concedes (move ii) that line 874 is in-line disclosure of the substitution. Disclosure is not equivalent to absence of substitution. The claim is not that the manuscript hides the substitution; the claim is that the substitution exists and that the analytical results downstream of line 855 describe `F_red` while the algorithm in §5 descends on `⟨E⟩_{β*}`. Blue's disclosure defense and the claim are compatible; the claim survives.

Second, blue's magnitude argument at `02_blue_opening.md:55` ("the gap scales as τ⁻¹ times a covariance ... O(1/τ) correction whose magnitude is bounded by ... at joint stationarity the covariance vanishes by sub-claim β; off-equilibrium the τ⁻¹ scaling makes it a perturbation of the canonical envelope gradient, not a wholesale substitution") is an empirical/scale heuristic without empirical validation. With `τ = κ√K` per `:857` and `κ` learnable per the same line, the working `τ` is not bounded away from zero by construction — `κ` is trained. At `K = 64` and the typical small-`κ` regime where attention sharpens, `τ ≈ κ · 8` can be O(1). The `O(1/τ)` bound on the gap then does not establish that the gap is a small perturbation. Blue presents no training-time measurement of `‖∇⟨E⟩ − ∇F_red‖ / ‖∇F_red‖` to substantiate the perturbation framing. Without that, the claim "the framework descends on `F_red` up to a small perturbation" is unsupported by the evidence pack.

Third, blue's GLU-correspondence defense (move iv) does not address what the framework's stationary-point analysis demonstrates about the implementation's dynamics. The §4.6 softmax-β derivation at line 768 and the envelope-form gradient at lines 861–866 are statements about `F` and `F_red` respectively. The §5.4 GLU correspondence at line 1937 is a statement about what autograd computes. The two are not in conflict — they describe different objects — and the framework can simultaneously hold (a) `F_red` has the envelope gradient at line 862, and (b) the autograd implementation computes a different gradient that is interpretable as a GLU activation. What the framework cannot hold without additional argument is (c) the implementation, by descending on the autograd gradient, descends on `F_red`. Blue's defense conflates (a)+(b) with (c). The claim attacks (c).

Fourth, the prior-debate verdict at `docs/debates/2026-05-19-reduction-to-standard-transformer/04_verdict.md:5` (`RED_WINS`) is structurally analogous. There the compound claim was "standard scaled-dot-product attention is recovered exactly (not approximately) with every intermediate step mathematically valid"; the verdict broke on the manuscript's own use of "approximate" three times at lines 1198, 1213, 1271, 1289. Here the compound claim is "the framework minimizes `F_red`"; the verdict-breaking lines are the manuscript's own line 874 ("not of the reduced free energy `F_red`; the two differ by ..."), line 1483 ("the autograd objective"), and line 1937 ("absent from the gradient of `F_red` by the envelope theorem, but present in standard autograd implementations"). The pattern is the same: the manuscript discloses the gap in its own text, blue defends the disclosed gap as a feature, and the disclosure is precisely the evidence that the headline overclaims.

The honest reading the manuscript supports under the present evidence is: `F_red` is a posited soft-assignment Lagrangian whose softmax-β stationary point motivates the attention rule, and the framework's iterative E-step descends on the attention-weighted energy `⟨E⟩_{β*}` rather than on `F_red`, with the two coinciding only at joint stationarity or in the `τ → ∞` limit. The retained `∂β/∂x` terms are correctly identified at §5.4 as a softmax-gradient nonlinearity belonging to the Gaussian-gated GLU family. The framework's stationary-point analysis (softmax-β rule, envelope gradient) describes `F_red`; the framework's algorithm descends on `⟨E⟩_{β*}`; the two are not the same vector field off-equilibrium, and "the framework minimizes `F_red`" is therefore not what the implementation does. The headline of sub-claim ε stands.
