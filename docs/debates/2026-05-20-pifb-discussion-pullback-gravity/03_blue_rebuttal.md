## Core attack

R3 misreads the precedent comparison. Red's argument is that Verlinde 2011 and Jacobson 1995 use derivation language ("Newton's law of gravitation is shown to arise naturally and unavoidably," "directly leads to the Einstein equations" [arXiv:1001.0785 abstract]; "The Einstein equation is derived from the proportionality of entropy and horizon area together with the fundamental relation $\delta Q = T\,dS$" [Jacobson 1995, PRL 75:1260, abstract]), while the manuscript at line 3121 states that "no analog of the Einstein field equations has been derived" — and therefore the manuscript's "(Speculative)" label imports the social meaning of those precedents without paying their structural cost.

This argument inverts the calibration logic. The debated claim is whether the subsection's hedges are correctly calibrated to its derivation level, not whether its derivation level matches Verlinde's. The primary-source language red cites cuts directly against red's framing: Verlinde 2011 says "is shown to arise"; Jacobson 1995 says "is derived"; the manuscript at 3121 says "has not been computed ... has not been derived ... has not been carried out beyond dimensional matching" (`Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:3121`). On the hedge-calibration axis — which is the axis the claim is litigated on — the manuscript outhedges the canonical emergent-gravity precedents. Verlinde and Jacobson present their results as derivations whose foundational premises (entropic counting in Verlinde, the Clausius relation applied to local Rindler horizons in Jacobson) are themselves conjectural; the manuscript presents its result as a structural ansatz whose mathematical content is admitted not to have been produced. Both are speculative; the manuscript is more transparent about exactly where the speculation enters.

Red's correct observation that the manuscript is qualitatively WEAKER than Verlinde/Jacobson in derivation content does not establish that the manuscript's hedging is INSUFFICIENT. It establishes the opposite: a weaker result correctly registered with a stronger hedge is calibrated. A weaker result registered with a Verlinde-strength hedge ("is shown to arise") would be miscalibrated. The manuscript registers, in plain words at 3121, that no curvature tensor has been computed and no Einstein-equation analogue has been derived. That registration is what makes the hedge calibrated.

The structural positive claim of the subsection survives red's attack and is identifiable from the text. Line 3114 specifies a conditional research program with three explicit prerequisites: "(i) that gravitational effects emerge from $G^{(s)}$ curvature (not yet shown), (ii) that this curvature couples to $\Sigma_{s,i}^{-1}$ rather than to some other functional of the model-fiber section (an assumption, not a derivation), and (iii) that some derivable relation between $\Sigma_{s,i}^{-1}$ and $\Sigma_{p,i}^{-1}$ would be needed to recover an equivalence-principle-style identification of inertial and gravitational mass (also assumed)" (`Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:3114`). This is the positive content: a three-prerequisite roadmap whose dynamical fulfilment would upgrade the subsection from speculative to derivational. The "(Speculative)" label and the 3121 admission together register exactly where on that roadmap the manuscript currently sits.
