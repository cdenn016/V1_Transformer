# Expert Memo — Red / Philosophy-of-Science — pifb-pullback-mechanism (opening)

## Steelman

The claim is carefully hedged. The manuscript labels the horizontal block "tw" not "YM" to avoid overclaiming a curvature invariant (:2747), discloses the gauge-noninvariance rather than burying it (:2768), flags the Hoffman attribution caveat (:2797), and states it "does not adjudicate the metaphysical question" of noumenal reachability (:2797). A charitable reading treats the whole section as an explicitly interpretive proposal ("we propose," :2795), and an interpretive proposal is not obligated to be falsifiable in the same way an empirical claim is. On that reading the section is honest philosophy, not disguised empirics.

## Strongest falsification

The framing of the debate claim conflates two registers and a circularity rides on the conflation.

(1) **Naming-as-authority circularity.** The claim's first conjunct — "$G^{(q)}$ is the genuine pullback of a bona-fide fiber-bundle metric ... and not merely an $L^2$ pullback" — is settled in the manuscript by *defining* $g_{E_q}$ and then *asserting* (:2726, :2747) that it is "a fiber-respecting metric on $E_q$" and "bona-fide." The word "bona-fide" does the work that a comparison to the canonical bundle-metric form (Kaluza–Klein / Sasaki) should do. The manuscript establishes the standard by stipulation rather than by matching an external canonical form. Under the source-of-truth rule, "the manuscript constructs $g_{E_q}$ and calls it bona-fide, therefore it is bona-fide" is malformed; the canonical form must come from Nakahara / Kobayashi–Nomizu, and (per the geometer and gauge-theorist memos) it does not match. The naming is the claim, not evidence for it.

(2) **Unfalsifiable tier-identification.** "Perceived space is most naturally identified with $G^{(s)}$" (:2795) has no falsification condition. The four tensors are "coexistent" (:2782); nothing in the construction forbids identifying perceived space with $G^{(p)}$ or $G^{(q)}$ instead, and no observation would adjudicate. "Most naturally" is an aesthetic predicate, not a testable one. The debate claim asks whether this is "well-posed (if interpretive) rather than an arbitrary choice." A choice among coexistent objects, defended by "most naturally" with no discriminating consequence, is precisely an interpretive preference; the qualifier "if interpretive" concedes the point the claim's verb "well-posed" tries to retain. Well-posed and interpretive-with-no-discriminator are in tension here.

(3) **Scope leakage / equivocation in the claim itself.** The debate claim bundles three propositions of different epistemic types: a formal mathematical claim (is $g_{E_q}$ canonical?), a definitional claim (are the four tiers well-defined?), and an interpretive claim (is the $G^{(s)}$ identification well-posed?). The middle is true and cheap (the four formulas are well-defined symbolic objects). The first is false on the canonical-form comparison. The third is interpretive with no discriminator. Aggregating them under one "is mathematically sound" verdict invites a split decision; they should be adjudicated separately, and the load-bearing first conjunct fails.

## External citation

[Popper, *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*, 1959] — the demarcation criterion: a claim with empirical (or here, structural-mathematical) content must specify what would refute it. The tier-identification specifies nothing. On the formal conjunct, the source-of-truth rule in this debate's own methodology [debate_methodology.md, "The source-of-truth rule"] is explicit: "Cite external standards ... not the user's own CLAUDE.md" — so "bona-fide" must be earned against [Nakahara2003 Ch. 10–11] / [KobayashiNomizu Vol. I], not asserted in the manuscript.

## Falsification condition

This frame-check is wrong if (i) the manuscript supplies a discriminator that would make "perceived space $=G^{(s)}$" testable, or (ii) the "bona-fide bundle metric" naming is backed by an external canonical-form match rather than by stipulation. Neither holds: (i) is absent (:2795 offers only "most naturally"), and (ii) fails against the canonical KK/Sasaki form (geometer memo).
