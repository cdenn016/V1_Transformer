# Expert Memo (Blue) — Philosophy of Science (MANDATORY)

## Lens

Falsifiability, well-posedness, theory-ladenness, circularity. My job is to frame-check the claim, police the temptation to defend the construction by citing the manuscript back at itself, and decide which sub-claims are well-posed and which are interpretive preference dressed as fact.

## Steelman of the claim

The claim is a conjunction of three distinct propositions, and they have very different epistemic status. (1) $g_{E_q}$ is a well-defined fiber-bundle metric and $G^{(q)}$ is its genuine pullback — a *mathematical* claim, true or false against differential-geometry canon. (2) The four tiers $G^{(q,p,s,r)}$ are well-defined coexistent metrics — also mathematical. (3) "Perceived space $=G^{(s)}$" — an *interpretive* claim about the contents of phenomenal experience. The honest defense separates these: (1) and (2) stand or fall on canon and are well-posed; (3) is a proposal that is well-posed only if a discriminant exists. The manuscript's own register — "We propose", "most naturally identified", explicit caveats at :2706, :2708, :2795 — already marks (3) as a proposal, not a derivation. The claim is defensible precisely because it does not overclaim (3).

## The circularity test — does the construction avoid self-authorization?

The signature blue failure mode is "the construction is sound because the manuscript constructs it." The pullback section largely avoids this. The vertical block is forced by Cencov uniqueness [Cencov1972] — external authority, not self-reference. The horizontal-block transformation law is the standard connection gauge-transformation [Nakahara2003 §10.4] — external. The bundle-metric recipe (H⊕V block sum from a connection) is the Sasaki/Kaluza–Klein construction [Bleecker1981; Frankel2011 Ch. 20] — external. So the *mathematical* claims (1) and (2) derive from canon, not from the manuscript asserting them. This is the correct standing for a defense: the manuscript is cited to identify *what is claimed*, and external canon is cited to establish *that it holds*.

One genuine circularity risk: the "$L^2$ vs bundle-metric pullback" distinction at :2726. The manuscript declares the score-only expression "does not earn the name pullback of a fiber-bundle metric." That declaration is true *by the manuscript's own definitional choice* of what $E$ and $g_E$ are. It is not a discovery that the $L^2$ form was deficient; it is the manuscript electing to specify a richer object. The honest framing: this is a definitional upgrade with real category-theoretic content (a metric on $E$ now exists) but whose only *numerical* consequence is the additive horizontal block — which vanishes when $A=0$ (:2771). So the distinction is substantive-as-definition, near-vacuous-as-formula in the flat-frame body regime. Concede this precisely rather than letting the manuscript's "does not earn the name" rhetoric carry weight it has not earned.

## The falsifiability of "perceived space $=G^{(s)}$"

This is the load-bearing scope question. An identification of a phenomenal property ("perceived space") with a formal object ($G^{(s)}$) is well-posed *as a scientific claim* only if there is a measurement that would distinguish the $G^{(s)}$-identification from a rival $G^{(p)}$- or $G^{(q)}$-identification. The manuscript offers a *rationale* (structure is stable and slow, posterior is fast and fluctuating, so perceived structure should map to the slow tier) but no *discriminant*. Worse for testability: in the reported simulations the slow channel is frozen (:2799), so $G^{(s)}$ is a constant and is never exercised against $G^{(q)}$ in a way that could expose a difference. Under Popperian falsifiability, the identification as stated is not yet falsifiable — it is an interpretive preference with an architectural motivation.

This is not fatal to the claim *as the manuscript frames it*, because the manuscript does not assert (3) as established fact — it proposes it, hedges it ("We propose… most naturally identified"), and explicitly states the framework "does not adjudicate the metaphysical question" (:2797). The well-posed core that survives is: *the tier-decomposition is mathematically well-defined, and the slow-tier assignment is architecturally motivated by the fast-state/slow-parameter split of hierarchical active inference.* That weaker claim is defensible. The stronger reading — that the framework establishes perceived space *is* $G^{(s)}$ — is not, and the manuscript does not make it.

## Kant / Hoffman / Friston citation hygiene

The Kant invocation (space as a form of intuition, a priori and prior to sensory content, :2797) is used as an *analogy* for "structural, slow, prior to content," not as a load-bearing premise — appropriate, since Kant is philosophical background not subject to mathematical review (per external_bibliography.md coverage note). The Hoffman citation is handled with unusual care: the manuscript explicitly distinguishes Hoffman's species-evolutionary timescale from its own within-lifetime Bayesian-architectural level and declines to attribute the within-lifetime reading to Hoffman (:2797). That is correct citation hygiene — the most common abuse (claiming a cited author supports a claim they did not make) is specifically avoided here. The Friston2017 citation is used for the genuine content it contains (parameter-vs-state learning), as the variational memo verifies.

## Falsification condition

The framing-level defense fails if: (a) the claim were a single undifferentiated proposition that smuggled the interpretive (3) in on the mathematical credibility of (1)–(2) — it does not; the manuscript separates them and hedges (3); or (b) the manuscript asserted (3) as established rather than proposed — it does not (:2795 "We propose", :2797 "does not adjudicate"); or (c) the mathematical claims rested on manuscript self-authorization rather than external canon — they do not (Cencov, Nakahara, Sasaki/KK supply the standing). The claim survives *as a conjunction of a defensible mathematical core and an explicitly-flagged interpretive proposal*. It would NOT survive if read as "the framework establishes that perceived space is $G^{(s)}$" — but that is a misreading the manuscript's own hedging forecloses. Concede (3)'s untestability as stated; defend (1), (2), and the architectural motivation of the $G^{(s)}$ assignment.
