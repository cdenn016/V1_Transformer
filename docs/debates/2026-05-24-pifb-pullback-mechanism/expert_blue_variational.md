# Expert Memo (Blue) — Variational Inference / Active Inference

## Lens

ELBO, hierarchical generative models, the fast-state-inference vs slow-parameter-learning separation in active inference. I assess sub-point 4: the identification of "perceived spatial / ambient geometry" with the structural tier $G^{(s)}$ (the slow generative-model pullback) rather than $G^{(p)}$ (prior/expectation) or $G^{(q)}$ (belief/posterior), and the use of the Friston2017 citation (:2797).

## Steelman of the claim

The four-tier $h\to s\to p\to q$ decomposition maps onto a genuine, canonical architectural separation in active inference: fast Bayesian state inference (posterior beliefs $q$ over hidden states, updated every observation) versus slow Bayesian parameter learning (the generative-model parameters $s$, updated on a much slower schedule). The claim that perceived *structural* geometry tracks the slow tier $G^{(s)}$ rather than the fast tier $G^{(q)}$ is the architecturally natural reading: structure is, by construction in hierarchical active inference, what the slow parameters encode, while the fast posterior encodes moment-to-moment content. So the manuscript's tier-assignment is not arbitrary — it is the assignment dictated by where the active-inference literature locates structural vs dynamic content.

## Derivation from external canon

Active inference rests on hierarchical (deep temporal) generative models with explicit timescale separation across levels [FristonEtAl2017 "Active inference: a process theory," *Neural Computation* 29:1–49]. The defining mechanism: hidden-*state* inference proceeds at the fast perceptual timescale (belief updating within a trial / observation epoch), whereas *parameter* learning — the concentration/Dirichlet parameters of the generative model — is updated at the *end* of each sequence of observations, i.e. on a slower across-trial schedule. This is the canonical two-timescale structure: fast state estimation, slow parameter learning. The process-theory paper makes the model parameters the carriers of learned structure precisely because they integrate evidence across many fast inference episodes (confirmed via the active-inference process-theory literature; the concentration-parameter update at trial boundaries is the standard learning rule, e.g. as restated in the discrete-state synthesis of Da Costa et al. 2020, *J. Math. Psych.* "Active inference on discrete state-spaces").

This grounds the manuscript's tier-assignment in canon, not in self-reference. The slow generative-model tier $s$ corresponds to what hierarchical active inference calls the learned structural model; the fast posterior $q$ corresponds to state inference. Identifying perceived *structure* (a stable, slowly-varying organizing scheme) with the slow tier and perceived *content* (instantaneous attention/expectation maps) with the fast tiers is the assignment the literature's own fast/slow split recommends. The manuscript's prose at :2797 states exactly this: it locates "the structural part of perceptual organization in Bayesian parameter learning of the generative model rather than in Bayesian inference at the state level," and attributes that to [Friston2017], which is the correct attribution — the parameter-vs-state-learning distinction is genuinely Friston's process-theory content.

The manuscript also handles the Hoffman citation honestly. It explicitly notes (:2797) that Hoffman's Interface-Theory of Perception [Hoffman2019] operates at the *species-evolutionary* timescale (a genome-level fitness-optimized interface), which is *not* the within-lifetime Bayesian-architectural level the framework commits to, and it states it does not attribute the within-lifetime reading to Hoffman. This is a correct and unusually careful use of the citation: Hoffman supports the broad thesis (perceived space is an optimized interface, not a faithful map of agent-independent geometry) at his timescale, and the manuscript routes the within-lifetime mechanism to Friston instead. That is the right division of citational labor.

## Honest concession — the load-bearing weakness

The *mathematical* tier-decomposition is well-defined: $G^{(s)}, G^{(p)}, G^{(q)}, G^{(r)}$ are four genuine pullbacks differing only in which fiber section ($s,p,q,r$) is pulled back, and they are well-posed objects (the info-geometer and geometer memos establish this). What is *not* settled by canon is the identification "perceived space $=G^{(s)}$" as opposed to $G^{(p)}$ or $G^{(q)}$. Active inference tells us where *structure* lives (slow parameters) and where *content* lives (fast states), but it does not tell us that phenomenal "perceived space" is the structural tier rather than, say, a fast attention-modulated overlay. Whether phenomenal spatial experience tracks the slow structural model or the fast posterior is an empirical/philosophical claim about the contents of experience, and the FEP canon does not adjudicate it. I therefore defend the *mathematical* well-posedness of the four tiers and the *architectural plausibility* of the $G^{(s)}$ assignment, but concede that "perceived space $=G^{(s)}$" is an interpretive identification, defensible as well-motivated but not as theorem. The manuscript's own hedging ("We propose…", "most naturally identified") is the honest register; it does not claim more than a proposal.

## Falsification condition

The variational defense fails if: (a) active inference located structural organization in fast state inference rather than slow parameter learning — it does not; the two-timescale fast-state/slow-parameter split is canonical [FristonEtAl2017]; or (b) the manuscript misattributed the within-lifetime mechanism to Hoffman — it does not, it explicitly disclaims that and routes it to Friston (:2797). The interpretive identification "perceived space $=G^{(s)}$" is falsifiable in principle only if a discriminating measurement exists (e.g., an agent with a frozen slow channel should report *stable* perceived geometry under rapid $G^{(q)}$ fluctuation; an agent with $G^{(s)}$ ablated should fail structural-perception tasks while passing attention tasks). The manuscript does not supply such a discriminant, and in the reported simulations the slow channel is *frozen* (:2799), so $G^{(s)}$ is constant and the assignment is not exercised dynamically. Absent that discriminant, the identification remains a well-motivated proposal, not a tested claim — which is exactly how the manuscript frames it.

## Newly-discovered canon

- [FristonEtAl2017 "Active inference: a process theory," *Neural Computation* 29:1–49] — fast state inference vs slow (across-trial) parameter learning; concentration parameters updated at trial boundaries carry learned structure. This is the canonical anchor for the slow/fast tier split.
- Da Costa, L. et al. (2020). "Active inference on discrete state-spaces: a synthesis." *J. Math. Psych.* 99:102447. — Restates the parameter-learning-as-slow-Dirichlet-update mechanism; freely available for verification of the two-timescale claim.
- [Friston2017Graphical "Graphical brain," *Network Neuroscience* 1(4):381–414] — hierarchical/nested formulation with structure encoded at slower, deeper levels.
