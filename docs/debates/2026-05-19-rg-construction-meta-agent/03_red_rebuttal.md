# Red Rebuttal — rg-construction-meta-agent

I grant blue's sub-claim A point on the *change-of-variables identities* (Theorems 1 and 2). The proofs at lines 4463-4465 and 4472-4481 are textbook pushforward-measure arguments `[Bogachev2007 Vol. I §3.6-3.7]`, the statements are correctly scoped, and the manuscript itself at line 4362 calls this "structural — it does not yet claim that $\mathcal{F}^{\mathrm{exact}}_{s+1}$ has multi-agent form." The objection I retain on sub-claim A is not against Theorems 1 and 2 as stated; it is against the *use* of that structural identity, plus the inter-cluster recursion at line 4404 and the schematic projection map $R_\mathfrak{M}(\theta_s) := \Pi_\mathfrak{M}(\mathcal{F}_{s+1}^{\mathrm{exact}})$ at line 4499, as a *renormalization group* in the sense of `[Wilson1971, Cardy1996]` rather than as a one-step change of variables (see core attack below).

I also grant that the body subsection's "RG-inspired" labeling at line 2137 and the finite-size scaling acknowledgment at lines 4577-4578 are correctly registered. The framing-as-RG-as-a-whole sub-claim D survives under that labeling discipline alone, and I will not press D further. My rebuttal therefore concentrates on A (Theorem 3 hypothesis), B (Theorem 4 class scope; Proposition 1 vacuity), and C (Theorem 5 free-Lipschitz assertion).

## Core attack

Blue's defense collapses into the following template: "the statement is conditional and the manuscript labels it as such; conditional theorems of the form 'suppose hypothesis $H$, then conclusion $C$' are standard." That template is true in general but fails here for three load-bearing reasons that blue's opening does not address.

**(1) Theorem 4 closes a different class than the manuscript claims to coarse-grain within.** The manuscript defines its functional class at line 4499 as $\mathfrak{M} = \{\mathcal{F}_\theta : \theta \in \Theta\}$ — the multi-agent *Gaussian quadratic KL* functional class — and defines the projected RG map as $R_\mathfrak{M}(\theta_s) := \Pi_\mathfrak{M}(\mathcal{F}_{s+1}^{\mathrm{exact}})$. Theorem 4 at lines 4508-4518 then proves the renormalized object lies *not in $\mathfrak{M}$* but in $\mathfrak{M}' := \mathfrak{M} + \{V : V(Y) = \tfrac{\tau}{2}\log\det{}'A_{\xi\xi}(Y), Y \mapsto A_{\xi\xi}(Y) \text{ smooth}\}$ — Gaussian quadratic KL augmented by a $Y$-dependent log-determinant potential. The proof at lines 4521-4522 explicitly states the augmented-class form is what closes; strict closure within $\mathfrak{M}$ "obtains in the special case where $A_{\xi\xi}$ is independent of $Y$" (line 4510). Blue agrees the substantive content is the augmented form (`02_blue_opening.md` line 30: "the substantive content is the augmented-class form with the local potential"). The implication blue does not address: an "RG" in the Wilsonian sense requires the renormalized free energy to lie in the *same* parametric class so that the map $R: \Theta \to \Theta$ can be *iterated* and a flow can be defined `[Wilson1971 §III; Cardy1996 §3.2-3.4; Goldenfeld1992 §9.4]`. A renormalized object that escapes $\mathfrak{M}$ at every step does not generate a flow on $\Theta$; it generates a one-shot coarse-graining. Theorem 4 therefore establishes that the construction is *not* a renormalization group on $\mathfrak{M}$ — it is a one-step pushforward whose image is in a strictly larger class.

**(2) Theorem 5's Lipschitz hypothesis is not "standard 'suppose $L$-Lipschitz' form."** Blue cites `[BleiKuckelbirgJordan2017 §3.3]` to claim the conditional structure is "identical to countless theorems in variational inference and statistical learning" (`02_blue_opening.md` line 44). In the variational inference literature blue cites, Lipschitz constants are bounds *on identified objects* (e.g., the score function of a known exponential-family model, the smoothness modulus of an explicit ELBO objective), and the auxiliary derivation pinning the constant to other established quantities is what makes the bound informative. Theorem 5 at line 4561 introduces *four* free parameters in a single sentence — the saving term $A_I > 0$, the belief-Lipschitz constant $L_q$, the model-Lipschitz constant $L_p$, and the closure residual $\varepsilon_I$ — with no auxiliary derivation showing $L_q, L_p < \infty$, no characterization showing $A_I > 0$, and (per the chain of dependence) no bound on $\varepsilon_I$ that is not Proposition 1's schematic bound with six further unspecified constants. The theorem's content is then "if all four free parameters cooperate, then $\Delta_I > 0$" — a tautology of the form "if the saving exceeds the cost, the gain is positive." Blue does not produce a single citation in which a variational-inference Lipschitz theorem introduces four unrelated free parameters with no auxiliary bounds on any of them.

**(3) Proposition 1 is a schema, not a statement.** Blue's defense at lines 33-34 of `02_blue_opening.md` is that "the honest labeling — Proposition, not Theorem — is the right form of mathematical hygiene." This conflates *honest labeling about underspecification* with *mathematical content*. The manuscript at line 4555 lists the three things unpinned: constants $C_k$, closure norm $\|\cdot\|_\mathcal{B}$, and regularity class of the parent ansatz — these are precisely the three pieces of information required for the inequality at lines 4548-4551 to be a falsifiable statement about *any* concrete object. Without them the inequality has no truth value: for any candidate witness $\varepsilon_{s+1}$ and any candidate right-hand side, one can choose $C_k$ to make the inequality hold or fail. Compare `[Wong2001 §II.1]` for the Laplace bound (constants explicitly tied to derivative norms of the integrand and to the Hessian's smallest eigenvalue) and `[Schwarz1978 Theorem 1]` for BIC (constant explicitly the model's effective dimension): both standard "schematic-looking" statements actually carry pinned-down constants. Proposition 1 does not. The paragraph heading "Controlled approximate closure" at line 4542 promises *control*; the proposition delivers a *schema for future control*. The honest labeling defense answers a different objection than the one I raised: I am not arguing the manuscript mislabeled a theorem as a proposition; I am arguing the *proposition itself* is not yet a mathematical statement, because the three universal quantifiers in its constants make every instance vacuously inequality-satisfying.

## Defense

Blue's opening foregrounded my opening's strongest attacks but did not retire them. I address the three points where blue's defense is strongest.

**Theorem 3 (sub-claim A) — gauge-invariance of the weights.** Blue claims at lines 22 of `02_blue_opening.md` that Theorem 3 invokes "three independent textbook facts" and the composition is gauge covariance. The manuscript's Theorem 3 statement at line 4491 is more circumscribed than blue's exposition admits: it *assumes* "the weights $w_i^I$ are gauge-invariant functions of gauge-invariant quantities such as transported KL divergences and support masks." This is a hypothesis, not a derived fact. The manuscript's actual working weight definition is at line 2127: $w_i^I(x) = \chi_i(x)\exp[-\mathrm{KL}(q_i \| \bar q_I)]$, with $\bar q_I$ itself defined as the weighted barycenter — that is, $\bar q_I$ appears on both sides of the weight definition through the barycenter Eq. 2122-2125, and the manuscript notes the formula is implicit and solved by fixed-point iteration (line 2128: "because $\bar{q}_I^{(s)}$ appears on the right-hand side through the weights, the formula is implicit and is solved by fixed-point iteration"). Whether the fixed-point weight is a gauge-invariant function of gauge-invariant quantities depends on (a) uniqueness of the fixed point, (b) gauge-covariance of the iteration map, and (c) gauge-equivariance of the chosen initialization $\bar q_I^{(s),(t-1)}$. None of (a)-(c) is established for the noncompact $\mathrm{GL}^+(K)$ setting of the framework's empirical work (lines 587, 2132). Theorem 3 therefore proves: *if the weights are gauge-invariant, then $\mathcal{R}_s$ is gauge-covariant* — a conditional whose hypothesis the manuscript does not discharge for its working definition. Blue's three-textbook-fact reading reads the proof; the open question is the *hypothesis*, which blue does not address.

**Theorem 4 (sub-claim B) — class scope.** I press the class-mismatch attack from the core attack above. Blue's "augmented-class form is non-trivial" position (lines 30-31) is correct on its own terms, but the substantive content blue defends — Gaussian KL plus smooth log-det $Y$-potential — is exactly what shows the construction does *not* close on the multi-agent functional class $\mathfrak{M}$ defined at line 4499. The honest reading of Theorem 4 is: "the manuscript's projection $\Pi_\mathfrak{M}$ at line 4499 is necessary precisely because the exact pushforward lands outside $\mathfrak{M}$." Blue's defense at line 12 of `02_blue_opening.md` treats this as a feature ("Theorem 4's substantive content is the standard adiabatic-elimination Laplace result"); it is in fact the *load* the manuscript's projection step must carry. The closure residual $\varepsilon_I$ at line 4372 is, in part, the energy of projecting the augmented-class log-det potential back into $\mathfrak{M}$. Whether that projection is small is precisely the question Proposition 1's schematic bound was supposed to answer — and Proposition 1 does not answer it (point 3 above).

**Theorem 5 (sub-claim C) — derivation of Lipschitz constants.** Blue concedes at line 44 of `02_blue_opening.md` that "the hypothesis is plausible in the high-coherence regime where the parent variational free-energy is bounded in second-derivative norm on a compact neighborhood, but it is not derived here." That concession is mine. Blue then defends the conditional form as standard `[BleiKuckelbirgJordan2017 §3.3]`; I addressed this in the core attack — the cited variational-inference Lipschitz theorems pin their constants to auxiliary objects, while Theorem 5 introduces $L_q, L_p, A_I$ fresh in the theorem statement with no auxiliary characterization. Blue's "the proof at lines 4569-4571 is a three-line consequence" point is correct — and the three-line proof is the entire mathematical content of the theorem, because the hypothesis does all the work. A theorem whose hypothesis already names the conclusion-direction quantity (the saving term $A_I > 0$) and provides the rate constants ($L_q, L_p$) is, mathematically, a definition of when the conclusion is true rather than a derivation that it is true.

**Sub-claim D — prior verdict overreach.** I conceded the framing-as-RG sub-claim above. I note for the record that the prior verdict at `docs/debates/2026-05-19-agent-meta-agent-hierarchy-theory/04_verdict.md` licensed "RG-inspired analogy" under the disclaimer at line 2137; the present claim's stronger phrase "mathematically correct and well-motivated renormalization-group construction" is *not* identical to "RG-inspired analogy" and does not automatically inherit the prior verdict's license. The manuscript still does not exhibit the iteration of $\kappa_{IJ}^R, \Omega_{IJ}^R$ that would constitute an RG flow on $\Theta$ in the sense of `[Wilson1971 §III, Cardy1996 §3.2]`. The body subsection's line 2140 mentions "$\beta_{IJ}^{(s+1)} = f_\beta(\{\beta_{ij}^{(s)}\})$" but does not specify $f_\beta$, exhibit a trajectory, or locate a fixed point. The prior verdict does not foreclose this attack; it foreclosed only the universality / critical-exponent attack. I retain the construction-is-not-iterated objection as part of point (1) of the core attack.

The integrated claim of "mathematically correct and well-motivated RG construction" therefore fails on three independent grounds: Theorem 4 closes a class strictly larger than the one the manuscript projects into; Proposition 1's underspecification is too severe to call the result a controlled approximate closure; Theorem 5's Lipschitz hypothesis is asserted with no auxiliary derivation of any of its four free parameters. The labeling discipline blue defends is a necessary condition for mathematical hygiene but is not sufficient for "mathematically correct" — labeling content as schematic does not convert schematic content into a mathematical statement.
