## Position

The Statistical Manifolds sub-claim (sub-claim 2) is falsifiable on a citation-scope mismatch at line 510. The manuscript invokes "the only Riemannian metric on probability spaces invariant under sufficient statistics" with a bare `\cite{Cencov1982}` and immediately specializes to Gaussian families on $\mathbb{R}^K$ and $\mathbb{R}^M$ (`:512-518`). Cencov's (Chentsov's) 1972/1982 theorem holds for finite sample spaces only; the uniqueness statement on continuous parametric models (which is what `:510` is operationally claiming, since the framework's statistical manifolds are continuous parametric Gaussian families) was supplied separately by Ay-Jost-Lê-Schwachhöfer (book, 2017) and by Lê (Ann. Inst. Statist. Math., published online 2016, journal 2017). The manuscript cites neither. This is a scope-misaligned citation, not a false mathematical claim — the underlying statement is correct under the extended theorem, but the cited source does not cover the stated scope.

## Evidence

- Manuscript `Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:510`: "The metric is unique up to scaling as the only Riemannian metric on probability spaces invariant under sufficient statistics~\cite{Cencov1982}." This is the sole uniqueness citation in §Statistical Manifolds (verified: grep over `:495-534` finds Cencov1982 only; Amari first appears at `:3876` in a natural-gradient context, AJLS/Lê do not appear in the manuscript at all).
- Manuscript `:512-518` immediately specializes to Gaussian families: $\mathcal{B}_{\mathrm{state}} = \{\mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma) : \mu \in \mathbb{R}^K, \Sigma \succ 0\}$. These are continuous parametric models, not finite sample spaces.
- [Lê 2013/2017, "The uniqueness of the Fisher metric as information metric", Ann. Inst. Statist. Math., arXiv:1306.1465 §1]: "In 1972 Chentsov proved that on statistical models $(M, \Omega, \mu, p)$ associated with **finite sample spaces** $\Omega$ the Fisher metric $g^F$ is a unique metric, up to a multiplicative constant, that satisfies (2)." The continuous-sample-space extension is what this paper and Ay-Jost-Lê-Schwachhöfer (2012, 2017) supply; the present 2013 paper itself is dedicated to filling the gap.
- [Ay, Jost, Lê, Schwachhöfer 2017, *Information Geometry*, Ergebnisse der Mathematik 64, Springer]: explicitly proves "generalizations and extensions of the classical uniqueness result of Chentsov" for parametrized measure models including continuous-sample-space cases; this is the canonical reference required when the uniqueness claim is applied to a Gaussian (or any continuous-parametric) family.

## Falsification conditions

The Statistical Manifolds sub-claim fails if either of the following holds.

First, if "probability spaces" at `:510` is read as inclusive of continuous parametric Gaussian families (which it must be, since the framework operationally uses Gaussian families at `:512-518`) and `\cite{Cencov1982}` is the only cited source for that uniqueness in §Statistical Manifolds (verified: it is), then the citation does not cover the stated scope and the load-bearing reference for Fisher-metric uniqueness in the framework's information geometry is mis-scoped. The correct citation is Ay-Jost-Lê-Schwachhöfer 2017 or Lê 2017 (added as a co-citation alongside Cencov 1982), neither of which appears in the manuscript.

Second, if the framework's downstream constructions (KL divergence as the only allowable divergence, natural-gradient flow being canonical, the Fisher-Rao geodesics being the canonical statistical distance) require the uniqueness theorem to hold *on the Gaussian families they use*, then a citation scoped only to finite sample spaces is insufficient support — the entire information-geometric backbone of the framework rests on a uniqueness result that is not cited in its operative form.

The fix is local and inexpensive: add AJLS 2017 or Lê 2017 as a co-citation at `:510` and qualify the claim with the scope of each ("Cencov 1972/1982 for finite sample spaces; AJLS 2017 / Lê 2017 for the extension to continuous parametric models including Gaussian families"). The substantive mathematical claim survives; the citation discipline does not.

A secondary, weaker strike — not load-bearing for the verdict but worth recording — is at line 561 ("the discrete analog of three well-developed mechanisms in modern gauge theory and quantum reference frames"). The cited literatures resolve related ambiguities but with mathematically distinct objects: Donnelly-Freidel 2016 edge modes are *new boundary degrees of freedom* introduced precisely because the bulk gauge connection is gauge-redundant (verified via arXiv:1601.04744 abstract: "introducing new degrees of freedom on the boundary"); Bartlett-Rudolph-Spekkens 2007 quantum reference frames promote a *separate physical system* to reference status; Rovelli 1996 relationalism is a metaphysical thesis that does not specify a frame field as such. The PIFB framework uses neither construction — it decomposes a single field $\phi_i$ by gauge subgroup action (global diagonal vs constant-per-agent residual), which the manuscript at `:565` does cleanly. The decomposition is mathematically sound on its own terms, but the appeal at `:561` to these antecedents is suggestive analogy rather than load-bearing structural import, and the manuscript would be tighter if it labeled the appeal as analogy (which the body of `:565` essentially does) rather than as "the discrete analog of" (which implies isomorphism). This is a Style/Editorial note, not a falsification of the sub-claim.
