# Blue Rebuttal — pifb-discussion-kinetic-term

## Concession

I grant red's strike (ii) in full. The sentence at `Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:3093` — "In traditional approaches predictive models are considered fixed" — is factually wrong against the active-inference canon. Friston (2010), *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* 11(2) §"Learning and Memory" (pp. 132–133) sets out slow-timescale plasticity updates of generative-model parameters driven by the same free-energy gradient that updates beliefs at fast timescale; Friston, FitzGerald, Rigoli, Schwartenbeck, Pezzulo (2017), *Neural Computation* 29(1) §"Learning" (pp. 22–25) gives within-trial Dirichlet concentration-parameter updates of the A, B, D matrices of the generative model; Parr, Pezzulo, Friston (2022), *Active Inference* (MIT Press) Ch. 3 organises the canonical timescale separation perception → action → learning → structure-learning, in which model parameters update on every timescale slower than belief inference. Treating predictive models as fixed is not "the traditional approach"; it is a fiction. The line at 3093 needs to be either deleted or rewritten to say what the actual divergence from standard active inference is — gauge-covariant transport across the multi-agent message-passing graph, or the fast-versus-slow timescale ratio under explicit gauge coupling, not the existence of model-parameter learning per se.

I also grant red's weaker reading of strike (i): the natural-gradient flow $\dot{\theta} = -F(\theta)^{-1}\nabla L$ in Amari (1998), *Neural Computation* 10(2) §2 is an intrinsic gradient flow on a Riemannian manifold whose metric is the Fisher information, not a Newtonian SDE in an overdamped limit; the Fisher matrix plays the role of a metric, not of a damping coefficient against thermal noise. The framing at line 3087 ("the overdamped reduction is not an oversight but the appropriate biophysical regime") and the equation at 3089–3091 are doing thermodynamic-analogy work, not deriving the natural-gradient flow from a damped SDE. The manuscript would be cleaner if it labelled the equation at 3089–3091 explicitly as a textbook stand-in illustrating the regime distinction, or if it switched to the Langevin formulation of Friston, Da Costa, Sajid, Heins, Ueltzhöffer, Pavliotis, Parr (2023), *Physics Reports* §3 ("Stochastic vs. deterministic dynamics"), where $\gamma$ has a defined referent as the friction coefficient against fluctuating noise — an object the present framework does not name. I do not concede red's stronger reading that the equation is incoherent: a textbook illustration of a regime distinction in a Discussion section does not need its symbols to bind to defined framework variables. But I grant that the calibration could be tighter.

## Defense

The load-bearing hedge at line 3095 — that the kinetic-term reintroduction is contingent on the fundamental-physics reading and inherits the postulate that the stiffness matrix $\Sigma_p^{-1}$ doubles as the inertia tensor, and is not a derivation of inertia from the variational principle — is the appropriate level of confidence for the construction the manuscript actually carries out, on the evidence of the external canon.

The reading of FEP as an action principle for which a kinetic-energy term is a natural extension is anchored in Friston, Sengupta, Auletta (2014), *Proceedings of the IEEE* 102(4), 427–445 §IV ("Cognitive dynamics"), where second-order extensions of free-energy dynamics through generalised motion are introduced as a way of putting free-energy minimisation into a form continuous with the variational principles of physics; in Ramstead, Friston, et al. (2018), *Physics of Life Reviews* 24, 1–16 §3, where the FEP is read explicitly as an action principle and the kinetic-term question is raised; and in Friston, Da Costa, Sajid, Heins, Ueltzhöffer, Pavliotis, Parr (2023), *Physics Reports* §2–§3, which separates the dissipative and conservative parts of the dynamics and gives both stochastic-Langevin and deterministic-Helmholtz decompositions. The "fundamental-physics reading" the hedge invokes is canon, not idiosyncratic invention, and the load-bearing clause at 3095 ("the variational principle as a candidate fundamental scaffolding rather than a phenomenological model of neural inference") sits inside that canon's framing.

Within that framing, the kinetic-energy reading the manuscript adopts requires a Riemannian metric on configuration space for the kinetic form $\tfrac12 \dot{q}^T g_{ij}(q) \dot{q}$ in the sense of Arnold (1989), *Mathematical Methods of Classical Mechanics*, 2nd ed., §22.2 (kinetic energy as a Riemannian metric on the configuration manifold) and §15.A (small-oscillation theorem $\omega^2 = K/m$ with the inertia tensor and the potential Hessian as independent $(0,2)$-tensors at the equilibrium configuration). The body of the manuscript at `Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:1849` cites Arnold 1989 explicitly and registers the non-independence of $M_{\mu\mu}$-as-stiffness and $M_{\mu\mu}$-as-inertia in the present construction; the body at 2013–2024 records the within-framework interpretation that "we reuse $M_i$... as both the stiffness... and the inertia... (kinetic-metric coefficient, postulated separately)"; the body at 2026–2034 labels the velocity-quadratic kinetic-metric form a "postulate, not a consequence of $\mathcal{F}$," and notes that under this postulate $\omega^2 \propto k/m$ reduces to a definitional consequence rather than an empirical scaling. The hedge at 3095 names exactly this postulate and exactly this non-derivation. That is the calibration the body-level disclosures demand.

The conservative-Hamiltonian regime in which this reading binds is the symmetric-attention or isolated-agent limit identified at `Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:1961`. In that regime the small-oscillation kinetic-energy reading of Arnold §22.2 / §15.A goes through; the manuscript's reuse of $M_{\mu\mu}$ in both roles is a postulate distinct from the variational principle, and it is registered as such. Outside that regime — asymmetric frozen $\beta_{ij}$ — the body of `sec:mass` at 1961 already records that the kinetic-energy reading fails, and the Discussion's "fundamental-physics reading" therefore does not apply. The Discussion's choice to point at the symmetric / conservative regime through the `\ref{sec:mass}` cross-reference is correct for the reading it advances.

The narrowed remediation that survives: edit the sentence at 3093 to fix the factual error about traditional approaches treating predictive models as fixed; rewrite the equation block at 3089–3091 either as a labelled textbook illustration or in Langevin form with $\gamma$ defined as a friction coefficient against fluctuating noise per Friston et al. 2023 §3; add a parenthetical cross-reference at 3095 to `\ref{sec:mass_block_caveats}` so a reader of the Discussion alone is alerted to the symmetric-attention regime restriction. None of these edits touch the load-bearing hedge sentence at 3095. That sentence is correctly calibrated against the canon and against the body of `sec:mass`.

## Falsification conditions

The defended position is wrong if any of the following hold:

If Arnold (1989) §22.2 / §15.A strictly forbids reusing the same matrix as both the kinetic-metric coefficient and the potential Hessian at the equilibrium configuration even in the canonical small-oscillation Lagrangian framework — i.e., if the operational-independence condition is logical rather than empirical — then the hedge at 3095 is insufficient and the construction needs a stronger qualifier than "postulate that $\Sigma_p^{-1}$ doubles as the inertia tensor"; it needs to say "construction that is degenerate in the small-oscillation theorem and is therefore not a small-oscillation theorem at all." On my reading of Arnold §22.2 the operational independence is empirical: the kinetic-energy form is any Riemannian metric on configuration space, and freely choosing that metric to equal the Hessian of the potential at equilibrium produces a degenerate but consistent harmonic limit in which $\omega^2$ reduces to a per-direction unit relation, exactly as `sec:velocity_quadratic` at 2026–2034 already records. If a closer reading of Arnold §22 contradicts that — produce the line — I concede the hedge is too weak.

If the canonical active-inference literature contains a primary source (Friston, Buckley, Parr, or co-authored) in which the natural-gradient flow $\dot{\mu} = -F^{-1}\nabla \mathcal{F}$ is derived as the $m \to 0$ overdamped limit of an inertial SDE on the same configuration space, then the equation at 3089–3091 is not merely a textbook illustration but the canonical derivation form, and my concession on (i) was too generous. On Amari (1998) §2 and Amari & Nagaoka (2000) §3.4 the natural-gradient flow is an intrinsic gradient flow on a statistical manifold, not an overdamped limit, and I have not been able to locate such a derivation in the active-inference canon.

If the body of `sec:mass` does not actually contain `sec:mass_block_caveats` as a labelled paragraph reachable through the `\ref{sec:mass}` cross-reference at 3095 — i.e., if the label has been removed or the paragraph has been moved out of the section — then the implicit-restriction reading on (iii) collapses and the Discussion's hedge becomes shorter than the body's hedge by a full paragraph of regime restriction, not by one cross-reference. The grep at `Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:1958` confirms the label is in place.

If a reading of the existing `\ref{sec:mass}` cross-reference at 3095 is shown to fail to reach `sec:mass_block_caveats` for a typical reader — e.g., the section is long enough that a casual reader stops before the off-diagonal-blocks paragraph — then strike (iii) lands and the parenthetical-cross-reference remediation is required, not optional. My defended position is the latter.
