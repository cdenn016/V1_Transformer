# Red Opening — pifb-discussion-kinetic-term

## Steelman (opposing position)

The Kinetic Term subsection correctly identifies a substantive structural divergence between standard active inference (first-order overdamped natural-gradient descent) and this framework's second-order velocity-quadratic Lagrangian, and the load-bearing hedge at line 3095 (the kinetic-term reintroduction "is contingent on the fundamental-physics reading and inherits the postulate that the stiffness matrix $\Sigma_p^{-1}$ doubles as the inertia tensor; it is not a derivation of inertia from the variational principle") names the postulate-status of the inertia identification at exactly the level the body of `sec:mass` already establishes, neither overclaiming a derivation that the body explicitly disclaims (1849, 2013–2024, 2026–2034) nor downgrading the second-variation structure to mere metaphor.

## Position

The subsection at `Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:3085–3095` is mis-calibrated in three structurally independent ways: (i) the overdamped-reduction equation at 3089–3091 uses a damping coefficient $\gamma$ that has no referent anywhere in the framework's defined variables, so the limit $m/\gamma \to 0$ is presentational and does not bind to the framework it claims to characterize; (ii) the assertion at 3093 that "in traditional approaches predictive models are considered fixed" is factually false against the canonical active-inference literature, which contains within-trial concentration-parameter updates and explicit slow-timescale model-parameter learning; (iii) the Discussion subsection invokes the "fundamental-physics reading" at 3095 without registering the asymmetric-attention regime limitation that the body of `sec:mass` itself records at 1958–1961, even though transformer attention is asymmetric by default in this framework. Each of (i), (ii), (iii) is a line-level calibration defect inside the subsection under debate, not a defensible interpretive reading.

## Evidence

**On (i) — the overdamped reduction has no referent.** The manuscript writes at 3089–3091:
```
m\ddot{q} + \gamma\dot{q} = -\nabla V  →_{m/\gamma → 0}  \gamma\dot{q} = -\nabla V.
```
Within this framework, $m$ is identified with $\Sigma_p^{-1}$ via the kinetic-metric postulate (`Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:2026–2034`, `sec:velocity_quadratic`: "we postulate the kinetic form by reusing the second-variation matrix as a Riemannian metric on velocities"; and 1849: "we take the kinetic form on belief velocities to be $\tfrac12 \dot{\mu}^\top M_{\mu\mu} \dot{\mu}$ with the same $M_{\mu\mu} = \Sigma_p^{-1}$"). A textual search of `Participatory_it_from_bit.tex` (lines 1–3095, ripgrep on `\\gamma|damping|friction`) returns no definition of $\gamma$ as a damping coefficient anywhere; every other occurrence of $\gamma$ in the body of the manuscript refers either to the meta-attention weight $\gamma_{ij}$ (e.g., 1233, 1239, 1255, 1259, 1360) or the hyperprior decay scalar of Section 2262 (`\gamma = 0.5`). The framework therefore has no $\gamma$ in the sense of 3090, and the equation at 3089–3091 is a generic textbook formula not anchored to the framework's variables. Worse, if $\gamma$ at 3090 is meant to be identified with the same precision $\Sigma_p^{-1}$ that already plays the role of $m$ in `sec:velocity_quadratic`, then $m/\gamma \equiv 1$ identically, and the claimed limit $m/\gamma \to 0$ is incoherent rather than a meaningful biophysical reduction. The line at 3087 explicitly upgrades this from metaphor to substantive identification: "the overdamped reduction is not an oversight but the appropriate biophysical regime for the systems active inference targets." That phrasing makes the Newtonian-with-friction framing a substantive characterization of active-inference dynamics, not an aside.

Citation against external canon: Amari's natural gradient is $\dot{\theta} = -F(\theta)^{-1} \nabla L$, where $F$ is the Fisher information matrix acting as a Riemannian metric on the statistical manifold [Amari1998 §2; `external_canon_math.md` §1 "Natural gradient"]. Amari himself does not derive this as the $m \to 0$ limit of an inertial SDE — it is a gradient flow on a Riemannian manifold whose metric is intrinsic to the statistical structure, not a damping coefficient added to a Newtonian system. The standard active-inference belief-update equation $\dot{\mu} = -\partial_\mu \mathcal{F}$ [Friston2010; `external_canon_inference.md` §1 "Variational free energy"] inherits this first-order gradient-flow status without invoking inertia. Stochastic-Langevin formulations of FEP [FristonDaCostaSajidEtAl2023] do introduce overdamped vs inertial Langevin distinctions, but those formulations cast $\gamma$ as a friction coefficient against thermal noise — an object the present framework does not define.

**On (ii) — "predictive models are considered fixed" is factually false against the canon.** The manuscript at 3093 states verbatim: "Furthermore, the framework allows agent priors to evolve dynamically.  In traditional approaches predictive models are considered fixed." This is wrong on the canonical record:

- Friston, K., FitzGerald, T., Rigoli, F., Schwartenbeck, P., Pezzulo, G. (2017), "Active inference: a process theory," *Neural Computation* 29(1), 1–49, contains explicit within-trial updates of the expected concentration parameters of the generative model (Dirichlet hyper-parameters of A, B, D matrices); the paper labels this "learning over trials" and treats it as part of the canonical active-inference loop. The model is not "considered fixed."
- Friston, K. (2010), "The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?", *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* 11(2), 127–138, includes a dedicated subsection on "Learning and Memory" in which slow-timescale plasticity updates the generative-model parameters via the same free-energy gradient that updates beliefs at fast timescale; this is the canonical E-step / M-step separation under variational EM [DempsterLairdRubin1977; `external_canon_inference.md` §5].
- Parr, T., Pezzulo, G., Friston, K. (2022), *Active Inference: The Free Energy Principle in Mind, Brain, and Behavior*, MIT Press, Ch. 3, gives the canonical timescale separation (perception → action → learning → structure learning) in which model parameters update on every timescale slower than belief inference.

The Discussion's claim therefore mischaracterizes the canon it is comparing against. This is not a hedge-calibration issue; it is a factual error embedded in a subsection that purports to identify what makes the present framework different from standard active inference. The actual difference — if any — is one of mechanism (gauge-covariant transport, multi-agent coupling) and timescale ratio, not of whether models evolve at all.

**On (iii) — the asymmetric-attention regime limitation is unregistered.** The body of `sec:mass` at 1958–1961 (`sec:mass_block_caveats`) states explicitly: "if attention weights $\beta_{ij}$ are treated as instantaneous and frozen-asymmetric ($\beta_{ik}\neq\beta_{ki}$) within the dynamics, the resulting flow is no longer the gradient flow of $\mathcal{F}$ alone, and the bilinear form $M_{\mu\mu}$ does not function as the inertia tensor of a conservative Hamiltonian. The reciprocal-attention conditions $\beta_{ik} = \beta_{ki}$ and $\Omega_{ik}\Omega_{ki} = I$ are sufficient for the dynamics to remain gradient-like and for the kinetic-energy reading to be Newtonian." The Discussion's "kinetic term" reading at 3095 inherits the postulate that $\Sigma_p^{-1}$ doubles as the inertia tensor (which is exactly the postulate that fails under asymmetric attention per 1958–1961). Standard transformer attention is asymmetric: the matrix $\beta_{ij}$ is row-stochastic but not symmetric in $(i,j)$ unless an explicit reciprocity constraint is imposed (which `Dennis2025trans` and this manuscript do not impose by default; see the discussion at 1842 and the off-diagonal mass blocks at 1944–1956 which carry separate $\beta_{ik}$ and $\beta_{ki}$ contributions precisely to handle the asymmetric case). A grep of the Discussion subsection (`rg "sec:mass_block_caveats|asymmetric" Participatory_it_from_bit.tex` lines 3085–3095) returns no occurrence. The subsection therefore advertises the "fundamental-physics reading" at exactly the regime where the body of the manuscript records that the reading collapses, and does not flag this. That is a calibration gap: the Discussion's hedge is shorter than the body's hedge.
