Two concessions on narrow derivability points. I dispute that these concessions defeat the integrated claim.

## Core attack

Red's load-bearing premise is a **semantic collapse of "well-motivated by the literature" into "derived from the literature."** This collapse breaks the integrated claim only if the claim asserts derivability, which it does not.

The claim under debate (`00_claim.md` line 11) asserts that the constructions are "**mathematically correct and well-motivated by the literature**" — a conjunction. The canon excerpt red cites against the manuscript (`external_canon_inference.md §1`, "What is not in this standard form") states the operative standard explicitly: multi-agent coupling terms with gauge-transport $\Omega_{ij}$ are "user-introduced ... a novel construction requiring its own justification." The canon does not say "novel constructions are not well-motivated by the literature" — it says they "require their own justification," which is a different requirement that the manuscript meets in three places: at line 546 ("structural commitment of the framework rather than a theorem of standard hierarchical variational inference"), at line 2100 (non-compact $\mathrm{GL}^+(K)$ caveat with explicit substitute construction), and at line 2137 ("RG-inspired rather than a literal RG analysis").

Red's own falsification clause for A1 anticipates this and concedes the ground: "Blue can also concede A1 and argue that the manuscript's labeling of the departure is itself the 'motivation' the claim asks for. I would dispute the semantics of 'well-motivated by' but the judge can adjudicate." This is a transparent admission that the semantic question is the actual issue, and that red is asking the judge to read "well-motivated by" as "derivable from."

Under the literal reading of the canon, "well-motivated by the literature" includes constructions that **extend** the literature coherently — that label themselves as novel, cite the standard form they depart from, and exhibit their own justification. The manuscript does this for all three sub-claim constructions: A (cross-scale shadow, with line 546 disclaiming the derivation and citing `[Friston2017, parr2022active]` as the standard form being departed from), B (Karcher-mean barycenter with line 2100 disclaiming the non-compact case and exhibiting the bi-invariant-compact substitute), and C (RG analogy with line 2137 disclaiming literal RG and citing `[Wilson1971, Cardy1996]` as the structural template). This is the field-standard form of "well-motivated by the literature" for a framework that extends rather than reproduces existing work — see `[BleiKuckelbirgJordan2017]` for analogous treatment of CAVI extensions to non-conjugate models, where the truncation of the natural-gradient correction is presented as a leading-order approximation rather than a theorem.

The canon citation red invokes against the manuscript (`external_canon_inference.md §10 item 5`, "point-passing scheme is 'not standard mean-field across the hierarchy'") establishes that the construction is **novel relative to standard hierarchical FEP** — which the manuscript already concedes at line 546 in identical language. Red has cited the canon to establish a property the manuscript has independently established about itself.

## Defense

**On sub-claim A** (red's strike A2 — Role A / Role B antecedents do not deploy a smooth $\mathfrak{g}$-valued bulk field doing both roles). The manuscript at line 565 reads: "Quantities like $\mathrm{tr}(A^{(i)}_\mu A^{(i)}_\nu)$ are not invariant under the full local gauge $g_i = g_i(c)$ that introduces a Maurer-Cartan derivative term, but are invariant under the constant-per-agent residual subgroup $\{(g_i)_{i \in I} : g_i \in G \text{ constant in } c\}$." Red reads this as a defect ("the dual role is well-defined only after the gauge group has been broken to a residual subgroup"). The literature reads this as a **feature** of edge-mode / quantum-reference-frame constructions, not a defect. `[DonnellyFreidel2016 §3]` explicitly works with the gauge group **reduced at the boundary** — the edge-mode degrees of freedom are precisely those obtained when the bulk gauge group is restricted to its boundary subgroup. The full bulk-plus-boundary local gauge is not preserved; what is preserved is the boundary residual subgroup acting at the codimension-one surface. The manuscript is doing the discrete-system analog: the constant-per-agent residual subgroup is the cognitive-frame analog of the boundary residual subgroup that survives at the edge in the continuum case. Red's premise that a "smooth $\mathfrak{g}$-valued bulk field doing both roles **under the full local gauge**" is what the antecedents license misreads `[DonnellyFreidel2016]` — the antecedents license dual-role frame fields under a **restricted residual gauge group**, which is exactly what the manuscript does and explicitly labels. The Bartlett-Rudolph-Spekkens quantum-reference-frame case (`[BartlettRudolphSpekkens2007 §III]`) is analogous: the reference frame is a physical system carrying state from its own perspective, and the gauge group acting on it is restricted to the superselection-respecting subgroup. The manuscript's residual-subgroup move is the discrete-cognitive analog of these constructions, not a departure from them.

**On sub-claim B** (red's strikes B1 — Karcher convex-normal-ball not verified — and B2 — $\mathrm{GL}^+(K)$ has no bi-invariant metric). The claim under debate concerns the **Theory section's mathematical coherence**, not whether the parent-frame construction operationally works on a non-compact group. The manuscript's empirical regime, stated at line 587, restricts to $G = \mathrm{SO}(3)$ which is compact and admits the bi-invariant metric $d_G(U,V)^2 = \|\log(U^{-1}V)\|_F^2$ (`Attention/Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:2100`). Under this metric, `[Karcher1977 Thm 1.2]` gives existence and uniqueness on convex normal balls of radius $< \pi/2$. The manuscript at line 2100 invokes this condition; at line 2131 it adds the BCH accuracy bound ("accurate to $\mathcal{O}(\|\phi_i\|^2)$ when constituent frames are close in the compact case"). The non-compact $\mathrm{GL}^+(K)$ caveat is registered at line 2100 with the substitute construction (left-invariant alternative or polar/Cartan decomposition) and the partial-gauge-symmetry-breaking flag. This is the **standard form of caveat** in Riemannian-mean machine-learning literature (`[doCarmo1992 Ch. 13]` for the general non-compact obstruction; `[BoumalAbsilCartis2019]` for the specific manifold-optimization treatment of $\mathrm{SO}(N)$ injectivity radius). The claim "mathematically coherent" is satisfied when (i) the compact-case construction is well-defined under stated conditions (true at line 2100 with the convex-normal-ball condition), and (ii) the non-compact obstruction is explicitly registered with the substitute construction (true at line 2100). The claim does not require the manuscript to solve the non-compact problem.

Red's B1 escalation — that meta-agents formed at the $\Gamma_{\min} = 0.5$ threshold may have post-transport pairwise frame separations exceeding the convex-normal-ball condition — is a genuine **empirical-regime question** about whether the simulations of `sec:meta_agent_emergence` always satisfy the Karcher condition. The manuscript does not verify this. But the claim under debate is about **the Theory section's mathematical correctness and well-motivation**, not about the simulator's empirical regime. The same parametric ambiguity exists in the standard treatments — `[Karcher1977]` does not specify when constituent frames will fall inside the normal ball in a given application; that is application-dependent. The manuscript's failure to verify the condition in a specific simulation regime is an empirical/implementation gap, not a Theory-section incorrectness.

**On sub-claim C** (red's strike C1 — "scale-invariant functional form" is a parametric template without $f_\beta, f_\gamma$). The manuscript at line 2137 reads: "the present construction is RG-inspired rather than a literal RG analysis: we do not exhibit a $\beta$-function, locate fixed points, or demonstrate scale invariance beyond the parametric form." Red reads this as an admission that the construction fails to be "well-motivated by `[Wilson1971]`/`[Cardy1996]`." The literature reads "RG-inspired" as a **recognized category**. `[MehtaSchwab2014]` ("An exact mapping between the Variational Renormalization Group and Deep Learning") explicitly motivates a deep-learning construction as RG-template-inspired before exhibiting the explicit recursion; that paper's section 2 names the parametric-form-preservation step as the structural commitment before the explicit recursion is constructed. The "scale-free dynamics in the brain" literature (e.g., `[Beggs2003]` for neuronal avalanches, the broad-band $1/f$ literature) uses RG-inspired arguments without exhibiting explicit $\beta$-functions in the original cortical setting. The manuscript's claim is not that it has executed a Wilson-Cardy RG calculation; it is that the **structural template** (coarse-grain-then-renormalize on coherent clusters) is well-motivated by `[Wilson1971, Cardy1996]` as a template, with the explicit recursion deferred to future work (line 2153, "investigation of RG fixed points and critical exponents ... constitutes an important direction for future work"). This is the field-standard form of "RG-inspired" in cross-disciplinary applications.

Red's strict-Wilson reading — "an RG scheme without an explicit recursion relation on couplings is not an RG scheme" — collapses "RG-inspired" into "RG calculation." That collapse is not the literature's reading of "well-motivated by" in interdisciplinary RG applications; it is red's reading. The manuscript honestly disclaims literal RG at line 2137; it correctly identifies what is and is not established.

**On the implementation barycenter** (`Attention/Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:2121-2125`, $\bar{\mu}_I, \bar{\Sigma}_I$ implementation formulae dropping the dispersion term). Red did not strike this directly, but the evidence pack flags it. The implementation drops the dispersion term as $\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon)$ in the high-coherence regime, with magnitude stated explicitly at line 2093 ("vanishes at strict consensus, when all $\tilde{\mu}_i$ coincide; in the high-coherence regime where meta-agents form, it is $\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon)$"). This is the standard leading-order approximation in variational mean-field methods — the analogous treatment of natural-gradient corrections in `[BleiKuckelbirgJordan2017 §3.3]` drops higher-order moment terms with the same justification. The manuscript at line 2127 explicitly labels the formula as "an implementation heuristic that agrees with the variational forward-KL barycenter at $\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon)$ when constituent post-transport means coincide" and identifies the dispersion-term reinstatement as "the natural refinement once the threshold detector is replaced by a continuous-time evaluation." This is honest labeling of a leading-order approximation, not a defect.

**On the threshold detector** (`Attention/Participatory_it_from_bit.tex:2114`). The manuscript explicitly states at line 2114 that "the relation between the detector and the variational criterion is heuristic and partial rather than a derivation." The claim under debate is not that the detector **implements** the variational criterion; it is that the meta-agent definition (which has three layers: the variational principle at Eq.~\ref{eq:meta_agent_FE_criterion}, the barycenter at Eq.~\ref{eq:meta_agent_barycenter}, and the detector at Eq.~\ref{eq:meta_agent_threshold}) is **well-motivated**. Each layer is well-motivated: the variational principle is the standard form `[Friston2010]`, the forward-KL Gaussian barycenter matches `[AmariNagaoka2000]` and `[Cuturi2014]` / `[Agueh2011]`, and the detector is acknowledged as a candidate-selection surrogate. The relationship between layers is correctly labeled at line 2114 as heuristic. Heuristic relationships between well-motivated layers do not break the well-motivation of each layer.

## Summary of position

I concede A1 (cross-scale shadow is not derived from standard hierarchical FEP) and C2 (Ouroboros exponential decay is not derived from a standard hierarchical-Bayesian object), on the narrow point of derivability. I dispute that these concessions defeat the integrated claim because the claim is "mathematically correct and well-motivated by the literature," and the manuscript honestly labels both constructions as structural commitments that extend rather than reproduce the standard schemes — which is the field-standard form of "well-motivated by" for an extension.

I defend A2 by noting that the residual-subgroup gauge structure the manuscript invokes at line 565 is the discrete-cognitive analog of the edge-mode-boundary-subgroup construction of `[DonnellyFreidel2016]`, not a departure from it.

I defend B1 and B2 by noting that the claim under debate concerns the Theory section's mathematical coherence, not the non-compact operational regime or the empirical-simulation Karcher-ball verification. The manuscript registers both caveats explicitly at line 2100.

I defend C1 by noting that "RG-inspired" is a recognized category in cross-disciplinary RG applications (`[MehtaSchwab2014]`, the scale-free brain dynamics literature), and the manuscript explicitly disclaims literal RG at line 2137.

The integrated claim — agent definition well-motivated, meta-agent definition mathematically coherent with implementation gap acknowledged, hierarchical emergence well-motivated as RG-inspired analogy — survives under the literature-standard reading of "well-motivated by the literature." If the judge reads that phrase as red asks them to (as "derivable from the literature"), then the claim fails on A1 and C2. The semantic question is the actual decision the judge must make.
