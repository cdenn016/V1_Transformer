# Blue Opening — subclaim-A-flat-bundle

## Steelman (opposing position)

The flat-bundle / constant-gauge limit `Ω_ij = Ω` (and a fortiori `Ω = I`) is a *gauge fixing* in the technical sense of gauge theory: it selects a single orbit representative under the GL(K) action. A gauge-fixed Lagrangian is, by standard practice in classical and quantum gauge theory ([Nakahara2003 §10.4], [Frankel2011 Ch. 18]), no longer manifestly gauge-equivariant under the gauge group it was fixed in. The resulting object is just ordinary scaled dot-product attention, and any "GL(K) gauge structure" attached to it after the fact is a post-hoc identification with no operational content beyond what the standard transformer already has.

## Position

The flat-bundle limit `Ω_ij = Ω ∈ GL(K)` (constant across pairs) — and the further reduction to `Ω = I` with `Ω^{-T}` absorbed into a learned bilinear `W_Q W_K^T` — is a mathematically well-defined *specialization* of the GL(K) framework that does not destroy the upstream gauge-equivariance theorem (Theorem `thm:glk_invariance`, `Attention/GL(K)_attention.tex:520–556`). The theorem is universally quantified over `Ω ∈ GL(K)`; instantiating it at one particular `Ω` is the textbook meaning of "a special case of the theorem," not a refutation of it.

The red position relies on a slide between two distinct claims:

(i) **Strong reading.** "The gauge-fixed object is itself manifestly equivariant under the residual GL(K) action." This is false in standard gauge theory, and I do not defend it.

(ii) **Weak reading.** "The upstream framework, before any specialization, is gauge-equivariant; specialization to a particular `Ω` instantiates this framework at one point and does not retroactively invalidate the theorem." This is the load-bearing reading, and it is mathematically trivially true.

The manuscript's claim (Section 5.7 summary at `Attention/GL(K)_attention.tex:1974`) is the weak reading: the W matrices in the standard transformer are *identified with* gauge transformations of the embedded representations, exhibiting standard attention as one point in the GL(K)-parameterized family rather than as a gauge-broken residue. The upstream theorem at `\ref{thm:glk_invariance}` certifies that *every* such point — including the trivial gauge — satisfies the invariance equation `D_KL(Ω_* P ‖ Ω_* Q) = D_KL(P ‖ Q)`.

## Evidence

**Theorem `thm:glk_invariance`, `Attention/GL(K)_attention.tex:520–556`.** The theorem statement is universally quantified: "*For any two Gaussian distributions P, Q on `R^K`, and any invertible linear transformation `Ω ∈ GL(K)`, the KL divergence is invariant under simultaneous push-forward*." The proof (lines 531–556) carries through for `Ω = I` (trivially: `Ω_* P = P` and the equation becomes `D_KL(P ‖ Q) = D_KL(P ‖ Q)`) and for any constant `Ω` (the trace, quadratic, and log-determinant terms each cancel by the same algebraic identities used in the proof — the `(det Ω)^2` factor cancels regardless of whether `Ω = I` or `Ω` is a non-trivial constant). The theorem makes *no assumption* that `Ω` vary across pairs. The flat-bundle limit is therefore not outside the theorem's domain of applicability; it is one element of that domain.

**Constant-gauge specialization, `Attention/GL(K)_attention.tex:1117–1119`.** The manuscript states: "Standard transformer attention emerges when we further assume a single global frame shared by all agents, `Ω_{ij} = Ω` for all `i,j`, corresponding to a flat connection with no position-dependent frame misalignment. The geometric bias `S(Ω)` becomes constant across all agent pairs and cancels under softmax, while `Ω^{-1}` is absorbed into learned projections." The operations described — "becomes constant," "cancels under softmax," "absorbed into learned projections" — are all algebraically well-defined for any constant `Ω ∈ GL(K)`. There is no singular limit, no divergent factor, and no discontinuity at `Ω = I` (the `S(Ω) = log det Ω` term is `0` at `Ω = I` and finite at any other constant `Ω`).

**Trivial-frame limit, `Attention/GL(K)_attention.tex:1160`.** Explicit limit statement: "*The trivial-frame specialisation `U_i = U` for all `i` removes the per-token positional variation; in that limit `Ω_{ij} = I` and the analysis of Section [dot-product-derivation] applies, with the learned bilinear `M` replacing the structural role played by per-token frame variation.*" The substitution `U_i = U` is a *choice of section*, which in bundle language is exactly the operation of specializing to a fixed gauge. Section 5.2 (lines 1162–1196) then carries out the derivation step by step; nothing breaks. The derivation is a mathematical proof, not a conjecture about a limit.

**Bundle-triviality admission, `Attention/GL(K)_supplementary.tex:53`.** The supplementary explicitly states: "*Throughout the main paper we work with the vertex-frame parameterization `Ω_{ij} = exp(φ_i) exp(-φ_j)`, which encodes a globally trivial principal G-bundle.*" The framework operates inside the flat-bundle subclass by *construction*. The constant-gauge specialization `Ω_ij = Ω` is therefore not an exotic boundary case; it is a degeneration *within* a subclass the framework already inhabits. Setting `φ_i = φ` for all `i` (constant gauge field) is a smooth specialization of the φ-parameterization, not a singular limit.

**§5.7 summary identification, `Attention/GL(K)_attention.tex:1974`.** "*The GL(K) gauge invariance theorem (Theorem `glk_invariance`) is essential as it guarantees that the learned projections `W_Q, W_K` are valid gauge transformations, explaining why transformers can learn arbitrary (invertible) attention patterns.*" The standard transformer's `W_Q, W_K` are reidentified as gauge transformations on the embedded representations. The standard transformer is then exhibited as a *particular point* in the GL(K)-parameterized family — the point at which the per-pair Ω structure collapses to a single learned `W_Q W_K^T = σ^{-2} Ω^{-T}`. This is the standard mathematical move: a specific element of a parameterized family is one realization of the family, not a counter-example to the family's existence.

**Canonical reading of "specialization" from `external_canon_math.md` §2.** The canon distinguishes "gauge invariant" from "gauge equivariant" and notes (lines 116–120): "Predictions/observables are typically gauge invariant. Internal representations are typically gauge equivariant. Conflating these is a common error." The relevant invariance for Theorem `thm:glk_invariance` is *invariance of the KL functional* under simultaneous push-forward, which is a property of the functional, not of any particular configuration. Plugging in `Ω = I` evaluates the functional at the trivial group element; the functional itself retains its invariance property at every other point in the orbit. The "loss of equivariance" red invokes from gauge-fixed Lagrangians ([Nakahara2003 §10.4]) is a statement about the *fixed object*, not about the original functional whose theorem we are citing.

**Standard analogy from differential geometry.** The Levi-Civita theorem asserts that every Riemannian metric admits a unique torsion-free metric-compatible connection. Specializing to the Euclidean metric on `R^n` instantiates this theorem at one particular metric. The Euclidean case is not a refutation of the theorem; it is an instance of it. Likewise: specializing `Ω` to a constant element of GL(K) instantiates Theorem `thm:glk_invariance` at one orbit point; the theorem still holds, and the standard transformer is one instance of the family the theorem parameterizes.

## Falsification conditions

I concede the position and red wins if any of the following holds:

1. **The limit `Ω_ij → const` is singular or divergent.** If passing from per-pair `Ω_ij = exp(φ_i) exp(-φ_j)` to constant `Ω_ij = Ω` involves a divergent factor, a discontinuity, or a non-removable singularity at any step of the derivation in `Attention/GL(K)_attention.tex` §5.2 (lines 1162–1342), the limit is not well-defined. (The derivation in the manuscript shows no such singularity: the only step that requires a non-zero quantity is `det Ω ≠ 0`, which is satisfied for any `Ω ∈ GL(K)` including `Ω = I`.)

2. **Theorem `thm:glk_invariance` requires `Ω` to vary across pairs.** If the proof at lines 531–556 fails when `Ω` is constant — for instance, if the trace, quadratic, or log-determinant cancellations require pair-dependent `Ω_ij` — then the theorem does not apply to the flat-bundle limit, and red wins. (Inspection of the proof shows the opposite: each cancellation uses only `Ω^{-1} Ω = I` and `det(Ω^T) = det(Ω)`, both of which hold for *any* single `Ω ∈ GL(K)` including the identity.)

3. **The claim being defended is the strong reading.** If the manuscript's claim is "the standard transformer, in isolation, is manifestly equivariant under the residual GL(K) action acting on its `W_Q, W_K` matrices," then red is right by standard gauge theory: a gauge-fixed object is not gauge-equivariant on its own. The manuscript's text at line 1974 says only that the W matrices "*are valid gauge transformations*," which is the weak reading (they live in the gauge group). If the manuscript is interpreted as making the strong reading, I cannot defend it.

4. **The downstream "framework" applies the equivariance theorem to gauge-fixed objects as if they were unfixed.** If any downstream derivation cites Theorem `thm:glk_invariance` to claim that the standard transformer's attention output transforms covariantly under a residual GL(K) action acting on the post-W_Q-W_K-fixed representation, that derivation is wrong, and the flat-bundle limit has been misused. (Red would need to point to a specific manuscript line where this fallacy is committed; the §5.7 summary at line 1974 does not commit it — it cites the theorem to justify the W matrices' algebraic role as elements of GL(K), not to assert post-hoc equivariance of the standard transformer.)

5. **The framework's empirical content reduces entirely to standard transformer behavior at the flat-bundle limit.** This is a different claim than the one under debate (it is the parent compound claim, sub-claim B), but I flag it: if the flat-bundle limit has no empirical content beyond standard attention — i.e., the gauge-theoretic generalization away from `Ω = const` makes no measurable difference — then the "GL(K) structure" is empirically vacuous *at the limit* even if it is mathematically well-defined. This does not falsify the *current* sub-claim (which is about whether the limit is well-defined and preserves the upstream theorem), but it would make the sub-claim less interesting. The current sub-claim is about mathematical well-posedness, not about empirical payoff.
