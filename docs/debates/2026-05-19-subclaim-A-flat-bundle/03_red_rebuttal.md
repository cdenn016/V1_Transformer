# Red Rebuttal — subclaim-A-flat-bundle

## Concession

I grant blue's mathematical claim and accept its central frame:

1. **Theorem `thm:glk_invariance` is correct as stated.** The proof at `Attention/GL(K)_attention.tex:531–556` is valid for *any* `Ω ∈ GL(K)`, including the identity. The `(det Ω)^2` cancellation at `:552`, the `Ω^{-T} Σ_Q^{-1} Ω^{-1} · Ω Σ_P Ω^T` trace cancellation at `:541`, and the `Ω^T Ω^{-T} = I` cancellation in the quadratic term at `:547` all go through under constant Ω. The proof never uses pair-dependence. Blue's falsification condition (2) is settled in blue's favor.

2. **The flat-bundle specialization is not singular.** The map `Ω_ij = exp(φ_i) exp(-φ_j) → Ω` is smooth — set `φ_i = φ_0` for all `i` and `Ω_{ij} = I` falls out. No divergent factor. Blue's falsification condition (1) is settled in blue's favor.

3. **The "strong reading" is indefensible and blue does not defend it.** I do not press the strawman that a gauge-fixed object becomes manifestly equivariant under the residual GL(K) action. Blue states this explicitly in its falsification condition (3).

Blue therefore wins on mathematical well-posedness. What remains is whether the **weak reading** carries the operational content the manuscript repeatedly assigns to it.

## Core attack

Blue's weak reading (`02_blue_opening.md:15`) says: "the upstream framework, before any specialization, is gauge-equivariant; specialization to a particular `Ω` instantiates this framework at one point and does not retroactively invalidate the theorem." Granted. But blue then writes (`02_blue_opening.md:17`): "the W matrices in the standard transformer are *identified with* gauge transformations of the embedded representations, exhibiting standard attention as one point in the GL(K)-parameterized family." This is the load-bearing move, and the manuscript citation blue uses to anchor it is `Attention/GL(K)_attention.tex:1978`. That line **does not say what blue's defense requires it to say** — and once read precisely, the manuscript itself commits the very fallacy blue's falsification condition (4) names as fatal.

The text at `Attention/GL(K)_attention.tex:1978` reads:

> "The GL(K) gauge invariance theorem (Theorem `glk_invariance`) is essential as it guarantees that the learned projections W_Q, W_K are valid gauge transformations, explaining why transformers can learn arbitrary (invertible) attention patterns."

Decompose this sentence. Theorem `thm:glk_invariance` at `:520–528` quantifies over **a single Ω applied simultaneously to both distributions P, Q**: `D_KL(Ω_* P || Ω_* Q) = D_KL(P || Q)`. The theorem says nothing about a *pair* `(W_Q, W_K)` of *independent* invertible matrices. In standard transformer attention, `W_Q ≠ W_K` generically; they are independently learned. The composite `W_Q W_K^T` is one element of `GL(d_k)`, but the *action* of `W_Q` on queries and `W_K` on keys is **not the diagonal action** Theorem `thm:glk_invariance` requires for its invariance result.

In gauge-theory vocabulary: the residual symmetry of the gauge-fixed object (constant gauge) is the **diagonal subgroup** `{(Ω, Ω) : Ω ∈ GL(K)}` acting simultaneously on both indices. The standard transformer's freedom to pick `W_Q ≠ W_K` is *off* this diagonal — it is freedom in the *parameter space* of the gauge-fixed action, not residual gauge equivariance of the fixed object. Yet `:1978` cites Theorem `thm:glk_invariance` to justify `W_Q, W_K` being "valid gauge transformations" — plural, independent — and uses this to explain "why transformers can learn arbitrary (invertible) attention patterns." This is exactly the misuse blue lists as fatal in falsification condition (4): "citing Theorem `thm:glk_invariance` to claim that the standard transformer's attention output transforms covariantly under a residual GL(K) action."

Read the failure more sharply. Theorem `thm:glk_invariance` certifies that the **KL functional** is invariant under one Ω acting on both arguments. It does **not** certify that an arbitrary `Ω_Q` acting on queries and an independent `Ω_K` acting on keys leaves the functional invariant. In fact the opposite is true: `D_KL(Ω_Q,* P || Ω_K,* Q) ≠ D_KL(P || Q)` for `Ω_Q ≠ Ω_K`. The standard transformer's *expressive power* — its ability to learn arbitrary attention patterns via independent `W_Q, W_K` — comes from precisely the off-diagonal directions that **break** Theorem `thm:glk_invariance`'s symmetry, not from instantiating it. The theorem is cited as the source of an expressive capacity it explicitly does not endow.

This is not a rhetorical slip in one sentence. The §5.7 summary at `:1962` calls the post-specialization object "variational natural-gradient descent on the **gauge-equivariant** free energy" — *after* the three limits (isotropic, constant gauge, `W_Q W_K^T = σ^{-2} Ω^{-T}`) have been applied. Blue's weak reading concedes that *the post-limit object is not gauge-equivariant in any operational sense*, only that the *pre-limit functional* was. The manuscript at `:1962` says the opposite: it asserts gauge-equivariance of the *post-limit* training dynamics. The phrase "gauge-equivariant" at `:1962` is doing operational work — it is describing the post-specialization training update, not the pre-specialization functional.

The §5 framing at `:992` and `:994` ("standard transformer self-attention **emerges** as a set of limiting cases of our gauge-theoretic framework. This establishes neural network training as a limit of a first-principles information-geometric minimization of a variational free energy over a bundle geometry") describes the gauge structure as something *inherited* by the standard transformer through the reduction. Under blue's weak reading, no such inheritance occurs: the standard transformer is one orbit representative, the original equivariance is a property of the pre-specialization functional only, and the standard transformer in isolation has no gauge structure of its own. Blue's weak reading and the manuscript's framing are mutually inconsistent.

## Defense

Blue's strongest move is the Levi-Civita analogy (`02_blue_opening.md:33`): "Specializing to the Euclidean metric on `R^n` instantiates this theorem at one particular metric. The Euclidean case is not a refutation of the theorem; it is an instance of it." Granted as a formal point. But the analogy collapses under the question I am pressing.

The Levi-Civita theorem makes a universal claim about Riemannian metrics; Euclidean is one instance; nobody then asserts that "the Euclidean metric inherits the property of admitting a Levi-Civita connection" as a substantive feature distinguishing it from other geometries — the inheritance is trivial because *all* metrics satisfy the theorem. That is the structure of blue's weak reading. If blue is consistent, then the manuscript's repeated invocation of `gauge-equivariant` language at `:1962` and `:1978` to describe the standard transformer's training dynamics is **as substantive as saying "Euclidean space admits a Levi-Civita connection"** — formally true, operationally vacuous, and not a property that distinguishes the standard transformer from any other algebraic object the framework parameterizes.

I press the operational question blue's weak reading concedes: under specialization, *does the framework predict any behavior of the standard transformer that an unframed standard transformer does not predict?* Blue's defense does not answer this, and the canon citation it uses (`external_canon_math.md` §2 on gauge invariance vs equivariance) cuts against blue: "Predictions/observables are typically gauge invariant. Internal representations are typically gauge equivariant." The standard transformer's attention weights `α_ij` are the predictions; they are not invariant under any nontrivial action of `GL(d_k)` on the post-specialization `W_Q, W_K, μ_i` triple (the action would have to be diagonal on `(W_Q, W_K)`, which destroys the off-diagonal expressive power blue claims for them). The standard transformer therefore has no gauge invariance under blue's weak reading, and the framework's invocation of "gauge-equivariant" as a property of the post-limit dynamics at `:1962` is unsupported.

My defense, restated: I concede blue wins on **mathematical well-posedness** (the specialization is well-defined; the upstream theorem is intact). I maintain that under the **only reading blue is willing to defend**, the manuscript's downstream operational use of "gauge-equivariant" and "gauge invariance" at `:992`, `:994`, `:1962`, and `:1978` is empty — it asserts a property the gauge-fixed object does not have, and the citation chain at `:1978` (Theorem `thm:glk_invariance` ⇒ `W_Q, W_K` are "valid gauge transformations" with independent values) commits the residual-equivariance fallacy blue identifies as fatal.

The judge should rule on which of these is the actual subject of subclaim A: (a) the mathematical specialization is well-defined (blue wins), or (b) the manuscript's downstream operational use of gauge-theoretic vocabulary is consistent with the weak reading (red wins). The claim text at `00_claim.md:11` says the specialization "**does not destroy** the upstream gauge-equivariance arguments **used in the manuscript**" — the qualifier "used in the manuscript" makes the operational reading the load-bearing one. The manuscript's actual usage is inconsistent with the weak reading blue defends.
