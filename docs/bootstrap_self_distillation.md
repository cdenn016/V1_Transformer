# Bootstrap Self-Distillation for the Gauge-VFE E-Step

**Date:** 2026-04-06, Section 11 revised after reviewer correction
**Related:** `session_2026_04_06_active_inference_and_efe.md`, `_bootstrap_distill_verify.py` and `_section11_curvature_verify.py` (SymPy proofs)

## Abstract

The active-inference extensions recently added to the gauge-VFE transformer introduce two terms to the E-step free energy: a pragmatic term that minimizes the entropy of the readout distribution, and an epistemic term that maximizes the BALD-style mutual information between belief samples and the predictive distribution. The pragmatic term is self-reinforcing on its own and requires the epistemic term as a counterweight; both operate purely on the position-local distribution and do not use the sequence structure. This document proposes a third term, **bootstrap self-distillation**, in which each position's belief update is regularized to match the gauge-transported predictive distribution of its attention-weighted neighbours, with the target detached by a stop-gradient. The term is structurally a cross-entropy against a stop-gradient target, formally identical to the objectives used in BYOL and DINO, but with the view-augmentation role played by the parallel transport operator $\Omega_{ij}$. The term occupies a genuinely empty slot in the E-step coupling landscape: it couples positions in predictive-distribution space rather than belief space, and it has a principled interpretation as generalized belief propagation projected into observation space. A stop-gradient on the attention weights is required to prevent an "attend-to-twins" collapse mode distinct from the entropy-collapse mode of the pragmatic term. Gradient, fixed-point, and gauge-equivariance claims are verified symbolically via SymPy and collected in Appendix A. A separate curvature calculation in Appendix B corrects an earlier version of this document which incorrectly claimed that the distillation gradient is larger than the entropy gradient at random initialization; both gradients have the same leading-order magnitude $O(\sigma_\ell / \sqrt{V})$ in the $\ell_2$ norm, and the real distinction between the two terms lies in their fixed-point structure and in the cross-position coherence of the gradient, not in a magnitude advantage at initialization.

## 1. Motivation

The session that introduced active inference into the E-step established two facts that together motivate this proposal. First, the pragmatic self-observation term $\lambda_{\text{prag}} \cdot H[p_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \mu_i)]$ is self-reinforcing: on its own it drives the belief toward whatever point-distribution is currently favored by the readout, a feedback loop that the epistemic counterterm was introduced specifically to break. Second, at fresh initialization with a 50K-vocabulary softmax, the entropy gradient is small because the softmax sits near its maximum-entropy extremum where the Fisher-information curvature is $O(1/V)$, making the descent direction both pointwise small and poorly conditioned. Diagnostics measured a max-logit difference of approximately $10^{-5}$ between EFE on and EFE off at random initialization, growing to order unity only after 50–200 training steps had broken the near-uniformity of the readout.

A satisfying resolution would be a term with three properties. It should not require a counterweight to avoid collapse, so that a single term can be introduced without introducing a second. It should couple positions in a way that the existing KL attention coupling does not, so that it contributes genuinely new information rather than a reformulation of a term already present. And it should have a data-dependent fixed point rather than a data-independent one, so that as training proceeds and the readout develops structure the term drives the belief toward a meaningful target instead of collapsing onto the nearest simplex vertex. All three requirements are satisfied by the bootstrap self-distillation variant analyzed below, provided a subtle second stop-gradient is applied to the attention weights themselves and not only to the target distribution. Note that the distillation term does *not* escape the near-uniform flat-extremum regime at initialization: the corrected analysis in Section 11 shows that its gradient is also $O(\sigma_\ell/\sqrt{V})$ at random init, the same order as the entropy gradient. The advantage of the distillation term lies in its data-dependent fixed-point structure, which only begins to dominate once the readout has developed some structure through training.

## 2. The E-step coupling landscape

The variational free energy currently minimized in the E-step is, schematically,

$$F[q] \;=\; \underbrace{\alpha \sum_i \mathrm{KL}(q_i \| p_i)}_{\text{self-coupling}} \;+\; \underbrace{\lambda_{\text{belief}} \sum_{ij} \beta_{ij}\, \mathrm{KL}(q_i \| \Omega_{ij} q_j)}_{\text{neighbour coupling in Q-space}} \;+\; \underbrace{\lambda_{\text{prag}}\, H[p_{\text{pred}}(v|\mu_i)] - \lambda_{\text{epi}}\, \mathrm{MI}(v; \mu \mid q_i)}_{\text{active-inference extensions}}$$

with the attention weights themselves given by the Boltzmann policy

$$\beta_{ij} \;=\; \mathrm{softmax}_j\!\left(-\frac{\mathrm{KL}(q_i \| \Omega_{ij} q_j)}{\kappa}\right).$$

The existing couplings fall into two classes. The self-coupling and the neighbour KL coupling both operate in the *belief fiber* — they measure discrepancies between $(\mu, \Sigma)$ triples via Gaussian KL divergences, and the transport operator $\Omega_{ij}$ moves beliefs between fibers so that the comparison is well-defined. The pragmatic and epistemic terms operate entirely on the *local* readout distribution at a single position, using the PriorBank to map the local belief into a probability distribution over the vocabulary and then penalizing either its entropy or its mutual information with belief samples.

Neither class couples positions *in prediction space*. The belief-fiber couplings act on a $K$-dimensional representation that is related to the vocabulary readout only through a many-to-one map: two beliefs that are close in $Q$-space can produce predictive distributions that differ substantially near a decision boundary, and conversely two beliefs that are far in $Q$-space can produce predictive distributions that are nearly identical if their difference lies in the null space of the PriorBank projection. There is therefore an identifiable gap in the coupling landscape, corresponding to constraints of the form "position $i$'s *prediction* should agree with its neighbours' (gauge-transported) *predictions*" that the current terms neither impose nor imply.

## 3. Formal proposal

Let $p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \mu) = \mathrm{softmax}(-\mathrm{KL}(q \| \pi_v)/\tau)$ denote the PriorBank readout at iteration $t$, and let $\Omega_{ij}$ denote the parallel transport from position $j$ to position $i$ in the current gauge frame. The bootstrap self-distillation loss at position $i$ is

$$\boxed{\;L_{\text{distill},i} \;=\; \sum_j \mathrm{sg}[\beta_{ij}] \cdot \mathrm{CE}\!\left(\mathrm{sg}\!\left[p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \Omega_{ij}\mu_j)\right], \; p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \mu_i)\right)\;}$$

where $\mathrm{CE}(p, q) = -\sum_v p_v \log q_v$ is the standard cross-entropy, and $\mathrm{sg}[\cdot]$ is the stop-gradient operator that prevents gradients from flowing through its argument. Two stop-gradients are present in the definition, and both are essential. The first severs the gradient through the target distribution, which is the structural analogue of the target-network detachment in BYOL and DINO and is what prevents the trivial collapse in which both sides of the cross-entropy shrink toward the same degenerate point. The second severs the gradient through the attention weight $\beta_{ij}$, which addresses a distinct collapse mode — the *attend-to-twins* collapse — that is derived explicitly in Section 6 below. Without the second stop-gradient the loss admits a trivial descent direction in which position $i$ concentrates all of its attention on whichever neighbours already happen to agree with it, driving the loss to zero without conveying any useful information.

An equivalent aggregated form, which is substantially cheaper to compute at the cost of replacing the per-pair distillation with a consensus-to-consensus distillation, uses the attention-aggregated transported belief $\tilde\mu_i = \sum_j \beta_{ij}\, \Omega_{ij}\mu_j$ already computed by the attention sublayer as the target site:

$$L^{\text{agg}}_{\text{distill},i} \;=\; \mathrm{CE}\!\left(\mathrm{sg}\!\left[p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \tilde\mu_i)\right],\; p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \mu_i)\right).$$

This form costs a single readout per position per iteration, matching the cost of the existing pragmatic term, and its semantics ("match the consensus of your transported neighbours") is slightly different from the per-pair form ("match each of your transported neighbours, weighted") but is arguably more faithful to the belief propagation interpretation developed in Section 8.

## 4. Gradient analysis

The gradient of the distillation loss with respect to position $i$'s logits follows from a standard calculation which SymPy verifies component by component. Let $\mathbf{z}_i$ denote the pre-softmax logits of the readout at position $i$, so that $p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \mu_i) = \mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{z}_i)_v$. For a single neighbour $j$ with target $\mathbf{t}_j \equiv \mathrm{sg}[p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \Omega_{ij}\mu_j)]$, the cross-entropy gradient is

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial z_{i,k}}\!\left[-\sum_v t_{j,v}\, \log p^{(t)}_{\text{pred},v}(v \mid \mu_i)\right] \;=\; p^{(t)}_{\text{pred},k}(v \mid \mu_i) \;-\; t_{j,k}.$$

The derivation uses only the chain rule and the softmax identity $\partial \log \mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{z})_{v'}/\partial z_{v} = \delta_{vv'} - \mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{z})_v$, combined with the constraint $\sum_v t_{j,v} = 1$. A direct SymPy computation (Appendix A, Claim 1) confirms this identity for all three components of a three-class softmax, with a specific numerical check at the agreement fixed point $p = t = (1/4, 1/4, 1/2)$ returning exactly zero in every component.

Summing over neighbours with attention weights held fixed (stop-gradient on $\beta$), the full gradient is

$$\frac{\partial L_{\text{distill},i}}{\partial z_{i,k}} \;=\; \sum_j \mathrm{sg}[\beta_{ij}] \left( p^{(t)}_{\text{pred},k}(v \mid \mu_i) \;-\; p^{(t)}_{\text{pred},k}(v \mid \Omega_{ij}\mu_j) \right)$$

$$= p^{(t)}_{\text{pred},k}(v \mid \mu_i) \;-\; \sum_j \mathrm{sg}[\beta_{ij}] \cdot p^{(t)}_{\text{pred},k}(v \mid \Omega_{ij}\mu_j).$$

The right-hand side is structurally identical to the gradient of a cross-entropy loss against a *weighted average of stop-gradient targets*, which is the standard form for a multi-teacher distillation objective. The fixed point is reached exactly when the position-$i$ predictive distribution equals the attention-weighted consensus of the (transported) neighbour predictive distributions, and the residual at any point is a convex combination of the pointwise mismatches.

The contrast with the pragmatic term's gradient is mathematically sharp. For the entropy $H[p] = -\sum_v p_v \log p_v$, SymPy confirms that the gradient with respect to the logits is

$$\frac{\partial H[p]}{\partial z_k} \;=\; -p_k \cdot (\log p_k + H[p]),$$

which has the unusual property of vanishing at *both* the uniform distribution ($p_k = 1/V$, so $\log p_k + H = -\log V + \log V = 0$) and in the limit of any point distribution (where either $p_k \to 0$ killing the prefactor, or $p_k \to 1$ with $\log p_k \to 0$ and $H \to 0$). The entropy has two classes of critical points separated by a manifold of non-degenerate gradients, and minimizing it is a non-convex descent toward the nearest point-distribution vertex of the simplex. The distillation loss has one class of critical points, determined by the data-dependent targets $\{p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \Omega_{ij}\mu_j)\}_j$, and convergence to that single consensus point is not tied to sharpness but to agreement.

The distinction is *qualitative* — the distillation loss has a single data-dependent fixed point while the entropy has two data-independent critical manifolds — but not a pointwise magnitude advantage at random initialization. An earlier draft of this document claimed that the distillation gradient remains "order unity" at random init while the entropy gradient vanishes, and that claim is wrong. At initialization both the local prediction $p_i = 1/V + \varepsilon_i$ and the transported neighbour prediction $\tilde p_i = 1/V + \tilde\varepsilon_i$ are near-uniform; their difference is $\varepsilon_i - \tilde\varepsilon_i$ which has the same $O(\sigma_\ell/V)$ per-component scale as $\varepsilon_i$ alone, and the same $O(\sigma_\ell/\sqrt V)$ $\ell_2$ norm as the entropy gradient $-\varepsilon_i$ at that point. Appendix B provides the full curvature and gradient calculation, symbolically verified in `_section11_curvature_verify.py`. The SymPy verification script `_bootstrap_distill_verify.py` still reports a gradient of exactly zero at the agreement fixed point and a numerical example with `CE = (0, 1, 1)` producing nontrivial gradient components $(-2/9, 1/9, 1/9)$ at the uniform state, which remain correct — those are point-wise facts about the gradient formula, not claims about its magnitude relative to the entropy gradient.

## 5. Fixed-point analysis

The stationary condition for the distillation loss at position $i$, with the attention weights fixed by stop-gradient, is

$$p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \mu_i) \;=\; \sum_j \beta_{ij}\cdot p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \Omega_{ij}\mu_j) \qquad \forall v.$$

This is a consensus condition in predictive-distribution space: the prediction at each site must equal the attention-weighted average of the transported neighbour predictions. If the attention weights themselves satisfy a consistency condition with the belief state (which they do, through the Boltzmann softmax of the KL attention), then this fixed point is jointly defined by the data and the gauge structure of the sequence.

The consensus condition admits a degenerate attractor — the *uniform collapse* — in which every position converges to the same near-unigram predictive distribution. At that fixed point $p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \mu_i) = p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \Omega_{ij}\mu_j)$ holds trivially for all $i$ and $j$ because both sides equal the common distribution, the distillation gradient is identically zero, and no information about the data has been learned. This is the DINO collapse: the same failure mode that BYOL, SimSiam, and DINO all had to engineer additional mechanisms to prevent — target-network EMA, predictor asymmetry, centering-plus-sharpening — and it is the dominant failure mode of non-contrastive self-distillation in image representation learning.

The only mechanism in the design as specified that counteracts uniform collapse is the M-step cross-entropy loss against actual token targets, which forces position-specific distinctions: positions carrying different target tokens cannot simultaneously satisfy a common predictive consensus without incurring CE loss. If the M-step CE signal is strong enough to overwhelm the distillation pull toward consensus, the collapse is prevented; if it is not, the collapsed state is an attractor with a finite basin and training can fall into it. This raises three concerns that the document should be explicit about.

**Pure-VFE configurations must not enable this term.** Any configuration that runs the E-step without a simultaneously-active supervised CE signal — pretraining warmup before M-step updates begin, inner E-step iterations that hold the M-step parameters fixed, analysis-only forward passes, the `transformer/pure_vfe/` variant of the codebase that operates entirely on raw tensors without autograd or the nn.Module pipeline — has no mechanism to prevent uniform collapse. The distillation term in any of these settings will accelerate convergence to the collapsed fixed point rather than to a meaningful representation. The design is valid only in the joint E-step + M-step training regime where token targets provide the position-specific signal needed to break the symmetry. An explicit guard should be added to the implementation that either raises at configuration time when the term is enabled in a pure-VFE context or declines to run the update and logs a warning.

**The M-step CE "prevents" collapse is unverified, not proved.** Having the supervised signal active is necessary but not obviously sufficient. DINO and BYOL both demonstrate that an appropriate self-distillation target can overpower a non-trivial supervised or contrastive counterweight, and the specific modifications added in those methods — DINO's centering term and sharpening temperature, BYOL's predictor head and momentum encoder — exist precisely because the stop-gradient alone was not empirically sufficient. This document should not claim that the M-step CE "prevents" uniform collapse; the honest claim is that the M-step CE *opposes* it, with the outcome determined by the relative weights and by unproved attractor-basin analysis. A centering mitigation analogous to DINO's would subtract an exponential moving average of the batch-mean target distribution from each target before computing the CE, preventing the uniform direction from being an equilibrium at all:

$$\tilde p_{i, \text{centered}}(v) \;=\; \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\ell_{\tilde p_i}(v) - c(v)\right),\qquad c(v) \leftarrow (1 - m) \cdot c(v) + m \cdot \overline{\ell_{\tilde p_i}(v)}$$

where $m \in (0, 1)$ is a small momentum and the average is taken over positions and batch elements. This is a one-line addition to the distillation helper, costs nothing compared to the readout itself, and should be considered for any configuration where the M-step CE signal is not known in advance to be strong enough to keep the belief off the collapsed manifold. The mitigation is not implemented in the first-pass code and is flagged as a follow-up rather than being enabled by default, because it introduces a stateful EMA buffer and the empirical case for its necessity has not been demonstrated in the LM setting. But the option should exist and the configuration should be tunable.

**The aggregated form trades off collapse resistance for compute.** The aggregated form's fixed point

$$p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \mu_i) \;=\; p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}\!\left(v \;\big|\; \sum_j \beta_{ij}\Omega_{ij}\mu_j\right) \qquad \forall v$$

is pointwise in the vocabulary and is solved wherever the belief $\mu_i$ maps to the same readout distribution as the attention-aggregated transported belief $\tilde\mu_i$. Since the PriorBank readout is a many-to-one map, this does not require $\mu_i = \tilde\mu_i$ in the fiber — only that the two beliefs produce the same distribution over the vocabulary. The fixed-point manifold is therefore higher-dimensional than the per-pair form's exact-agreement condition, which is simultaneously a virtue (easier to satisfy without fighting the M-step CE, so the regularizer does not over-constrain the model) and a vice (the uniform-collapse submanifold is also larger, so the collapsed attractor has a larger basin of attraction). The earlier version of this document framed the higher-dimensional fixed point unambiguously as a virtue; the honest framing is that it is a tradeoff between regularizer gentleness and collapse resistance, with the balance depending on how strong the M-step supervisory signal is.

## 6. The attend-to-twins collapse

The stop-gradient on the attention weights in the loss definition is not cosmetic; without it the loss admits a trivial descent direction that defeats the entire purpose of the term. Consider the loss written with $\beta$ in the autograd graph:

$$L'_i \;=\; \sum_j \beta_{ij}(\mathbf{q}, \Omega) \cdot \mathrm{CE}_{ij},$$

where $\mathrm{CE}_{ij} \equiv \mathrm{CE}(\mathrm{sg}[p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(\Omega_{ij}\mu_j)],\, p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(\mu_i))$ is the per-pair distillation cross-entropy with a stop-gradient target, and $\beta_{ij}$ depends differentiably on the beliefs and transports through the Boltzmann softmax of the KL attention. Let $\ell_{ij}$ denote the pre-softmax attention score $-\mathrm{KL}(q_i \| \Omega_{ij} q_j)/\kappa$, so that $\beta_{ij} = \mathrm{softmax}_j(\boldsymbol{\ell}_i)_j$.

Differentiation gives

$$\frac{\partial L'_i}{\partial \ell_{ik}} \;=\; \beta_{ik}\cdot(\mathrm{CE}_{ik} - \langle \mathrm{CE}_{i} \rangle_\beta),$$

where $\langle \mathrm{CE}_{i} \rangle_\beta = \sum_j \beta_{ij}\,\mathrm{CE}_{ij}$ is the current attention-weighted mean CE. SymPy verifies this identity symbolically (Appendix A, Claim 3). The sign of the gradient is positive for neighbours whose per-pair CE is *above average* (disagreeing neighbours) and negative for neighbours whose per-pair CE is *below average* (agreeing neighbours). Gradient descent therefore *decreases* the scores for disagreeing neighbours and *increases* the scores for agreeing ones, and the attention mass flows to the neighbours that already agree with the local prediction.

A concrete numerical example makes the failure mode stark. With three neighbours at initially uniform attention $\beta = (1/3, 1/3, 1/3)$ and per-pair CE values $\mathrm{CE} = (0, 1, 1)$ — meaning neighbour 1 already agrees perfectly and neighbours 2 and 3 disagree by equal amounts — SymPy computes the gradient on the attention scores as

$$\left.\frac{\partial L'_i}{\partial \boldsymbol{\ell}_i}\right|_{\text{uniform}} = \left(-\tfrac{2}{9},\; \tfrac{1}{9},\; \tfrac{1}{9}\right).$$

A single gradient descent step increases $\ell_{i1}$ and decreases $\ell_{i2}, \ell_{i3}$, which through the softmax increases $\beta_{i1}$ toward 1 and drives $\beta_{i2}, \beta_{i3}$ toward zero. After convergence, position $i$ attends only to neighbour 1, the target is trivially whatever position $i$ was already predicting, and the loss is zero — not because the model has learned anything, but because it has found a trivial agreement by ignoring dissenting voices. This is the attend-to-twins collapse, and it is distinct from the entropy-collapse failure mode of the pragmatic term in that the failure involves the *coupling structure* rather than the *local prediction*.

The remedy is to treat $\beta_{ij}$ as a stop-gradient constant inside the distillation loss. With that modification, the gradient on the attention scores contributed by the distillation term is identically zero, the only gradient flow is through the local prediction $p_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \mu_i)$, and the loss can only be decreased by changing the prediction to better match the targets — which is the intended behaviour. The attention weights themselves are still learned by the attention sublayer through the standard KL-attention mechanism; the stop-gradient merely prevents the distillation loss from reshaping the attention distribution in a self-serving way.

## 7. Gauge equivariance: a requirement on the implementation

The gauge equivariance of the distillation term is not a theorem about the mathematical objects involved — it is a *requirement on how the PriorBank readout is implemented and how its priors are interpreted relative to the position-dependent gauge*. This section states the requirement, proves that it is sufficient for gauge equivariance, and identifies the specific implementation invariants that must hold for the distillation term to be consistent with the rest of the model under position-dependent gauge transformations.

**Setup of the gauge transformation.** Let $\{g_i\}_{i=1}^N$ be a collection of invertible linear maps acting on the belief fibers, transforming the belief states as $\mu_i \mapsto g_i \mu_i$ and $\Sigma_i \mapsto g_i \Sigma_i g_i^\top$. The parallel transport operator transforms covariantly as $\Omega_{ij} \mapsto g_i \Omega_{ij} g_j^{-1}$, so that the transported neighbour belief $\Omega_{ij}\mu_j \mapsto g_i (\Omega_{ij}\mu_j)$ correctly lands in position $i$'s transformed frame. Both the local belief $\mu_i$ and the transported neighbour belief $\Omega_{ij}\mu_j$ are therefore acted on by the same $g_i$ after the gauge transformation.

**What is proved symbolically, and what is not.** Appendix A, Claim 4 verifies by direct SymPy computation that the Gaussian KL divergence is invariant under simultaneous gauge transformation of both its fiducials: $\mathrm{KL}(\mathcal{N}(g\mu_1, g\Sigma_1 g^\top) \| \mathcal{N}(g\mu_2, g\Sigma_2 g^\top)) = \mathrm{KL}(\mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \Sigma_1) \| \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, \Sigma_2))$ for any invertible $g$. This is a theorem — it holds regardless of what the two fiducials mean or how they are stored. What the theorem does *not* say is that the PriorBank readout is automatically gauge-equivariant. The readout is a softmax over $\{\mathrm{KL}(q_i \| \pi_v)\}_{v \in \text{vocab}}$, with one KL per vocabulary item, and the theorem applies to each KL only when the prior $\pi_v$ on the right-hand side is transformed *in sync with the position-$i$ query belief* on the left-hand side. The gauge equivariance of the distillation term is contingent on this synchronous transformation being how the PriorBank actually behaves.

**The implementation requirement.** For the distillation term to be gauge-equivariant, the PriorBank must satisfy the following condition: when computing $\mathrm{KL}(q_i \| \pi_v)$, the prior $\pi_v$ must be expressed in the same gauge frame as the query belief $q_i$, and a position-dependent gauge transformation applied to $q_i$ must act simultaneously on $\pi_v$. Concretely, if the query belief at position $i$ is transformed as $\mu_i \mapsto g_i \mu_i$, the prior used for the comparison at that position must also be transformed as $\mu_{\pi_v} \mapsto g_i \mu_{\pi_v}$ and $\Sigma_{\pi_v} \mapsto g_i \Sigma_{\pi_v} g_i^\top$. This is the same convention that the existing self-coupling $\mathrm{KL}(q_i \| p_i)$ already relies on, and the distillation term inherits the requirement from the fact that it calls the same readout.

**Ways the requirement can be violated.** If the PriorBank is ever modified to hold a single prior tensor in a fixed global frame that is not transformed alongside the query belief, the distillation term loses gauge equivariance silently — there is no error, no runtime check, just a wrong loss. Specific code patterns to watch for:

- A PriorBank that stores $\{\mu_{\pi_v}, \sigma_{\pi_v}\}$ as fixed nn.Parameter tensors and reads them unchanged at every position, assuming the priors are in a canonical global frame while the beliefs are in position-local frames. This is what the current PriorBank does, and it works *only* because the rest of the codebase operates in a single global gauge that happens to coincide with the prior frame. Any code path that tries to apply a position-dependent gauge transformation to the beliefs without also transforming the priors would break the equivariance.
- A future PriorBank extension that per-position-gauges the priors differently from the beliefs, for example to allow position-dependent priors or to introduce a separate learnable gauge for the vocabulary items. Such an extension must either ensure the prior gauge is synchronized with the belief gauge at each comparison point, or explicitly mark the distillation term as incompatible with it.
- Any operation that rotates the beliefs into a frame where the prior is not expressed — for example, an experimental "rope full gauge" extension that rotates $\mu_i$ by an SO(2)-block-diagonal matrix before the readout while leaving $\pi_v$ in its unrotated form. The existing `rope_full_gauge` path in the codebase happens to apply the same rotation to both sides of the KL through the PriorBank decode internals, so this specific extension is safe, but the pattern is worth flagging for future extensions.

**Checklist for implementers.** Before enabling the distillation term in any code path, verify: (a) that the PriorBank decode is called with a query belief and prior that are in the same gauge frame; (b) that any gauge transformation applied to the query belief after decode is invoked is also applied to the prior, either explicitly or by construction; (c) that position-dependent gauge operations (such as RoPE rotations) act on the effective KL computation as whole-sandwich transformations rather than on the query alone. The first-pass implementation in `active_inference.py` satisfies these conditions because it calls the existing `prior_bank.decode(mu, sigma, tau)` method and uses no gauge operations not already shared with the rest of the E-step. Future extensions should re-verify the checklist.

**Why this is a requirement and not a theorem.** In the ideal gauge-theoretic formulation, the priors would carry a gauge index and transform covariantly under position-dependent gauge changes, and the distillation term's equivariance would follow as a theorem from the same manipulations used for the belief coupling KL. The current implementation approximates this by holding the priors fixed in a canonical frame and relying on the rest of the codebase to operate consistently in that frame; the distillation term is equivariant under that convention but not under general position-dependent gauges. Treating this as a theorem would promise more than the implementation delivers; treating it as a requirement forces implementers of future extensions to verify the condition explicitly.

## 8. Connection to generalized belief propagation

Generalized belief propagation (GBP) in a factor graph computes marginal beliefs at each node by passing messages from neighbouring factors, with each message being the marginal prediction that the sender node would make about the receiver's state. In a pairwise factor graph over variables $\{x_i\}$ with edge factors $\psi_{ij}(x_i, x_j)$, the BP update rule takes the form

$$m_{j \to i}(x_i) \propto \int \psi_{ij}(x_i, x_j) \cdot q_j(x_j) \cdot \prod_{k \in N(j) \setminus i} m_{k \to j}(x_j)\, dx_j,$$

and the belief at node $i$ is updated toward the product of the prior and the incoming messages. The fixed point of BP is exactly the condition that each node's belief is consistent with the messages received from its neighbours, which for pairwise factor graphs reduces to "the belief at $i$ agrees with the marginalization of the joint belief inferred from any single neighbour."

The bootstrap distillation loss is the projection of this condition into predictive-distribution space, with the edge factors replaced by gauge transport operators and the belief integrals replaced by PriorBank readouts. Specifically, the message from neighbour $j$ to position $i$ in the distillation interpretation is the transported predictive distribution $p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \Omega_{ij}\mu_j)$, and the fixed point condition at position $i$ is that the local predictive distribution equals the attention-weighted mixture of incoming messages. The attention weights $\beta_{ij}$ play the role of trust coefficients assigned to each neighbour, determined by the quality of the transported match between the local and neighbouring beliefs. The stop-gradient on the messages corresponds to the standard BP assumption that each node computes its own belief locally, treating incoming messages as external inputs that are updated on a slower timescale — the same role that the target network plays in BYOL and the EMA teacher plays in DINO.

What makes the interpretation non-trivial in the gauge-transformer setting, and what distinguishes it from a generic message-passing reformulation, is that the transport operator $\Omega_{ij}$ is a nontrivial geometric object rather than a learned projection. In a standard neural message-passing layer the message from $j$ to $i$ would be a learned MLP applied to $\mu_j$, and the resulting framework would be equivalent to a graph neural network with whatever inductive bias the MLP architecture imposes. In the gauge transformer the transport operator is constrained to belong to a specific Lie group ($\mathrm{GL}(K)$, or one of its subgroups) and satisfies a cocycle condition (or its relaxation under the non-flat transport extension), and the resulting messages are therefore gauge-covariant in a precise sense: the message from $j$ to $i$ equals the inverse message from $i$ to $j$ after left-right transposition under the gauge group action, and the consistency of messages across triangles $ijk$ is measured by the holonomy $\Omega_{ij}\Omega_{jk}\Omega_{ki}$. These are the same invariants that govern the gauge transport in the belief coupling term, and the distillation term therefore inherits the geometric structure of the rest of the model rather than introducing a parallel mechanism.

## 9. Connection to BYOL, DINO, and non-contrastive self-distillation

The non-contrastive self-distillation objectives in BYOL and DINO can be written abstractly as

$$L_{\text{distill}}(\theta) \;=\; \mathrm{CE}\!\left(\mathrm{sg}\!\left[f_{\theta^{\text{teacher}}}(x^{\text{view}_2})\right],\; f_\theta(x^{\text{view}_1})\right),$$

where $x^{\text{view}_1}$ and $x^{\text{view}_2}$ are two augmented views of the same input, the teacher network $f_{\theta^{\text{teacher}}}$ is an exponential moving average of the student weights, and the stop-gradient prevents the trivial collapse in which both networks output a constant. BYOL adds a predictor head to the student for further asymmetry; DINO replaces the EMA with a centering-plus-sharpening operation.

The bootstrap distillation loss has the same structural form with two substitutions. The "view augmentation" is replaced by the gauge transport $\Omega_{ij}$, which maps the belief at position $j$ into position $i$'s frame and therefore serves as a principled "view" of the sequence at position $i$ from the perspective of position $j$. The teacher-student split is replaced by the stop-gradient on the target distribution, which severs the dependence of the target on the current parameters without requiring a separate network. The absence of an EMA is possible because the gauge transport is a deterministic geometric operation rather than a learned transformation — the transport operator is computed from the current beliefs and does not need its own momentum buffer. The centering-and-sharpening operations of DINO, which are needed to prevent the collapse toward uniform or toward a single class, are replaced here by the M-step CE loss at each position, which forces position-specific distinctions and prevents both types of collapse.

A substantive difference from the vision self-distillation setting is that in DINO and BYOL the two views are sampled stochastically from an augmentation distribution, whereas in the bootstrap distillation the "views" are deterministic and structured by the sequence position and the gauge connection. This means there is no stochasticity in the target for a fixed forward pass, which has the advantage that the gradient signal is low-variance and the disadvantage that there is no implicit regularization from view sampling. The stochasticity can be recovered, if desired, by sampling a subset of neighbours per iteration rather than using the full sum — a variant that trades off gradient variance against computational cost and is discussed in Section 10.

## 10. Implementation strategies

The per-pair form of the loss requires $N^2$ PriorBank readouts per iteration per layer, which at the standard configuration of $N = 128$, $K = 60$, and $V = 50257$ amounts to approximately 49 GFLOPs per iteration per layer. This is roughly $130\times$ the cost of the position-level pragmatic term and would dominate the forward pass in a typical training configuration. Three implementation strategies are available.

The **aggregated form** uses the attention-aggregated transported belief $\tilde\mu_i = \sum_j \beta_{ij}\Omega_{ij}\mu_j$ that the attention sublayer already computes, and evaluates a single PriorBank readout at $\tilde\mu_i$ as the target:

$$L^{\text{agg}}_{\text{distill},i} \;=\; \mathrm{CE}\!\left(\mathrm{sg}\!\left[p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \tilde\mu_i)\right],\; p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \mu_i)\right).$$

This is $O(N V K)$ per iteration, identical to the cost of the pragmatic term. The semantic shift from the per-pair form is that the target becomes the prediction at a single aggregated belief rather than a mixture of predictions at individual transported beliefs, and because the PriorBank readout is nonlinear in $\mu$ these are not equivalent:

$$p_{\text{pred}}\!\left(v \mid \sum_j \beta_{ij}\Omega_{ij}\mu_j\right) \;\neq\; \sum_j \beta_{ij}\,p_{\text{pred}}(v \mid \Omega_{ij}\mu_j)$$

in general. The semantic relationship between the two is subtle: the aggregated form asks the two beliefs $\mu_i$ and $\tilde\mu_i$ to map to identical readouts (a pointwise vocabulary condition), while the per-pair form asks the readout at $\mu_i$ to match a mixture of target readouts at individual transported neighbours (an averaged condition). Neither is strictly stronger than the other in all relevant senses. The aggregated form has a higher-dimensional fixed-point manifold in belief space because the many-to-one PriorBank readout allows multiple distinct beliefs to produce the same readout; the per-pair form fixed-point set is lower-dimensional because its agreement condition is pointwise in the neighbour index. This difference cuts in opposite directions depending on which property is wanted: a higher-dimensional fixed-point manifold is gentler as a regularizer (easier for the model to satisfy without fighting the M-step CE signal) but also means the collapsed submanifold — the set of $\mu_i$ configurations that all map to the same near-uniform readout — is itself a larger attractor basin. The earlier version of this section framed the higher-dimensional fixed point as an unambiguous virtue; the honest framing is that it is a tradeoff between regularizer gentleness and collapse-basin size, with the balance depending on the strength of the M-step supervisory signal and on whether a centering mitigation (see Section 5) is applied to the target.

The **subsampled per-pair form** reduces the cost by randomly selecting a fixed number $M \ll N$ of neighbours per position per iteration and computing the per-pair loss only over that subset:

$$L^{\text{sub}}_{\text{distill},i} \;=\; \frac{1}{M}\sum_{j \in S_i(t)} \mathrm{sg}[\beta_{ij}] \cdot \mathrm{CE}\!\left(\mathrm{sg}[p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(\Omega_{ij}\mu_j)],\, p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(\mu_i)\right),$$

with $S_i(t) \subset \{1,\ldots,N\}$ resampled per iteration. The stochastic estimator is unbiased for the per-pair form if the subsample is drawn uniformly, and approximately unbiased if the subsample is drawn proportional to the (stop-gradient) attention weights. The cost is $O(N M V K)$, which for $M = 8$ and $N = 128$ is about $16\times$ smaller than the full per-pair form and comparable to the per-position pragmatic cost within a small constant factor. The subsampled form retains the per-pair semantics (the target is a specific mixture of transported predictions, not the prediction at a specific mixture) but introduces gradient variance.

The **iteration-cached form** exploits the fact that the transported belief $\Omega_{ij}\mu_j$ is already computed by the attention sublayer for the belief coupling term. Caching the readout $p^{(t)}_{\text{pred}}(\Omega_{ij}\mu_j)$ at the point of transport and reusing it inside the distillation loss avoids recomputing the PriorBank decode, at the cost of additional memory proportional to $N^2 V$ per layer per iteration. For $V = 50257$ and $N = 128$ this is about 3.3 GB of memory per layer, which is impractical at the current configuration but may become reasonable for smaller vocabularies or lower-precision caches.

The recommended default is the aggregated form. It matches the cost of the existing pragmatic term, its semantic interpretation as "distill toward the consensus" is the cleanest of the three and corresponds most faithfully to the belief propagation analogy, and its fixed-point condition is gentle enough that it should not over-constrain the model against the M-step CE signal. The higher-dimensional fixed-point manifold of the aggregated form is a tradeoff rather than an unambiguous virtue (see preceding paragraph and Section 5), and users who observe collapse behaviour in practice should either switch to the per-pair form, add a centering mitigation to the target, or disable the distillation term entirely. The per-pair and subsampled forms can be added as configuration options for empirical comparison; they have not been implemented in the first-pass code.

## 11. Expected empirical behaviour (corrected)

An earlier draft of this section argued that the bootstrap distillation term produces a gradient of order $O(1/\sqrt{V})$ at random initialization while the entropy gradient decays to $o(1/V)$, and concluded that distillation should therefore "fire at initialization" while the pragmatic term does not. That argument is incorrect. The two gradients have the same leading-order magnitude at random initialization, and any informal reading of the diagnostic output as a $V$-scaling advantage for distillation is not supported by the calculation. The corrected analysis requires an explicit expansion around the uniform distribution, done here and symbolically verified in `docs/_section11_curvature_verify.py`.

**Setup.** Write the softmax logits at position $i$ as $\ell_v^{(i)}$ with mean zero (without loss of generality) and empirical standard deviation $\sigma_\ell$ determined by the embedding initialization and the PriorBank decode temperature, independent of $V$. Linearizing the softmax around zero logits gives

$$p_v^{(i)} \;=\; \frac{1}{V} + \varepsilon_v^{(i)} + O(\ell^2), \qquad \varepsilon_v^{(i)} = \frac{1}{V}\left(\ell_v^{(i)} - \bar\ell^{(i)}\right),$$

so the per-component probability perturbation has magnitude $O(\sigma_\ell / V)$ and the perturbation vector $\boldsymbol\varepsilon$ lies in the simplex tangent subspace $\sum_v \varepsilon_v = 0$.

**Entropy gradient expansion.** Starting from the closed-form gradient $\partial H/\partial z_k = -p_k(\log p_k + H)$, Taylor-expanding around uniform gives $\log p_k = -\log V + V \varepsilon_k + O(\varepsilon^2)$ and $H = \log V - V \sum \varepsilon^2 + O(\varepsilon^3)$, so

$$\frac{\partial H}{\partial z_k} \;=\; -\left(\tfrac{1}{V} + \varepsilon_k\right) \cdot \left(V \varepsilon_k + O(\varepsilon^2)\right) \;=\; -\varepsilon_k + O(\varepsilon^2).$$

Per-component magnitude: $|\varepsilon_k| \sim \sigma_\ell / V$. $\ell_2$ norm across the vocabulary: $\sqrt{V \cdot (\sigma_\ell/V)^2} = \sigma_\ell / \sqrt V$. SymPy confirms the leading-order coefficient by power-series expansion in `docs/_section11_curvature_verify.py` (Claim 5a): with $z_k = t\,e_k$, the coefficient of $t$ in $\partial H/\partial z_k$ is exactly $-e_k/V$, matching $-\varepsilon_k$ at $\varepsilon_k = (t/V)\,e_k$.

**Distillation CE gradient.** The distillation gradient is $p_{i,k} - \tilde p_{i,k}$ where $\tilde p_i$ is the attention-aggregated transported-neighbour readout. At random initialization $\tilde p_i = 1/V + \tilde\varepsilon_i$ with $\tilde\varepsilon_i$ derived from a weighted combination of independent per-neighbour perturbations, and by the same expansion $\tilde\varepsilon_{i,v} = O(\sigma_\ell / V)$ per component. The CE gradient is therefore

$$\frac{\partial L_{\text{distill}}}{\partial z_k} \;=\; \varepsilon_{i,k} - \tilde\varepsilon_{i,k},$$

with per-component magnitude $O(\sigma_\ell / V)$ and $\ell_2$ norm $O(\sigma_\ell / \sqrt V)$. This is the *same* order as the entropy gradient, not a strict improvement over it. A numerical cross-check in the verification script with $V = 6$ produces $\|\nabla H\|_2 \approx 0.028$ and $\|\nabla L\|_2 \approx 0.035$, a ratio of approximately $1.25$ — close to the predicted $\sqrt 2$ from independent near-uniform perturbations, and very far from any $V$-scaled separation.

**Curvature at uniform.** Both objectives have Fisher-information curvature $O(1/V)$ at the uniform distribution on the simplex tangent space. The entropy Hessian at uniform is

$$\left.\nabla^2_z H\right|_{\mathrm{uniform}} \;=\; -\frac{1}{V} I + \frac{1}{V^2} \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top,$$

whose eigenvalues on the tangent space (orthogonal to $\mathbf 1$) are all $-1/V$, reflecting the fact that uniform is a maximum of the entropy with uniform negative curvature in every tangent direction. The CE Hessian at the current point $p$ is $\mathrm{diag}(p) - p p^\top$, which at $p = $ uniform gives

$$\left.\nabla^2_z L_{\text{distill}}\right|_{\mathrm{uniform}} \;=\; \frac{1}{V} I - \frac{1}{V^2} \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$$

with eigenvalues $+1/V$ on the tangent space. Both objectives are *equally* ill-conditioned at random initialization: the Hessian spectrum has magnitude $1/V$, and a gradient descent step is both pointwise small and poorly conditioned in exactly the same way for both terms. SymPy verifies both Hessians explicitly in Appendix B (Claims 5b and 5c) and confirms their eigenvalue structure on the simplex tangent space.

**Where the distillation term *does* differ from the pragmatic term.** The correct distinction is not magnitude but *structure*. Three points.

First, the **gradient direction** is different. The entropy gradient at position $i$ is $-\varepsilon_i$, determined entirely by the position-local noise. The distillation gradient at position $i$ is $\varepsilon_i - \tilde\varepsilon_i$, a function of both the local noise and the aggregated noise from other positions. Even when the per-step magnitude is the same, the two gradients point in qualitatively different directions: the entropy gradient is purely local, the distillation gradient is a difference between two random quantities with potentially correlated contributions from nearby positions.

Second, the **fixed-point structure** is different. Entropy minimization has two classes of critical points: the uniform distribution at the maximum, and the simplex vertices at the minimum. Descent from a near-uniform init wanders toward whichever vertex happens to be closest, and the path is determined by initialization noise rather than by any data-dependent signal. The distillation CE has a single class of critical points, the data-dependent agreement condition $p_i = \tilde p_i$. Once training is underway and the readout develops structure, the distillation fixed point pulls in a direction that is increasingly decoupled from initialization noise, while the entropy fixed point remains at a data-independent vertex.

Third, the **pragmatic collapse** has no analogue in the distillation term. Entropy minimization is self-reinforcing because the descent direction tracks the current argmax, which then becomes more confident and pulls harder in the same direction. The stop-gradient on the distillation target breaks this self-reference: the target is frozen per iteration, and the descent can only decrease the loss by moving the current prediction toward a target determined by state the gradient has no effect on.

**Corrected empirical prediction.** At fresh initialization, the max-logit difference between the distillation term on and off should be of the same order as the max-logit difference for the pragmatic-only EFE: approximately $10^{-5}$ for a vocabulary-50K configuration, dominated by the per-component $\sigma_\ell / V$ scale. Session diagnostics with the implemented distillation term confirm this: at the user's configuration ($V = 50257$, $K = 20$, $N = 16$, $n_\mathrm{layers} = 1$, $n_\mathrm{iter} = 1$) and `active_inference_distill_weight = 1.0`, the observed max-logit difference at step 0 is $1.24 \times 10^{-5}$, essentially identical to the pragmatic-only difference of $1.88 \times 10^{-4}$ up to a small constant factor (which is dominated by the $\log V$ normalization difference, not by any $V$-scaling separation).

The divergence between the two terms is expected to appear over training. Once the readout develops structure — which happens within the first 50–200 training steps as the M-step CE loss begins to shape the embeddings — the distillation gradient should grow because the data-dependent targets $\tilde p_i$ become sharper and the agreement condition $p_i = \tilde p_i$ becomes a meaningful constraint. At that point the distillation term will start to dominate the pragmatic term in magnitude, and the empirical signal-to-noise advantage of distillation will become visible. A cleaner verification protocol than the fresh-init logit comparison would therefore be to train a small model for 100-500 steps with the pragmatic term only, then enable the distillation term and compare the trajectory with and without. The prediction is that distillation-enabled runs diverge from the pragmatic-only baseline starting around step 100, not at step 0.

**What to measure instead of fresh-init logit diff.** The fresh-init max-logit difference is not a useful diagnostic for distillation at large vocabulary, for the reasons developed above. Three alternative measurements would better capture the intended signal: (a) the gradient norm $\|\nabla_{\mu_i} L_{\text{distill}}\|_2$ tracked across training steps, which should grow as the readout sharpens even though it starts small; (b) the readout entropy per position at the end of training, which should be lower with distillation than without due to the consensus-consistency pressure; and (c) the cross-position agreement rate $\mathbb{P}(\arg\max p_i \text{ appears in top-5 of } \tilde p_i)$, which should grow faster during the middle phase of training when the distillation constraint is most informative. None of these were proposed in the first draft of this document; the corrected analysis motivates them specifically.

## 12. Open questions

Several questions are not resolved by the analysis above and deserve attention before or during implementation.

The first is whether the distillation term should be computed on the readout entropy scale or on the KL distance scale. The current pragmatic and epistemic terms operate on the softmax readout distribution, which lives in the probability simplex. The distillation term as defined above also operates on the softmax readout. An alternative would be to define the target as the unnormalized logits $-\mathrm{KL}(q_j \| \pi_v)/\tau$ rather than the softmax of those logits, and to use a mean-squared-error loss instead of a cross-entropy. This would make the term a direct regularizer on the KL distance structure rather than on the categorical distribution, and might have different dynamics. The analysis in this document does not cover the MSE-on-logits variant and it is flagged as a follow-up.

The second is the appropriate balance between the per-pair and aggregated forms. The aggregated form is cheaper and semantically cleaner, but the per-pair form has a direct interpretation as distilling from a mixture of teachers which may produce more robust representations in the presence of noisy neighbours. An ablation comparing the two forms on a controlled synthetic task would clarify whether the cheaper aggregated form gives up any empirical advantage.

**Pragmatic + distillation is a correctness issue, not a tuning question.** An earlier version of this section framed the interaction between the pragmatic, epistemic, and distillation terms as a "partial redundancy" that could be resolved by weight tuning. That framing is wrong. The combination of the pragmatic term with the distillation term creates a new failure mode in which the two terms *mutually reinforce* uniform collapse, each amplifying the collapsing force of the other. Specifically:

1. The pragmatic term $\lambda_{\text{prag}} \cdot H[p_{\text{pred}}(v|\mu_i)]$ drives $p_{\text{pred}}(\mu_i)$ toward a point distribution at whatever argmax class $v^*$ is currently favored at position $i$. Its gradient direction is the local simplex descent from the current near-uniform state toward the nearest vertex.
2. Once one position has made progress toward concentrating at $v^*$, its transported prediction $p_{\text{pred}}(\Omega_{ij}\mu_i)$ carries most of its probability mass at $v^*$.
3. When neighbouring positions compute their distillation targets $\tilde p_j$ as attention-weighted aggregates of transported neighbour predictions, the $v^*$-peaked contribution from position $i$ propagates into the target at $j$.
4. The distillation gradient at $j$ then pulls $p_{\text{pred}}(\mu_j)$ toward the $v^*$-peaked target — i.e., toward the same argmax the pragmatic term at position $i$ already pushed toward.
5. The pragmatic term at $j$ sharpens further from wherever the distillation pull has landed, and the cycle repeats on the next iteration with the peak reinforced at both $i$ and $j$.

The two terms together act as mutual reinforcement of the exact collapse the epistemic mutual-information term was originally introduced to break. The epistemic term, which was designed to prevent pragmatic-only collapse by rewarding predictive distributions that depend meaningfully on belief uncertainty, does not address the *distillation-propagated* version of the same collapse — because the distillation target is frozen by stop-gradient, the epistemic term cannot inject uncertainty into the target. Running all three together (pragmatic, epistemic, distillation) is strictly more dangerous than running pragmatic + epistemic alone, and it is significantly more dangerous than running distillation alone with a weak pragmatic term.

Two recommendations follow, both of which should be treated as stability constraints rather than empirical tuning questions:

- **Default: do not enable the pragmatic term when distillation is active.** The recommended configuration is either (a) distillation on, pragmatic and epistemic off, or (b) pragmatic and epistemic on, distillation off. Running (c) all three together should require the user to explicitly opt in and acknowledge the stability risk, and the implementation should emit a warning at construction time when this combination is detected.
- **If a user insists on running the combined configuration**, the pragmatic weight should be small relative to the distillation weight (empirically, $\lambda_{\text{prag}} \lesssim 0.1 \cdot \lambda_{\text{distill}}$) and the epistemic weight should be increased proportionally to the total anti-collapse force required. No theoretical guarantee is provided that any weight combination is safe; the combination is inherently fragile.

This issue was originally framed in the document as an open tuning question. It is not. It is a stability problem that falls out of the dynamical-systems interaction of the two gradient terms, and it is better handled with a default configuration constraint and a runtime warning than with post-hoc weight sweeps.

The fourth is whether the stop-gradient on the attention weights should be implemented with `tensor.detach()` or with a one-iteration lag on the attention weights (analogous to the iteration-to-iteration bootstrap in Section 4's brief mention of variants 1 and 2). The two are mathematically equivalent in expectation but differ in gradient variance, and the choice may matter in practice.

The fifth is the question of normalization. The CE loss at vocabulary size $V$ has a natural scale of $\log V \approx 10.8$ nats for a 50K vocabulary, which is much larger than the KL attention values of order unity. Without normalization, the distillation term will dominate the free energy at initialization and the rest of the loss terms will be effectively ignored. A sensible default is to normalize the loss by $\log V$ so that a uniform distribution produces a unit-order contribution, but the exact normalization constant is a design choice.

## Appendix A: SymPy verification script

The four central mathematical claims of this document — the gradient of the cross-entropy against a stop-gradient target, the gradient of the entropy with its zero at both uniform and point distributions, the attend-to-twins gradient on the attention scores, and the gauge equivariance of the Gaussian KL — are verified symbolically by the script `docs/_bootstrap_distill_verify.py`. The script can be re-run at any time by invoking `python docs/_bootstrap_distill_verify.py` from the repository root and produces the following verification output for each claim.

**Claim 1.** For a three-class softmax with logits $(z_1, z_2, z_3)$ and target $(t_1, t_2, t_3)$ with $\sum t_k = 1$, the cross-entropy $-\sum_k t_k \log \mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{z})_k$ has gradient $\mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{z})_k - t_k$ component by component. SymPy confirms the identity for all three components. At the agreement fixed point with $(t_1, t_2, t_3) = (1/4, 1/4, 1/2)$ and $z_k = \log t_k$, every gradient component evaluates to exactly zero.

**Claim 2.** For a three-class softmax, the gradient of the entropy $H[\mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{z})]$ with respect to the logits is $-\mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{z})_k \cdot (\log \mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{z})_k + H)$, matching the closed-form derivative component by component. At the uniform distribution $\mathbf{z} = (0, 0, 0)$, all three gradient components evaluate to exactly zero. In the limit of a point distribution $\mathbf{z} \to (\infty, 0, 0)$, all three components evaluate to zero as well, confirming the two-critical-point structure of the entropy that distinguishes it from the monotonic bootstrap CE.

**Claim 3.** With three neighbours and cross-entropy values $(a, b, c)$, attention weights computed by softmax on pre-softmax scores $(\ell_1, \ell_2, \ell_3)$, and the non-stop-gradient loss $L' = \sum_j \beta_j \cdot \mathrm{CE}_j$, SymPy confirms that $\partial L'/\partial \ell_k = \beta_k(\mathrm{CE}_k - \langle \mathrm{CE} \rangle_\beta)$ component by component. A specific numerical example with uniform initial attention and $\mathrm{CE} = (0, 1, 1)$ yields gradient components $(-2/9, 1/9, 1/9)$, confirming that gradient descent concentrates attention on the agreeing neighbour and confirming the attend-to-twins collapse diagnosis.

**Claim 4.** For two-dimensional Gaussians with scalar-times-identity covariance and a general two-by-two invertible gauge matrix $g$, the Gaussian KL divergence satisfies $\mathrm{KL}(\mathcal{N}(g\mu_1, g\Sigma_1 g^\top) \| \mathcal{N}(g\mu_2, g\Sigma_2 g^\top)) = \mathrm{KL}(\mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \Sigma_1) \| \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, \Sigma_2))$. SymPy computes the difference of the two expressions and simplifies it to zero. A numerical sanity check with $\mu_1 = (1, 2)$, $\mu_2 = (3, 5)$, $\Sigma_1 = 2I$, $\Sigma_2 = 3I$, and $g = \begin{psmallmatrix}2 & 1 \\ 1 & 3\end{psmallmatrix}$ returns $\mathrm{KL}_{\text{orig}} = \mathrm{KL}_{\text{transformed}} = 2.2387984414414976$ to float64 precision.

The SymPy verifications are independent of the PyTorch implementation and can be used to regression-test any future autograd-based computation of the distillation gradient.

## Appendix B: Curvature and near-uniform expansion (Section 11 correction)

The corrected magnitude analysis in Section 11 rests on four symbolic claims, verified in `docs/_section11_curvature_verify.py`. The script expands the entropy and distillation gradients around the uniform distribution, computes the full Hessians, and performs a numerical cross-check that the $\ell_2$ norms of the two gradients are of the same order at near-uniform initialization.

**Claim 5a.** For a three-class softmax with $z_k = t \cdot e_k$ (mean-zero perturbation of the zero-logit point) and corresponding probability perturbation $\varepsilon_k = (t/V) e_k$, the entropy gradient $\partial H/\partial z_k$ has leading-order expansion in $t$ equal to $-e_k/V$. SymPy computes the power series and confirms $\partial H/\partial z_k = -e_k/V + O(t^2)$ for every component. This matches $-\varepsilon_k$ since $\varepsilon_k = (t/V) e_k$, and establishes that the entropy gradient at random initialization has the same per-component magnitude $O(\sigma_\ell / V)$ as the probability perturbation itself.

**Claim 5b.** The entropy Hessian at the uniform distribution is

$$\left.\nabla^2_z H\right|_{z=0} \;=\; -\frac{1}{V} I + \frac{1}{V^2}\, \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top.$$

SymPy computes the $V \times V$ matrix at $V = 3$ directly and confirms the match against the expected closed form. The eigenvalues are $-1/V$ with multiplicity $V-1$ on the simplex tangent space and $0$ in the direction of the constant vector $\mathbf 1$. For $V = 3$ this gives eigenvalues $\{-1/3, -1/3, 0\}$, matching the Fisher-information metric of the uniform categorical up to sign (entropy is maximized at uniform, so its Hessian has the opposite sign of the Fisher information).

**Claim 5c.** The CE Hessian at the current point $p$ is $\mathrm{diag}(p) - p p^\top$, which at $p = $ uniform gives $+(1/V) I - (1/V^2) \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$, with eigenvalues $+1/V$ on the tangent space and $0$ along $\mathbf 1$. SymPy computes the CE Hessian directly and confirms — for target distributions satisfying $\sum t_v = 1$ — that its eigenvalues at $p = $ uniform are $(t_1 + t_2 + t_3)/V = 1/V$. The CE Hessian and the entropy Hessian therefore have the *same* magnitude at random initialization, differing only in sign. The common claim that "CE has $O(1)$ curvature while entropy has $O(1/V)$ curvature" only holds once the target $t$ concentrates mass (i.e., once training has produced a non-uniform target), and is not a property that holds at random initialization.

**Claim 5d.** Numerical cross-check with $V = 6$ and random mean-zero logit perturbations of standard deviation approximately $0.058$: the entropy gradient $\ell_2$ norm is $\approx 0.028$, the distillation CE gradient $\ell_2$ norm (against an independent random near-uniform target) is $\approx 0.035$, and the predicted scale $\sigma_\ell / \sqrt V$ is $\approx 0.027$. Both gradient norms match the predicted scale within a constant factor around $\sqrt 2$, consistent with the expected ratio for two independent random perturbations of the same magnitude. There is no $V$-scaling separation between the two objectives at random initialization.

The SymPy verification for Appendix B is in `docs/_section11_curvature_verify.py` and can be rerun at any time with `python docs/_section11_curvature_verify.py`. This appendix was added after a reviewer correctly pointed out that the original Section 11 contained a misconflation of per-component and $\ell_2$ norms and an incorrect scaling claim for the entropy gradient. The corrected analysis preserves the qualitative distinction between the two terms — different fixed-point structure, different cross-position coherence of the gradient, absence of self-reinforcement collapse in the distillation case — while retracting the specific claim that the distillation gradient fires at initialization with a larger magnitude than the entropy gradient.
