## FINDINGS

### 1. Richness lemma elides the global normaliser dependence
- Location: Appendix `app:conditional_uniqueness`, "Richness lemma" paragraph at lines 157–162 of the appendix; equations `eq:ode_app`, `eq:geometric_mean_target_app`.
- Severity: MAJOR
- Claim under review: "Since $p_i(c)$ and $q_j(c)$ may be chosen independently and may take any positive values, the geometric-mean expression for $q_i^*(c)$ takes any positive value, and the ratio $q_i^*(c)/(\Omega_{ij}(c)q_j(c))$ ranges over all of $(0,\infty)$."
- Defect: The geometric-mean target is the *normalised* density $q_i^*(c) = Z_i^{-1}\, p_i(c)^{1/(1+B)} \prod_k (\Omega_{ik}q_k)(c)^{\beta_{ik}/(1+B)}$, where $Z_i = \int p_i(c')^{1/(1+B)} \prod_k (\Omega_{ik}q_k)(c')^{\beta_{ik}/(1+B)}\,dc'$ is a *global* functional of $p_i$ and all $\{q_k\}$. The argument "choose $p_i(c)$ and $q_j(c)$ independently to make the ratio at point $c$ take any positive value" requires that one can vary $p_i(c)$ at a single base point without changing $Z_i$ — but $Z_i$ explicitly depends on the values of $p_i$ on a set of positive measure. A localised perturbation of $p_i$ on a vanishingly small neighbourhood of $c$ changes $q_i^*(c)$ by the same factor as it changes the integrand at $c$, yet leaves $Z_i$ essentially unchanged, so the *pointwise* ratio can in principle be moved freely by a Dirac-localised perturbation; but a Dirac is not in the admissible class of densities. For genuine perturbations supported on a finite-measure set, $Z_i$ moves coupled to the pointwise value, and the argument that $r(c) \in (0,\infty)$ is *attained* (not merely approached as a limit) is not given. The lemma needs either (i) a Lebesgue-density argument restricting to perturbations whose $Z_i$-correction vanishes faster than the pointwise change, or (ii) an explicit construction (e.g.\ a one-parameter family of admissible $(p_i, \{q_k\})$ for which $r(c_0)$ traces $(0,\infty)$ continuously). As stated, the lemma proves only that the attainable ratios are *dense* in some subinterval that depends on the regularity class chosen for the densities, which is strictly weaker than the pointwise functional identity $f'(t) = \log t + k$ on $(0,\infty)$.
- Suggested fix: Either (a) restrict the divergence class to those whose $f'$ is real-analytic, in which case density on any open subinterval of $(0,\infty)$ together with analyticity implies the pointwise identity globally; or (b) supply an explicit two-parameter family of admissible densities for which the ratio at a fixed base point $c_0$ ranges over a known open subinterval of $(0,\infty)$, and lift the conclusion to $(0,\infty)$ via either monotonicity of the geometric mean or a continuation argument; or (c) downgrade the conclusion explicitly to "$f' = \log + k$ on the attainable range" and argue separately why this range is wide enough to fix the standard normalisation $f(1)=0, f'(1)=0$.

### 2. Mean-field factorisation is doing load-bearing work in the softmax derivation
- Location: `sec:mixture_derivation`, Eq.~\eqref{eq:mixture_posterior} and following derivation.
- Severity: MAJOR
- Claim under review: "$Q(k,z) = q_i(k)\cdot\beta(z)$" labelled "mean-field factorization", followed by "Solving and normalizing we find the softmax form".
- Defect: The factorisation $Q(k,z) = q_i(k)\beta(z)$ is equivalent to the substantive constraint $Q(k\mid z) = q_i(k)$, i.e.\ the variational consensus state is independent of the source $z$. The KL between $Q$ and $P$ separates into the source-conditional term $\sum_j \beta_{ij}\, D_{\mathrm{KL}}[q_i(k)\|P(k\mid z=j)] = \sum_j \beta_{ij}\,D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_i\|\Omega_{ij}q_j)$ *only because* $Q(k\mid z)$ does not depend on $z$. If a richer ansatz $Q(k\mid z=j) = q_i^{(j)}(k)$ is allowed (which the variational principle alone does not forbid), the KL becomes $\sum_j \beta_{ij}\,D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_i^{(j)}\|\Omega_{ij}q_j) - H(\beta) + \mathrm{const}$, and minimising jointly over $\{q_i^{(j)}\}$ and $\beta$ gives $q_i^{(j)} = \Omega_{ij}q_j$ at the optimum, collapsing the alignment energy to zero and the softmax to the prior — *not* the standard transformer form. The "softmax-of-negative-KL" output is therefore a consequence of the source-independence constraint, not of the variational principle, and the prose presents the factorisation as an approximation when its real role is to restrict the variational class. The status note flags the construction as "consensus-energy ansatz rather than generative-model derivation" but does not surface this specific factorisation defect.
- Suggested fix: State explicitly that the softmax form is conditional on the source-independence constraint $Q(k\mid z) = q_i(k)$, and frame the construction either as (i) a hard architectural choice (the agent maintains a *single* belief, not one per source candidate), or (ii) a tightening of the variational class motivated by tractability. Clarify that under (i) the form is exact and under (ii) the argument is mean-field with the standard caveats.

### 3. Cross-block $[C^{\mu\Sigma}]_{ii}$ at the same agent omits a sender-side contribution
- Location: `sec:mass`, "Cross Mean-Covariance Blocks" subsection, Eq.~\eqref{eq:cross_block} and surrounding text.
- Severity: MAJOR
- Claim under review: "$[C^{\mu\Sigma}]_{ik} = 0 \quad \text{when } \mu_i = \tilde{\mu}_k.$" (and the prose covers only prior, sensory, and the receiver-from-$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_i\|\tilde q_k)$ contributions to $[C^{\mu\Sigma}]_{ii}$).
- Defect: The total Hessian cross-block $[C^{\mu\Sigma}]_{ii} \equiv \partial^2 \mathcal{F}/(\partial\mu_i \partial\Sigma_i)$ also receives contributions from agent~$i$'s role as a *sender* in the consensus pairs $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_j \| \Omega_{ji}q_i)$. The first-derivative expression in the manuscript is $\partial D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_j\|\tilde q_i)/\partial\mu_i = \Lambda_{q_i}\Omega_{ji}^{-1}(\tilde\mu_i^{(j)} - \mu_j) = \Omega_{ji}^{-T}\Lambda_{q_i}^{(j)\,\mathrm{frame-correct}}\cdot$ (residual). Differentiating again with respect to $\Sigma_i$ moves the precision factor $\Lambda_{q_i} = \Sigma_i^{-1}$, producing $-\Lambda_{q_i}V\Lambda_{q_i}\Omega_{ji}^{-1}(\tilde\mu_i^{(j)}-\mu_j)$ for the directional derivative in $V = \delta\Sigma_i$. This contribution vanishes at consensus ($\tilde\mu_i^{(j)} = \mu_j$) but the manuscript never computes it, never lists it as a contribution to $[C^{\mu\Sigma}]_{ii}$ before declaring "$[C^{\mu\Sigma}]_{ii}^{\mathrm{sensory}}=0$, prior$=0$" and proceeding directly to the receiver consensus block. Off consensus the omission is non-trivial and changes the cross-block structure on which the mass analogy depends. The boxed claim that $[C^{\mu\Sigma}]_{ik}=0$ at consensus is correct *only* once both sender and receiver contributions are computed and shown each to vanish — which the manuscript has not done.
- Suggested fix: Add the sender-contribution computation explicitly (it is a one-line directional derivative), confirm it vanishes at consensus, and update the boxed equation's preamble to say "summing receiver and sender contributions, both vanish at consensus, hence ...". If off-consensus behavior is needed for any downstream argument (e.g.\ stability of the consensus minimum under $\mu$–$\Sigma$ coupling), state it explicitly and propagate it.

### 4. "Alignment-dominated" precision regime requires a coupling not present in the canonical functional
- Location: Appendix `app:covariance_dynamics`, "Alignment-dominated regime", Eq.~\eqref{eq:beta_weighted_precision}.
- Severity: MINOR
- Claim under review: "$\Sigma_i^{-1} \approx \sum_j \beta_{ij}(\Omega_{ij}\Sigma_j\Omega_{ij}^\top)^{-1}$" (in the small-$\tau$, high-$A/P$ regime).
- Defect: The manuscript correctly identifies that small $\tau$ alone does not amplify the alignment block — because $\sum_j \beta_{ij} = 1$ — and patches the argument by appealing to "an external coupling strength $A$ multiplying the alignment block ... large compared with $\|\Sigma_{p,i}^{-1}\|$". But the canonical free-energy functional Eq.~\eqref{eq:free_energy_functional_final} has *no* such overall coupling $A$ in front of the alignment integral: the alignment block enters with coefficient $\beta_{ij}(c)$ summing to one, and the prior block enters with coefficient $1$. The regime in which Eq.~\eqref{eq:beta_weighted_precision} holds is therefore not a regime of the canonical functional unless one rescales the alignment integral by an additional global factor $A$, which is not introduced in the body. The "high-coupling" limit is reachable in the *adaptive* functional Eq.~\eqref{eq:free_energy_adaptive} via $\alpha_i \to 0$ (since $\alpha_i$ down-weights the prior block), but that connection is not made.
- Suggested fix: Either (i) rescale the canonical functional to expose an explicit coupling parameter $A$ and identify "$A \gg P$" with a specific regime of that parameter, or (ii) connect "alignment-dominated" to the small-$\alpha_i$ limit of the adaptive functional and observe that adaptive precision is exactly the mechanism by which the canonical functional reaches this regime, or (iii) drop the boxed approximation in favor of the unmodified fixed-point Eq.~\eqref{eq:sigma_fixed_point_beta}, which is what the canonical functional yields.

### 5. Positive-definiteness of the full mass matrix is asserted, not established
- Location: `sec:mass`, `sec:velocity_quadratic`, Eq.~\eqref{eq:full_kinetic} and surrounding text.
- Severity: MINOR
- Claim under review: The Hessian "$M$" is reused as the coefficient of a kinetic form $\mathcal{T} = \tfrac12 \dot\mu^\top M_{\mu\mu}\dot\mu$ and is interpreted as a Riemannian metric on velocities under the kinetic-metric postulate.
- Defect: For $M_{\mu\mu}$ to be a Riemannian metric, the *full block matrix* (diagonal + off-diagonal) must be positive-definite, not just at consensus equilibria but on the open neighborhood of states where the dynamics live. The diagonal blocks $[M_{\mu\mu}]_{ii} = \bar\Lambda_{p_i} + \sum_k \beta_{ik}\tilde\Lambda_{q_k} + \sum_j \beta_{ji}\Lambda_{q_i} + \Lambda_{o_i}$ are manifestly positive. The off-diagonal blocks $[M_{\mu\mu}]_{ik} = -\beta_{ik}\Omega_{ik}^{-T}\Lambda_{q_k} - \beta_{ki}\Lambda_{q_i}\Omega_{ki}^{-1}$ can in principle drag the global spectrum negative when attention is sharp ($\beta\to 1$ on a single edge) and the transported precisions are commensurate with the diagonal contributions. The manuscript does not exhibit a positivity argument (e.g.\ Gershgorin, diagonal dominance, or a Schur-complement bound). The asymmetric-attention caveat in `sec:mass_block_caveats` flags non-symmetry but not non-positivity.
- Suggested fix: Either (i) add a one-paragraph diagonal-dominance argument (the diagonal sum picks up $\bar\Lambda_p + \Lambda_o$ in addition to the alignment terms, so for sufficient sensory/prior precision the matrix is PD), with an explicit sufficient condition; or (ii) restrict the kinetic-postulate paragraph to the regime where $M_{\mu\mu}$ is verifiably PD (e.g.\ symmetric attention plus prior+observation precision dominating the off-diagonal mass), and note that outside that regime $M_{\mu\mu}$ is a degenerate symmetric form rather than a metric.

### 6. Envelope theorem invoked without strict-convexity / interior-optimum hypothesis
- Location: `sec:envelope_theorem`, Eq.~\eqref{eq:envelope_gradient}; appendix `app:conditional_uniqueness`, Eq.~\eqref{eq:envelope_app}.
- Severity: MINOR
- Claim under review: "the envelope theorem guarantees that the gradient of $\mathcal{F}_{\mathrm{red}}$ ... contains contributions from every term in the full functional in which $x_i$ appears" with the cross-term cancellation justified by $\partial\mathcal{F}/\partial\beta_{kl} = 0$ at $\beta^*$.
- Defect: The envelope theorem requires (i) the inner optimisation to have a *unique interior* maximiser at the parameter values of interest, and (ii) the value function to be differentiable in the parameter — both at the same point. For the entropy-regularised attention with $\tau > 0$ on the open simplex, both are satisfied (the inner objective in $\beta$ is strictly concave because of the $-\tau\sum\beta\log\beta$ term, the optimum is interior, and the value function is smooth). For $\tau \to 0$, however, the optimum collapses to a vertex and the envelope identity fails (the value function is only directionally differentiable). The manuscript writes "$\tau\to 0$" sharp-attention regimes as if the envelope formula still holds — see the "small $\tau$ together with $A \gg P$" regime in App.~`app:covariance_dynamics`, and the `\beta_{ij^\star}` selector limit. Strictly, gradient computations at $\tau=0$ require Danskin's theorem (subdifferential form), not the smooth envelope theorem. This is a presentation/scope deficiency rather than a load-bearing error.
- Suggested fix: Add a single sentence near Eq.~\eqref{eq:envelope_gradient} restricting the smooth envelope identity to $\tau > 0$ and noting that the $\tau\to 0$ limit must be taken either after differentiation or through Danskin's theorem on the active set. Cite a standard reference (Milgrom–Segal 2002 or Bonnans–Shapiro).

### 7. Dual cost identification implicitly conflates two different free-energy variables
- Location: Appendix `app:conditional_uniqueness`, "Dual Relation via the Envelope Theorem" (Eq.~\eqref{eq:envelope_app}) and "Dual Cost Identification".
- Severity: MINOR
- Claim under review: "the cost of increasing attention to $j$ equals the forward KL divergence between the updated belief $q_i^*$ and the transported neighbour $\Omega_{ij}q_j$".
- Defect: $F_i[\beta_i]$ is defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:F_i_app} as a $\min_{q_i}$ of the *integrand without entropy regulariser on $\beta$*, while the $\beta$-update in Eq.~\eqref{eq:J_i_app} optimises $\sum\beta C_{ij} + \tau\sum\beta\log\beta$, i.e.\ a *separate* functional with explicit $\beta$-entropy. The envelope identity $\partial F_i/\partial\beta_{ij}|_{q_i^*} = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_i^*\|\Omega_{ij}q_j)$ is correct for $F_i$, but it identifies $C_{ij}$ with a divergence *inside* the $\beta$-optimisation problem; the actual free energy minimised over $(\{q_i\},\{\beta_i\})$ jointly is $F_i + \tau\sum\beta\log\beta$, not $F_i$ alone. The presentation suggests these are the same object. This is harmless for the standard derivation but obscures what "the dual variable" actually is dual to.
- Suggested fix: State the two-stage variational structure explicitly: (i) given $\beta$, $q_i^*$ minimises $F_i[\beta_i]$ (envelope theorem applies, gives $C_{ij}$); (ii) given $\{C_{ij}\}$, $\beta_i^*$ minimises $\sum\beta C_{ij} + \tau\sum\beta\log\beta$ on the simplex (gives softmax). Then $C_{ij}$ is the dual variable to $\beta_{ij}$ in the second problem with $C$ frozen at $C_{ij} = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_i^*\|\Omega_{ij}q_j)$ from the first. This is what the boxed result is saying, but the current prose elides the two-stage structure.

### 8. Theorem on Gaussian closure of RG drops the $\xi$-marginalisation Gaussian-integration legitimacy assumption
- Location: Appendix `app:rigorous_rg`, Theorem~\ref{thm:rg_exact_closure}.
- Severity: MINOR
- Claim under review: "internal Gaussian integration produces another quadratic form in $Y$ with Schur-complement matrix ... plus the log-determinant of the constrained internal Hessian".
- Defect: The Gaussian Laplace integration $\int e^{-\mathcal{F}_s(Y,\xi)/\tau}d\xi$ on the constrained $\xi$-subspace requires that $A_{\xi\xi}$ be PD on that subspace (stated as "positive definite on the constrained internal subspace") *and* that the integrand be integrable, i.e.\ the integration domain extends to all of $\mathbb{R}^{\dim\xi^{\mathrm{constrained}}}$. The constraint $\sum_{i\in I}w_i^I\xi_i = 0$ restricts $\xi$ to a linear subspace of full measure, so this is fine. But the constrained Hessian determinant $\det' A_{\xi\xi}$ is the determinant of the restriction of $A_{\xi\xi}$ to the constraint surface, computed e.g.\ via $\det(P^\top A_{\xi\xi} P)$ for a basis $P$ of the constraint space; the prime in $\det'$ is not defined in the manuscript. Furthermore, the manuscript later uses "constrained internal gap" $m_I$ in Proposition~\ref{thm:rg_residual} without specifying whether the gap is the smallest eigenvalue of $A_{\xi\xi}$, of $P^\top A_{\xi\xi}P$, or of the projected Schur complement.
- Suggested fix: Define $\det' A_{\xi\xi}$ explicitly as $\det(P^\top A_{\xi\xi}P)$ for $P$ a basis of $\mathrm{ker}(\sum w_i^I\xi_i = 0)$, and tie $m_I$ to the smallest eigenvalue of the same restriction so the proposition is unambiguous.

### 9. "Sender contribution simplifies at consensus" remark is a key algebraic step but is asserted, not derived
- Location: `sec:mass`, "Remark on the consensus simplification" (after Eq.~\eqref{eq:mass_sigma_diagonal}).
- Severity: MINOR
- Claim under review: "At consensus ($\Sigma_j = \Omega_{ji}\Sigma_i\Omega_{ji}^\top$) this trace-term contribution simplifies to $+\Lambda_{q_i}\otimes\Lambda_{q_i}$".
- Defect: This is the algebraic step that delivers the clean factor $(1 + \sum\beta_{ik} + \sum\beta_{ji})$ in the boxed equation. The simplification follows from the identity $\partial^2 \mathrm{tr}((\Omega\Sigma\Omega^\top)^{-1}\Sigma_j)/\partial\Sigma^2 = ?$ at $\Sigma_j = \Omega\Sigma\Omega^\top$, which the manuscript states but does not derive. A reader has to redo the chain-rule computation through $\partial(\Sigma^{-1})/\partial\Sigma = -\Sigma^{-1}\otimes\Sigma^{-1}$ twice and then specialise to consensus to verify the cancellation.
- Suggested fix: Either supply the four-line derivation in an appendix or mark the simplification with a direct symbolic-verification reference (Mathematica, sympy notebook, etc.) so a reviewer can check the algebra without re-deriving it.

### 10. "Vanishing-holonomy theorem" is correctly proved but then over-extended in the prose
- Location: `sec:gauge_transport_arbitrary`, Theorem~\ref{thm:vanishing_holonomy} and following paragraph.
- Severity: MINOR
- Claim under review: "the holonomy around any closed loop vanishes: $H_{ijk} = \Omega_{ij}\Omega_{jk}\Omega_{ki} = I$. ... For our parameterisation ... the holonomy vanishes for every closed loop regardless of the learned gauge frames $\phi_i$."
- Defect: $H_{ijk}$ as written is the holonomy around a *triangle on the agent index graph*, not around a *base-manifold loop* in $\mathcal{C}$. These are different objects: a base-manifold loop holonomy is the path-ordered exponential of the connection one-form $A_\mu = U^{-1}\partial_\mu U$ around a closed curve in $\mathcal{C}$. For a single-valued $U_i(c)$, this is also zero by the Maurer–Cartan identity (already noted in the section). But the prose elides the distinction, writing "the gauge bundle is automatically flat" as a consequence of the index-triangle calculation. The flatness conclusion follows from the *single-valuedness* of $U_i(c)$, not from the cocycle identity on the index graph; the cocycle identity is a vacuous consequence of the same single-valuedness.
- Suggested fix: Distinguish the two holonomies in one sentence: (a) on the agent-index graph, $\Omega_{ij}\Omega_{jk}\Omega_{ki} = I$ by direct algebra; (b) on the base manifold, the connection $A_\mu^{(i)} = U_i^{-1}\partial_\mu U_i$ is pure gauge for any single-valued $U_i$, hence $F_{\mu\nu}^{(i)} = 0$ and base-loop holonomies vanish. State explicitly that the two facts have the same root cause (single-valuedness of $U_i$) but are not the same identity.

### 11. Thin-SVD factorisation $M_h = (U\Lambda^{1/2})(V\Lambda^{1/2})^\top$ is well-defined but the gauge-content-vs-isometric-content claim is not proved
- Location: `sec:dot_product_derivation`, Eq.~\eqref{eq:thin_svd_decomposition}–\eqref{eq:low_rank_lift}.
- Severity: MINOR
- Claim under review: "The gauge-theoretic identification $\sigma^{-2}\Omega^{-\top} \leftrightarrow M_h$ operates at the level of the invertible head-space factor; the isometric columns of $U_Q, U_K$ select the per-head image subspaces and carry no gauge content."
- Defect: The claim that $U_Q, U_K$ "carry no gauge content" is plausible for the gauge group $\mathrm{GL}(d_{\mathrm{head}})$ acting on the head subspace, but it is asserted without explicit verification of how the simultaneous-pushforward gauge action of Theorem~\ref{thm:glk_invariance} interacts with the embedding $\mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{head}}} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^{d_{\mathrm{model}}}$. In particular, a $\mathrm{GL}(d_{\mathrm{model}})$ pushforward acting on the ambient $\mu$ would mix the head subspaces; the claim that the multi-head architecture realises only the block-diagonal subgroup $\mathrm{GL}(d_{\mathrm{head}})^H$ is correct but the statement that $U_Q, U_K$ themselves are "non-gauge" requires fixing the residual $\mathrm{O}(d_{\mathrm{head}})$ ambiguity in the SVD as part of the gauge-fixing prescription (which the manuscript notes only as "residual orthogonal freedom ... that learning fills in during training", not as a gauge slice).
- Suggested fix: Add a sentence identifying the residual $\mathrm{O}(d_{\mathrm{head}})$ as the gauge-fixing freedom that the gradient-descent training implicitly resolves, and clarify that the "no gauge content" claim is up to this orthogonal slice.

### 12. Dirac-limit observation-to-environmental-agent identification is handled but the step beyond cross-entropy substitution is over-claimed
- Location: `sec:framework`, "Eliminating External Observations", paragraph following Eq.\ giving $\partial_{\Sigma_i}\mathrm{KL}(q_i\|q_{e_k})$.
- Severity: MINOR
- Claim under review: "Environmental agents therefore reproduce observation dynamics at the mean-gradient level for any $\Sigma_o > 0$, while full variational equivalence requires either the cross-entropy substitution above or restriction to fixed-covariance dynamics".
- Defect: The cross-entropy substitution $-\mathbb{E}_{q_i}[\log q_{e_k}]$ is no longer an $f$-divergence and breaks the appendix's conditional-uniqueness justification for using forward KL throughout. The manuscript invokes both: forward KL for inter-agent coupling (justified by the appendix), and cross-entropy for observation coupling (justified by the equivalence calculation). These are *two different couplings* in the same functional, and the appendix's argument does not cover the cross-entropy form. The "self-contained framework" claim — that observation terms are absorbed into agent-agent KL couplings — therefore holds only at the mean-gradient level, not at the level of the canonical functional.
- Suggested fix: State explicitly that the canonical functional retains the original observation log-likelihood term and that the environmental-agent identification is a parsimony argument at the mean-gradient level only. Avoid the suggestion that the framework "is" self-contained at the level of the full variational structure.

### 14. State-dependent precision optimum has an undocumented domain restriction
- Location: `sec:state_dependent_precision`, Eq.~\eqref{eq:state_dependent_alpha}.
- Severity: NIT
- Claim under review: "$\alpha_i^*(c) = c_0/[b_0 + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_i(c)\|p_i(c))]$".
- Defect: The closed-form solution comes from setting $D_{\mathrm{KL}} + b_0 - c_0/\alpha_i = 0$ and solving for $\alpha_i$. The result is positive iff $b_0 + D_{\mathrm{KL}} > 0$, which holds for $b_0 > 0$. But the *second-order* condition for this stationary point to be a *minimum* of $\mathcal{F}_i^{\mathrm{adaptive}}$ in $\alpha_i$ is $\partial^2 R/\partial\alpha_i^2 > 0$, i.e.\ $c_0/\alpha_i^2 > 0$, which is automatic. So the optimum is a minimum. However, the domain $\alpha_i > 0$ requires $\log\alpha_i$ to be defined and finite; the regulariser is a *barrier* not a *penalty*, and the closed-form solution is only meaningful when $b_0 > 0$ to bound $\alpha_i$ from above. The text correctly states $b_0 > 0, c_0 > 0$ but does not explicitly verify the second-order condition.
- Suggested fix: One-line check that $\alpha_i^*$ is a minimum (not just a stationary point) of the regularised functional.

**Verdict on math-rigor axis: Major Revision.**

The Theory and Mass sections are mathematically careful in the load-bearing computations (KL invariance, GL-correct precision transport, off-diagonal mean Hessian block, sender vs receiver decomposition for the mean gradient, envelope-versus-autograd distinction). The manuscript already self-acknowledges most of the structural caveats (kinetic-metric postulate, asymmetric-attention non-conservativity, augmented-model framing as motivational, exponential map non-surjectivity, conditional-not-unconditional uniqueness). The three MAJOR findings are not knockouts of the framework — each can be patched with a paragraph of additional argument or a clarified hypothesis — but they are load-bearing enough that the current text states more than it proves. The richness-lemma issue (#1) in particular converts a strong-sounding "uniqueness" result into a "uniqueness on attainable ratios" result whose width must be argued separately; the mean-field factorisation issue (#2) clarifies that the softmax form is an architectural commitment rather than a derivation; the cross-block omission (#3) is straightforward to fix but currently leaves the boxed result not fully justified. Recommended action: revise to address findings 1–3 with explicit derivations, and propagate the minor presentation fixes throughout.
