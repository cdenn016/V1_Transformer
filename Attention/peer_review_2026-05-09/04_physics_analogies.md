## FINDINGS

### 1. Action-principle table conflates inference with dynamics; units are not commensurable

- **Location:** lines ~1397–1400 (Section "Comparison to Physical Action Principles"); table preceded by paragraph at line 1402.
- **Severity:** MAJOR
- **Quoted claim:** Table places `Classical Mechanics ($S = \int L\,dt$)`, `General Relativity ($S = \int R\sqrt{|g|}\,d^4x$)`, `Gauge Theory ($S = -\tfrac14 \int F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}\,d^4x$)`, and `Our Framework ($\mathcal{F}[\{q\},\{p\},\{\phi\}]$)` side-by-side, all annotated `$\delta S = 0$` / `$\delta\mathcal{F} = 0$`. The accompanying prose states that "Like physical action principles, our free energy functional is a scalar... extremized by dynamics... and contains kinetic-like (alignment) and potential-like (self-consistency) terms."
- **Defect:** The three physical entries in the column are actions for *matter and geometry dynamics*: trajectories, metrics, and gauge fields are the dynamical variables, and the action's stationarity gives the Euler–Lagrange / Einstein / Yang–Mills field equations. They have units (Joule·second; SI dimensions $[M\,L^2\,T^{-1}]$ for $S$), and the constants $G, c, \hbar$ that set their normalisation are physical. The framework's $\mathcal{F}$ is a *KL divergence functional* in nats — dimensionless. It governs *parameter inference* (variational posterior selection), not the dynamics of physical degrees of freedom evolving in spacetime. The "kinetic-like (alignment)" and "potential-like (self-consistency)" labels are interpretive overlays. The KL term $\mathrm{KL}(q_i \| p_i)$ is a divergence (a relative entropy), not a kinetic energy in any sense the physics literature would recognise: it has no time derivative, no inverse-mass coefficient, and it vanishes at $q=p$ rather than at $\dot q = 0$. The table thus juxtaposes objects of incommensurate physical and mathematical type.
- The further claim of "Euler–Lagrange equations from $\delta\mathcal{F} = 0$" (line ~17 of `sec_pullback2.txt`, paragraph "Action principle structure") is also not Euler–Lagrange: stationarity of $\mathcal{F}$ over Gaussian parameters yields *gradient flow* (first-order in the variational parameter, no second time derivative), not the second-order EL equations characteristic of an action principle for matter. The manuscript itself notes the overdamped/first-order character at line 9 of `sec_grav.txt` ("standard treatments... employ first-order gradient descent... overdamped limit"). EL equations from a Lagrangian are second-order; gradient flow is not Hamilton's principle.
- **Suggested fix:** Either (a) demote the table to "structural analogy (variational stationarity)" and remove the side-by-side juxtaposition with $S_{\rm GR}$ and $S_{\rm YM}$, explicitly stating that the entries differ in dimension, dynamical variable, and order of resulting equation; or (b) construct a genuine Lagrangian $L = T - V$ on the configuration space with a kinetic term independent of the potential — the manuscript already concedes at lines 135 and 315–319 that the kinetic-metric postulate is not derived from $\mathcal{F}$, so option (b) is not currently available. Adopt (a). The "Euler–Lagrange" label in the prose at line 17 of `sec_pullback2.txt` should be replaced by "natural-gradient flow stationarity condition" — the manuscript's own kinetic-postulate paragraph (line 315 of `sec_pullback.txt`) makes this honest.

### 2. Wilson observable is only nontrivial in Regime II; manuscript's own Regime I implementation has identically vanishing curvature

- **Location:** Section `sec:discrete_regime_ii` (Wilson observable at lines ~54–87 of `sec_wilson.txt`; Regime I/II distinction at lines 5–7); also the gauge-curvature linguistic conjecture (Section `sec:gauge_curvature_conjecture`, line 145 of `sec_grav.txt`).
- **Severity:** MAJOR (but partly self-disclosed)
- **Quoted claim:** "$W_{ijk} := \operatorname{Re}\operatorname{Tr}(H_{ijk}) = \operatorname{Re}\operatorname{Tr}\big[\exp(\delta_{ij}\cdot G) \exp(\delta_{jk}\cdot G) \exp(\delta_{ki}\cdot G)\big]$ is the gauge-invariant Wilson observable" (Eq. `eq:wilson_observable`); "we propose a potentially falsifiable conjecture: in the Regime II extension, language is a gauge theory and linguistic evolution is driven by minimization of gauge field curvature."
- **Defect:** The Wilson loop in lattice gauge theory (Wilson 1974; Kogut–Susskind 1975) is a trace of an ordered product of independent link variables $U_\ell \in G$ associated to *unconstrained* connection links. Its content as a measure of holonomy comes from the fact that $U_\ell$ are free dynamical variables, not derived from a vertex potential. In the Regime I parameterisation actually implemented in this manuscript, $\Omega_{ij} = U_iU_j^{-1}$, and the chained product around any loop telescopes ($U_jU_j^{-1}=I$), giving $H_{ijk} \equiv I$ and $W_{ijk} \equiv K$ identically. The "Wilson observable" is a constant in the implemented theory; calling it a Wilson observable is formally correct only after the (currently non-implemented) Regime II edge variable $\delta_{ij}$ is introduced and given independent dynamics. The manuscript flags this (line 7 of `sec_wilson.txt`: "the curvature $F^{(i)}_{\mu\nu}$ vanishes identically"; line 145 of `sec_grav.txt`: "the conjecture is therefore a conditional prediction"), but elsewhere — most damagingly in the discussion at the top of Section `sec:gauge_curvature_conjecture` — the conditional is allowed to slip ("language is a gauge theory and linguistic evolution is driven by minimization of gauge field curvature" reads as a categorical claim before the Regime II conditional is invoked).
- A second concern: even granting Regime II, the proposed Wilson action $S_{\rm YM}[\delta] = \beta \sum (1 - K^{-1} W_{ijk})$ is a regulariser on a *learned attention graph*, not a quantum or classical action for a gauge field over spacetime. There is no spacetime measure $d^4x$, no field-strength $F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}$ contracted with a metric, no coupling constant with units of inverse length squared. Calling this "the Yang–Mills action" (line 67 of `sec_wilson.txt`, Eq. `eq:wilson_action`) overstates the parallel: it is a graph regulariser inspired by the Wilson plaquette action, not a Yang–Mills action.
- **Suggested fix:** Restate the conjecture (and any prose calling the framework "a gauge theory of language") as strictly conditional on the not-yet-implemented Regime II promotion. Rename `S_YM` to something like `S_{\rm Wilson-reg}` or `S_{\rm holonomy-penalty}` to avoid implying a Yang–Mills field theory. Either provide measurements of $W_{ijk}$ in a bidirectional model (where Regime II content is dynamically active) demonstrating non-trivial values and a correlation with linguistic-evolution proxies, or move the conjecture to a "future work" section rather than presenting it as the strongest testable prediction.

### 3. Lorentzian signature derivation is algebraic, with the signature put in by hand

- **Location:** Section `sec:signature_resolution` (lines ~110–195 of `sec_pullback.txt`), particularly the worked example (Eq. `eq:complex_gauge_frame` line 165, and Eq. `eq:lorentzian_metric` line 180). Also `sec:signature_alternative_route` (lines 197–220).
- **Severity:** MAJOR (extensively self-disclosed but the disclosure does not undo the underlying issue)
- **Quoted claim:** "We now \emph{postulate} that the temporal component is imaginary: $\phi(\tau, x) = i\psi_\tau(\tau, x) \cdot T + \psi_x(\tau, x) \cdot T$. This is the assignment that drives the signature in the calculation that follows; it is not derived." Followed by computation yielding $G_{\tau\tau} = -2(\partial_\tau\psi_\tau)^2$, $G_{xx} = +2(\partial_x\psi_x)^2$ and the boxed Lorentzian metric `eq:lorentzian_metric`.
- **Defect:** The construction is Wick rotation performed inside the Lie-algebra coordinate, not a derivation of Lorentzian signature from variational dynamics. There is no physical principle stated that selects $\phi_\tau \in i\mathbb{R}$ over $\phi_\tau \in \mathbb{R}$. The "real-part projection" $G^{\rm Lor}_{\mu\nu} := \mathrm{Re}(G_{\mu\nu})$ is a second independent input: $G_{\tau x} = i(\partial_\tau\psi_\tau)(\partial_x\psi_x)\mathrm{tr}(T^2)$ is purely imaginary, and discarding it leaves no physical justification — there is no measurement-theoretic prescription, no Hermitian-conjugation argument, no reality structure derived from a Lagrangian. This is two postulates (imaginary frame + real-part projection) applied to a $K=2$ linear example; the dimension count $1+3$ vs $2+2$ is then a third independent postulate (the manuscript notes "the construction does not currently distinguish $1+3$ from $2+2$ on dynamical grounds" at line 191 of `sec_pullback.txt`).
- The construction is also not the Wick rotation in the standard physicist's sense. Wick rotation in QFT is a *coordinate-level* analytic continuation $t \to -i\tau$ on the time coordinate of spacetime, used to make path integrals convergent; reverting it recovers Minkowski Green's functions. Here the rotation is performed *on a Lie-algebra component of an internal frame field*, not on a coordinate. The two operations are formally different: one acts on the base manifold, one on a fiber. The manuscript acknowledges this distinction (line 157 of `sec_pullback.txt`: "Where the standard physicist's prescription continues $\tau \to i\tau$ on the base manifold, the construction below continues $\phi_\tau \to i\phi_\tau$ in the Lie algebra"), but does not establish that the two operations are physically equivalent — they are not, in general; one can perform a Wick rotation on a coordinate without complexifying any internal symmetry, and conversely.
- The `sec:signature_alternative_route` route (causal cone from finite-speed propagation, lines 197–220) is more honest physically — it postulates a finite information speed and obtains a Lorentzian conformal class — but the manuscript correctly flags (line 217) that the framework's first-order natural-gradient dynamics is *parabolic* and hence has *infinite* signal speed, contradicting the finite-speed postulate. So this route is currently inconsistent with the implementation; making it consistent requires a hyperbolic second-order epistemic dynamics that does not exist in the manuscript. Even if one had finite-speed dynamics, the construction selects only the conformal class, not a metric, and does not pick the dimension.
- **Suggested fix:** The existing self-disclosures (lines 157, 183, 195) are, on the whole, adequate. Strengthen the section title and headline statement: replace "Lorentzian signature from $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ gauge frames" with "Algebraic compatibility of Lorentzian signature with $\mathrm{GL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ gauge frames under two postulates", and remove any prose elsewhere in the manuscript that cites this section as a "derivation" of Lorentzian structure. The summary box at line 88 of `sec_pullback2.txt` ("What we postulate: ... this signature is conditional on additional postulates ... and is not derived from variational free-energy minimization in the present formulation") is the right tone; ensure the abstract and introduction do not overclaim relative to it.

### 4. "(3+1) dimensions" argument is numerology — three independent hypotheses with no derivation

- **Location:** Section `sec:observable_sectors` and subsection "Why Exactly $(3+1)$ Dimensions?" (line 355 of `sec_pullback.txt`, `sec_pullback.txt` line 359).
- **Severity:** MAJOR
- **Quoted claim:** "For human cognitive agents, we hypothesize that $|\mathcal{D}_{\text{obs}}| = 4$, comprising one temporal and three spatial directions. This dimensional structure could arise through several mechanisms. Neural architecture constraints may limit human cortical representations to 4D manifolds due to developmental or anatomical factors. Alternatively, our sensory modalities may sample only 4D environmental structure... Finally, evolutionary optimization may have selected against processing higher-dimensional belief variations..."
- **Defect:** No derivation is offered; three speculative biological mechanisms are listed and none is developed. The eigenvalue-hierarchy framework establishes only that *some* number of large eigenvalues is observable. It does not predict 4, it does not predict 1+3 over (say) 2+2 or 0+4, and the manuscript explicitly admits this at line 359: "The framework does not explain why exactly three spatial dimensions rather than two or four... it makes no quantitative predictions about measured spacetime properties that could be tested experimentally." Given this admission, the section title "*Why Exactly* (3+1) Dimensions?" is misleading: the section explains nothing of the kind — it postulates that the answer is 4 and notes that the framework is consistent with that postulate but does not select it. The header should reflect the content.
- The (3+1) hypothesis is also independent of the temporal-direction selection (which itself depends on the imaginary $\phi_\tau$ postulate of finding 3 above) and the spatial-direction count (a separate postulate noted at line 88 of `sec_pullback2.txt`: "We postulate that the observable sector has exactly 3 large spatial eigenvalues"). So "(3+1)" is at least three independent inputs to the framework, not one derived prediction.
- **Suggested fix:** Rename the subsection "Hypothesised (3+1) Structure" or "Conjectured (3+1) Structure"; remove any rhetoric suggesting the framework derives or explains the dimensionality. The text at line 359 already does the right work; the header and surrounding prose should be brought into agreement with it.

### 5. "Rocks have large effective mass" is dimensional analysis without physical content

- **Location:** Section `sec:mass`, particularly line 310 of `sec_pullback.txt` ("A macroscopic object maintains extraordinarily precise self-localization..."), and `sec:macroscopic_objects` (lines ~57–75 of `sec_grav.txt`).
- **Severity:** MAJOR
- **Quoted claim 1 (line 310):** "A macroscopic object maintains extraordinarily precise self-localization: $\bar{\Sigma}_{\mathrm{rock}} \approx \epsilon I$ with $\epsilon \ll 1$ a small dimensionless precision (we make no claim that $\epsilon$ corresponds to specific physical units; cf.\ the dimensional caveat in Section~\ref{sec:scope_limitations}) yields a large effective stiffness $M_{\mathrm{rock}} \sim \epsilon^{-1}$ in the Newtonian-analogy reading of Section~\ref{sec:mass}. Rocks are certain, thus exhibit large second-variation rigidity, thus are hard to move under the dynamics."
- **Quoted claim 2 (line 64 of `sec_grav.txt`):** "A rock maintains extraordinarily precise self-localization with prior covariance $\Sigma_{\mathrm{rock}} \approx \epsilon I$ where $\epsilon \sim \ell_P^2$ approaches the Planck scale."
- **Defect:** The two claims are inconsistent: line 310 explicitly disclaims any units for $\epsilon$ ("dimensionless precision... no claim that $\epsilon$ corresponds to specific physical units"), while line 64 of `sec_grav.txt` *assigns* $\epsilon$ the dimensions of length squared and the magnitude of the Planck length squared ($\ell_P^2 \approx 2.6\times 10^{-70}\ \rm m^2$). These cannot both be true: either $\epsilon$ is dimensionless and the comparison $\epsilon \sim \ell_P^2$ is meaningless, or $\epsilon$ has dimensions, in which case line 310's caveat is wrong and the "framework's effective mass" matrix $\Sigma_p^{-1}$ acquires units of inverse area, which contradicts the stated dimensionlessness of the Hessian elsewhere. The Planck-scale identification $\epsilon \sim \ell_P^2$ has no derivation: it is a numerical guess, not a physical prediction. There is no calculation showing why a rock's prior precision would localise to $\ell_P^{-2}$ rather than to $\hbar^2/(m_{\rm rock}\,\Delta x_{\rm rock})^2$ from a quantum-mechanical Heisenberg estimate, or to any other scale.
- The "rocks are hard to move because they are certain" reading reduces to: $\omega^2 \propto 1/m \propto \Sigma$, so small $\Sigma$ gives large $1/\omega^2$ and slow response. This is correct *within the framework's harmonic-oscillator analogy after the kinetic-metric postulate of Section `sec:velocity_quadratic` is in place*, but the manuscript itself flags (line 315 of `sec_pullback.txt`) that the postulate "would be vacated by any other choice of kinetic metric." So the rock claim is: under our chosen kinetic metric, sharp-prior agents respond slowly. This is dimensional analysis on a parameter $\Sigma_p^{-1}$ of unspecified units, with no physical content equating that response time to a kilogram-scale inertial mass.
- **Suggested fix:** Resolve the dimensional inconsistency between line 310 and line 64 of `sec_grav.txt`. Either drop the Planck-scale identification entirely (the more honest option, given the framework's stated dimensionlessness), or commit to a unit assignment for $\epsilon$ and provide a derivation (or at minimum an order-of-magnitude argument distinguishing $\ell_P^2$ from competing scales). State plainly that "rocks are hard to move" reduces to "$\omega^2 \propto 1/m$ under the stated kinetic-metric postulate", with no implication that the framework's "mass" is the physicist's inertial mass.

### 6. "Gravitational effects in pullback geometry" derives no GR content (no geodesic deviation, no equivalence principle, no field equations)

- **Location:** Section "Inertial Mass and Gravitational Effects in Pullback Geometry" (lines 19–48 of `sec_grav.txt`).
- **Severity:** MAJOR (partly self-disclosed)
- **Quoted claim:** "If gravitational effects emerge, they would manifest as curvature in this pullback geometry." Followed by the ansatz $R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}^{(i)}[g^{(i)}] \propto \text{functions of } \Sigma_{p,i}^{-1}$ (Eq. `eq:gravity_curvature_ansatz`).
- **Defect:** The section is honest at line 44 ("We do not claim the equivalence principle follows as a consequence of the present framework. The induced curvature tensors in the pullback metrics have not been computed, no analog of the Einstein field equations has been derived, and the identification of the scalar mass $m_{\text{eff},i}$ with a gravitational coupling has not been carried out beyond dimensional matching"). But the section title "Inertial Mass and Gravitational Effects" and the prose at line 39 ("the framework's intersubjective analog of objective spacetime") are softer than the disclosure warrants. To deliver any GR content the framework would need at minimum: (i) a derivation that the pullback metric satisfies the second Bianchi identity (it must, as the pullback of a metric, but this needs explicit verification of smoothness conditions); (ii) a stress-energy tensor sourcing the curvature; (iii) a derivation of geodesic deviation in $g^{(i)}$ (Jacobi equation) showing it reduces to Newtonian tidal forces in some limit; (iv) a derivation of the equivalence principle (test particles follow $g^{(i)}$-geodesics independent of internal structure). None of these is even sketched. The manuscript supplies an *ansatz* that curvature is some function of prior precision (Eq. `eq:gravity_curvature_ansatz`); ansätze are not derivations, and "$\propto$ functions of" is not a relation that constrains anything.
- The equivalence-principle paragraph (line 37) is stated in the conditional mood appropriately ("would, if it could be derived, be consistent with the framework"), but the section header should not promise "Gravitational Effects" when the content is "the metric exists and could in principle be curved."
- **Suggested fix:** Rename the section "Toward a Pullback-Curvature Reading of Gravity" or similar. Either compute one example pullback curvature explicitly (e.g., for the Gaussian-on-$\mathbb{R}^2$ example of line 92 of `sec_pullback.txt`, evaluate the Riemann tensor of the conformal metric $g^{(i)}_{\mu\nu} = \sigma_i^{-2}\delta_{\mu\nu}$ and exhibit it as a function of $\sigma_i$) and discuss whether the resulting curvature behaves like a gravitational field, or remove the gravitational reading from the headline and present it strictly as a research direction.

### 7. "Quantum systems: pre-consensus dynamics" is metaphor, not bridge to QM

- **Location:** Section "Quantum Systems: Pre-Consensus Dynamics" (lines 77–98 of `sec_grav.txt`).
- **Severity:** MAJOR (self-disclosed)
- **Quoted claim:** "Quantum systems, in this reading, represent the opposite regime from the macroscopic-object case where agents have not yet established consensus... What physics calls 'wavefunction collapse' is modelled by the transition from pre-consensus (perspectival, observer-dependent) to post-consensus (shared, objective) physics... Our framework suggests a potential resolution to the measurement problem."
- **Defect:** The section's own opening paragraph (line 79) admits the gap: "the actual derivation of wavefunction collapse from classical Bayesian agents requires extending the framework to include quantum superposition states, which we have not done. Treat the following as a motivational reading awaiting a quantum extension rather than a derivation of measurement." Given that admission, the framing as a "potential resolution to the measurement problem" is overreach. The manuscript operates with classical Gaussian probability distributions throughout (acknowledged at line 79 and at line 138 of `sec_wilson.txt`: "extending to a strictly separate complex/quantum fiber with complex-valued amplitudes is a separate program not adopted in the present manuscript"). Without superposition, there is no Born rule, no non-commuting observables, no entanglement, no Bell-inequality violation, no Wigner-function negativity — none of the structure that distinguishes quantum from classical probability. Calling the agreement-formation dynamics among classical Gaussian agents a "resolution to the measurement problem" misappropriates the term: the measurement problem is precisely the question of *why classical-looking outcomes emerge from non-classical superposition states*, and a framework with no superposition cannot, by construction, address it.
- The relational/QBist parallel (lines 108–112 of `sec_grav.txt`) is also too generous: relational QM and QBism are rigorously formulated *within* quantum mechanics with full Hilbert-space machinery; the present framework offers a classical-probability analog of their *conceptual stance* on observer-dependence, not the formalism. The text at line 109 partially acknowledges this ("The model is compatible with the relational reading; it does not establish that physical laws themselves are observer-dependent"), but the section header "Quantum Systems" and the "potential resolution to the measurement problem" claim outrun the disclaimer.
- **Suggested fix:** Rename to "An Inferential-Consensus Analogy for Measurement"; remove "potential resolution to the measurement problem"; replace with "an interpretive picture awaiting quantum extension." Move the section to after the explicit statement (already in the manuscript) that the framework does not handle quantum amplitudes.

### 9. Yang–Mills sign convention and gauge-noninvariance of $\mathrm{tr}(A_\mu A_\nu)$

- **Location:** Eq. `eq:horizontal_metric` and surrounding paragraphs (lines 41–51 of `sec_pullback.txt`); Section `sec:consensus_metric` (lines 256–274 of `sec_pullback.txt`).
- **Severity:** MAJOR (largely self-disclosed)
- **Quoted claim:** "$g_{\mathcal{C},\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{tw}}(c) := \kappa\big(A^{(i)}_\mu(c), A^{(i)}_\nu(c)\big)$" with the bilinear form $\kappa(A,B) = \mp\mathrm{tr}(AB)$ depending on compact vs non-compact case. Followed by gauge-invariance disclosure at line 72: "Under a local gauge transformation $U_i \to U_i g(c)$... $\kappa(A_\mu, A_\nu)$ is therefore not invariant... The horizontal block is consequently agent-frame-dependent."
- **Defect:** The horizontal block of the manuscript's bundle metric is *not gauge invariant* under local gauge transformations (correctly noted by the manuscript). In Yang–Mills theory the gauge-invariant local quantity built from the connection is $\mathrm{tr}(F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu})$, which the manuscript correctly distinguishes from $\mathrm{tr}(A_\mu A_\nu)$ and even labels the latter "tw" rather than "YM" to keep them distinct. However, in Regime I, $F_{\mu\nu}\equiv 0$, so $\mathrm{tr}(FF)$ supplies no escape hatch (manuscript at line 73). The proposed Haar-average rescue (Section `sec:consensus_metric`, lines 256–265) is correctly criticised in the same section: for constant $g$ the trace is already invariant by cyclicity, so the averaging is trivial; for local $g(c)$ the gauge-orbit average is an infinite-dimensional functional integral requiring a regulator that the manuscript does not construct; and the non-compact $\mathrm{SO}(1,3)$ case has infinite Haar measure even for constant $g$.
- The net status: the horizontal contribution to the framework's "induced metric" is not a gauge-invariant geometric object, and there is no finite construction in the manuscript that makes it one. The "consensus metric" is acknowledged (line 274 of `sec_pullback.txt`) to be "a heuristic target rather than a finite gauge-invariant observable."
- The Yang–Mills *sign* in $-\frac14 F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}$ (compact group, mostly-plus or mostly-minus convention dependent) is irrelevant to the manuscript's construction since $F=0$, but the choice of $\mp\mathrm{tr}$ for $\kappa$ on the Lie algebra controls the *signature* of the horizontal block and is therefore a physically loaded postulate (manuscript correctly flags this at line 159 of `sec_pullback.txt` in the worked example).
- **Suggested fix:** The disclosures are good. Strengthen the framing in the abstract / introduction: any passage describing the framework's "induced metric" as a frame-independent geometric object should be qualified to "agent-frame-dependent until a regulator on the gauge-orbit average is supplied". Drop any prose elsewhere that treats $\mathrm{tr}(A_\mu A_\nu)$ as a Yang–Mills invariant.

### 11. "Conformal class" terminology used loosely in the causal-cone construction

- **Location:** Section `sec:causal_cone_route` "Conformal-class ambiguity" paragraph (line 213 of `sec_pullback.txt`).
- **Severity:** MINOR
- **Quoted claim:** "The cone determines $g$ only up to a positive conformal factor: $g$ and $\lambda g$ for any smooth positive function $\lambda$ have identical null sets. The construction therefore establishes a Lorentzian conformal class $[g]$, not a unique metric."
- **Defect:** The terminology is correct — a Lorentzian conformal class is precisely the equivalence class of metrics related by positive conformal rescalings, equivalently the data of the null cone. But the manuscript does not draw out the consequence: a conformal class fixes the causal structure but not the metric; in particular, *no length is selected*. Without a length (i.e., a representative of the class), no proper-time interval is defined, no clock rate can be calibrated, no comparison with relativistic time dilation is possible. The manuscript is correct that "selecting a representative requires fixing the overall scale, equivalent to a choice of unit for the information speed $c_{\mathcal{I}}$", but should also note that without that choice the construction yields no quantitative kinematics, only a causal partial ordering.
- **Suggested fix:** Add a sentence to line 214: "Until a representative of $[g]$ is selected (equivalently, until $c_{\mathcal{I}}$ is calibrated to a measurable speed), the construction supplies a causal structure but no metric kinematics: proper time, time dilation, and length contraction are not defined."

- **Severity:** MINOR (largely self-disclosed in the same paragraph)
- **Quoted claim:** "We identify $\mathrm{tr}(\mathcal{M})$ with the effective mass quantifying belief rigidity... Within the framework, where information is primal..., the Fisher information takes the structural role that inertial mass plays in classical mechanics."
- **Defect:** Standard Fisher information $\mathcal{M}_{ij}[q] = \mathbb{E}_q[(\partial_i \log q)(\partial_j \log q)]$ has dimensions of (1 / parameter)² in each index — it depends on the parameterisation. For Gaussian mean parameters in $\mathbb{R}^K$, the Fisher matrix is $\Sigma^{-1}$, with units of (1 / position)². The identification of this with inertial mass (units kg = $M$) requires a conversion constant with units kg·position². No such constant is defined in the manuscript; the empirical $R^2 = 0.9998$ scaling $\omega^2 \propto 1/M_{\rm eff}$ is a within-framework numerical observation, not a derivation that physical mass equals $\mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_p^{-1})$.
- The paragraph's own concession at line 11 is correct: "What remains open is not whether mass *is* statistical precision (within the framework, it is, by construction) but whether the specific mass values of physical particles ($m_e$, $m_p$, etc.) can be derived as information-geometric invariants." The phrase "by construction" honestly admits that the identification is definitional within the framework, not an empirical bridge to physics.
- **Suggested fix:** When the manuscript invokes "mass" in physics-bridging contexts (rocks, gravity, equivalence principle), explicitly carry the disclaimer "framework-internal effective mass; not equated to physical inertial mass". The current text does this in some places (e.g., line 310 of `sec_pullback.txt`) but slips elsewhere.

### 15. "Equivalence principle would be consistent with the framework" — the equivalence principle is not "consistency", it is empirical content

- **Location:** Line 37 of `sec_grav.txt`.
- **Severity:** MINOR
- **Quoted claim:** "Under this identification, the equivalence principle would, if it could be derived, be consistent with the framework: inertial and gravitational mass would share a single origin in prior precision because both would be properties of the agent's experienced geometry, not of some external 'true' spacetime."
- **Defect:** The weak equivalence principle is the empirical claim that in a freely falling frame, gravity is locally indistinguishable from acceleration; it is tested at the $10^{-15}$ level by torsion balances and lunar laser ranging. The Einstein equivalence principle adds local Lorentz invariance and local position invariance. None of these are properties of "consistency"; they are empirical postulates whose violation is a measurable consequence. The framework's claim "inertial and gravitational mass would share a single origin in prior precision" requires (i) deriving inertial mass from prior precision (currently only the framework's effective mass is identified with $\Sigma_p^{-1}$, with no kg conversion), and (ii) deriving gravitational mass (which couples to the Einstein tensor in $G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G T_{\mu\nu}$) from the same precision, which requires a stress-energy tensor and field equations the manuscript does not provide. The conditional ("if it could be derived") is appropriate but the framing as "consistency" understates the gap.
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "would be consistent with the framework" with "would be a derived consequence of the framework, *if* (i) inertial mass and (ii) gravitational mass could each be identified with prior precision via independent dimensional-conversion arguments and a sourced field equation, neither of which is constructed here."

## SUMMARY

The manuscript is, in substantial portions, *self-aware* about the gap between its physics analogies and physics proper. The Lorentzian-signature section (`sec:signature_resolution`), the macroscopic-objects section, the gravitational-effects section, and the quantum-systems section all contain explicit disclaimers that the framework does not derive the relevant physical content. This is to the manuscript's credit and is unusual in the "information-as-fundamental" literature.

However, the disclaimers are unevenly distributed: section headers, abstract-level claims, and certain rhetorical passages systematically *outrun* the disclosures buried in the body. A reader skimming will come away with the impression that the framework has derived Lorentzian signature (it has not — finding 3), explained (3+1) dimensions (it has not — finding 4), identified physical mass with prior precision (it has not, beyond a framework-internal definitional move with no kg conversion — findings 5, 12), demonstrated gravitational effects (it has not — finding 6), and resolved the measurement problem (it has not, lacking superposition — finding 7). The action-principle table (finding 1) juxtaposes incommensurable objects and is the most damaging single piece of overstatement, because it is exactly where a non-specialist reader will look for the headline claim of unification.

On the technical physics side: the Wilson-observable construction (finding 2) is formally correct as lattice-gauge-theory inspired, but the implemented theory is in Regime I with $F_{\mu\nu}\equiv 0$ and $W_{ijk}\equiv K$ identically, so the "Wilson observable" carries no dynamical content as implemented. The horizontal-block metric is gauge-noninvariant (finding 9), self-acknowledged but not finite without a regulator. Dimensional constants $\hbar, c, G$ are honestly flagged as undefined / unmeasured / dimensional-gap (finding 8), which is correct but undercuts every passage in the manuscript that uses physical units (most notably the Planck-scale claim in finding 5, which directly contradicts the dimensional caveat).

The framework's metaphysical move (information is primal; physical units are cognitive labels) is internally coherent but is acknowledged by the manuscript itself to risk unfalsifiability (line 397 of `sec_pullback.txt`, line 236 of `sec_grav.txt`). I concur. As a *physics paper*, this is a serious limitation; the framework supplies analogies and a coherent interpretive stance, but does not predict any physical observable, derive any physical constant, or recover any quantitative result of GR or QM. As a *philosophy of physics / information geometry* contribution, the construction has more standing — it offers a precise mathematical realisation of certain Wheeler / Kant / QBist intuitions, even if the bridge to operational physics is not built.

### Verdict

**MAJOR REVISIONS REQUIRED.** The manuscript contains substantial honest self-criticism that should be propagated to the section headers, abstract, and high-visibility prose. Specifically:

1. The action-principle table (finding 1) should be removed or radically restated; it currently misrepresents the relationship between $\mathcal{F}$ and $S_{\rm GR}, S_{\rm YM}$.
2. The Wilson-observable / gauge-curvature-conjecture material (finding 2) should be marked as Regime II conditional in every appearance, including the discussion sections.
3. The Lorentzian-signature section (finding 3) is correctly self-described; the headline statements elsewhere (abstract, introduction, summary boxes) should be brought into line.
4. The "(3+1)" subsection title (finding 4) should be renamed to reflect that no derivation is offered.
5. The dimensional inconsistency between line 310 of `sec_pullback.txt` and line 64 of `sec_grav.txt` (finding 5) must be resolved: $\epsilon$ cannot be both dimensionless and $\sim \ell_P^2$.
6. The "gravitational effects" and "quantum systems" sections (findings 6, 7) should be reframed as future-work directions, not as candidate resolutions of physics problems.
7. The dimensional-constants table (finding 8) is correct; ensure no passage in the manuscript invokes Planck units, time dilation, or wavefunction collapse without re-citing the gap.

After these revisions, the paper would stand as a careful, mathematically rigorous information-geometric construction with disciplined philosophical interpretation — but not as a physics-derivation paper. That should be the explicit positioning.

I do not recommend rejection: the underlying mathematics in Sections `sec:framework`, `sec:mass`, `sec:pullback`, and `sec:discrete_regime_ii` is largely sound and the self-criticism is unusually thorough. The recommended revisions are about *positioning* and *consistency between disclosure and headline claim*, not about throwing out content.
